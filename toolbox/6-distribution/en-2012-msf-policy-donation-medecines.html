MSF POLICY ON DONATION OF MEDICINES

Approved by the MSF Medical Directors on May 2012

Donations of medicines may happen for a variety of reasons, e.g. to deal with overstocks or to
cover the needs in case of stock ruptures. This document aims to outline the main considerations
for good donation practices. Two cases will be addressed in this document:

a) MSF donates medicines to a third party
b) MSF receives donations from a third party

MSF donates medicines to a third party

Donations need to be planned well in advance to be able to find a good solution with maximum
benefit for the recipient. Identifying potential recipients and agreeing on a donation requires time
and discussions. Therefore it is good to be aware of the need for donating stocks as early as
possible.

Who can we donate to?

-
Other MSF sections
-
Ministry of Health
-
Other organizations providing medical care
-
Specific health facilities (public, faith based, private not for profit)

The choice of the recipient will depend on what possibilities there are and should focus very
much on the individual needs of the recipient. In general, there should be trained personnel
available who are able to use the drugs in an appropriate and safe manner.

As the free provision of health care and medicines is a key concern for MSF, the donation is
supplied by MSF for use by the recipient in the facility accepting the donation, and on the
understanding that the patient will not be charged for this product (i.e. in accordance with free
patient healthcare policy of MSF). Therefore the recipient should agree to provide any medicines
donated by MSF for free to the patients.

In general, donations should be done within the country. Any donations to another country
requires to adhere to the specific legislation of the donating and the receiving country, i.e.
application for an export license and at the other end to apply for an import license, and
therefore should not happen to the extent possible.

What kind of drugs to donate?
Experience shows that donors do not always take sufficiently into account which medicines
should be given to a certain recipient, with the result of unused medicines or in the worst case
putting patients at risk of adverse events etc.

The general rule is to donate only medicines that are relevant and applicable for the recipient.
Therefore medicines should be part of the essential drugs list and/or part of a protocol in use by
the recipient. The medicine should also be in a formulation and strength that is known by the
users. These points need to be kept in mind by MSF as it is possible that MSF protocols and
medicines are different from what is commonly used and known by the health professionals in a
country.

Other important aspects to ensure proper use and patient’s safety concern the packaging and
labeling of the medicines. All packaging and labeling must be in a lingua franca of the country.
Both outer and inner packaging should mention clearly at least the generic name, the strength,
dosage form and expiry date.

Quality aspects
A key principle for good donations is to donate medicines only that comply with accepted quality
standards. As MSF applies for its international procurement a stringent quality assurance policy
and a careful source selection, the quality of medicines imported from the MSF procurement
centers is fulfilling these quality standards

When drugs are purchased locally in a country following the recommendations of the pharmacist
network, they comply with local regulations and therefore are considered as acceptable.

Therefore, both internationally and locally procured drugs are apt for donation.

However, the quality of medicines can be affected also by the conditions during transport and
storage. Do not offer any products for donations if there are any doubts on storage conditions,
i.e. storage within the required temperature range, protection from humidity and light (where
applicable), cold chain maintained properly throughout transport and storage.

Medicines already provided to patients and then returned back to MSF should not be donated
even if they are unused.

Shelf life:
The WHO guidelines recommend at least 1-year of remaining shelf life, to ensure that the
products can be used within the remaining shelf life period. However, exceptions can be made
for donations directly to health facilities where the responsible professional at the receiving end
acknowledges that (s)he is aware of the shelf-life; and that the quantity and remaining shelf-life
allow for proper administration prior to expiration. Another exception is allowed if total shelf-life is
less than 2 years, in which case at least one third of the shelf-life should remain, with a minimum
of six months.

The recommendation for MSF in general is:
-
To donate medicines only with a remaining shelf life of at least 6 months. Donations with a
shorter shelf life may be considered in exceptional cases if it is assured that the product is
used before expiry. Therefore it is important to get information from the recipient on their
estimated monthly consumption of each item. Recipients often feel that they have to accept
a donation or in situations of a general scarcity of medicines estimates may be too high.
Discuss with the recipient and remember that asking for some evidence of the use is
responsible behavior as a donor.
-
Under no circumstances should drugs be donated that are already expired or are likely to
expire before the recipient can use it!


Donation agreements
For each donation, it is advised to sign an agreement with the recipient, which includes at least the
following information:
-
Name of recipient including person responsible
-
Generic name, strength, dosage form, batch no, expiry date and amounts of the
medicines donated

There should be a disclaimer in the agreement saying that after the donation MSF is no longer
liable for the quality and the appropriate use of the medicines.

MSF responsibility once donation is done:
Even if MSF does not remain responsible for the drugs donated, MSF must ensure the traceability of
the product throughout the donation, in order to be able to contact the recipient in case of any
quality alerts or batch recall.

Improving stock management
In order to reduce the amount and value of drugs donated (and destroyed) and avoid
unnecessary donation, regular inventory and analysis of monthly consumption should be done in
MSF.

MSF receives donations from a third party

MSF drug procurement policy gives priority to importation through MSF European Procurement
Centers. Exceptions in favor of local purchase must be evaluated on a country-by-country basis
and on a case-by-case basis; local purchase, when approved at Operations level, should always
be carried out following a pharmacist’s assessment, in accordance with pharmacist’s
recommendation, and by checking each order on a product-by-product basis.

Donations of drugs, condoms and sterile medical materials may be proposed directly to MSF
teams at country level, by different entities and organizations, including other NGOs, United
Nations organizations, Ministries of Health, etc. According to MSF experience, locally donated
drugs, condoms and sterile medical materials may present a range of problems that may threaten
patients health, even when they come from reputable Donors:
-
They may come from manufacturers that do not ensure appropriate quality
-
They may come from intermediate distributors that do ensure transparency on the origin
of the products
-
They may have been transported and/or stored improperly, so losing their original
efficacy and safety.

Thus, any “local donations” should be handled, from a pharmaceutical point of view,
exactly as local purchases: before accepting a donation, a list detailing each item and its
origin (manufacturer, intermediate distributor if any, donor), should be sent to the
headquarter pharmacist for assessment and approval.

Donated drugs must fulfill the MSF quality criteria like other medicines provided in our projects.
Therefore donations have to be understood and evaluated like a local purchase. Considerations for
different cases are outlined below.

-
From other MSF sections: in general acceptable if the drugs were supplied by one of the
MSF procurement centers. In case the medicines were bought locally, it needs to be
crosschecked with the HQ pharmacist. In terms of storage conditions, it may be assumed
that another MSF section is conscious of the appropriate storage conditions and adhering
to it.
-
From other organizations or Ministry of Health: Both the quality of the manufacturer, which
should be crosschecked with the HQ pharmacist, and the storage conditions, which should
be assessed and considered adequate for the given product(s), should be checked.
