MSF GUIDANCE FOR MIXED SUPPLY OF MEDICAL PRODUCTS

TERMS AND DEFINITIONS

Medical Products include medicines, medical devices (including in-vitro diagnostics, laboratory reagents and
equipment, medical equipment, surgical instruments) and specialized food.

Mixed Supply is a shared and systematic supply between MSF and the Ministry of Health (MOH).

Supply and Procurement are considered equivalent and include purchase, donation and loan. This relates to
local, regional and international supply

Local supply refers to the purchase of medical products (permanently or occasionally) in a given country, for use in
the same country.

Regional supply refers to the purchase of medical products in a given country, for use in another country in a defined
region. Regional supply is a variation of international supply; therefore only medical products validated according to
the MSF International Qualification Scheme should be procured.

International supply refers to the procurement of medical products via the MSF European Procurement Centers:
MSF Logistique, MSF Supply and MSF- APU.

Supply Chain (or Supply System) designates the planning and management of all activities involved in sourcing,
procurement, and distribution. In turn, the Distribution System includes transport, distribution, storage and
dispensing to patients.


INTRODUCTION

Mixed supply happens when MSF is working in an MOH health structure, supporting MOH staff, or even in MSF
structures. It can take place in vertical HIV and TB projects or in primary health care and other settings projects
when the MOH, with its own funds or with the support of donors, procures some of the required medicines or
medical devices.

In 2013, OCG and OCB reported having mixed supply in 59% and 23% of their missions respectively. In Kenya
specifically, both OCB and OCP have mixed supply HIV / TB projects: most of the ARVs and about 70% of the
first line TB medicines are received from the MOH. As the projects are situated in Kenyan MOH facilities, other
medicines may also be systematically supplied by the MOH, such as infusions and antibiotics.

However, little detail is known of these mixed supply situations, because the MOH supply is not reflected in MSF’s
internal reporting on medicines and medical devices' expenditures, and operational decisions on mixed supply
may be taken without the knowledge or advice of a technical referent.

Mixed supply creates two supply systems with two potentially different levels of quality in the same structure.
However, the quality of medicines, medical devices and specialized food used by MSF matters, regardless of the
procurement mechanism used (local, international or mixed). This paper aims to analyze the potential risks in
these situations and to provide guidance on mixed supply situations in MSF-MOH integrated projects, in order to
support the operations and to help ensure patients have access to quality assured medical products.

Related MSF policies

The “MSF Policy for the Procurement of Medicines” approved by the MSF Medical Directors on 24 April 2008
(hereafter referred to as the Procurement Policy) defines the international and local supply.

Procurement Policies for medicines and Medical devices state that international procurement is always preferred
over local purchase, except when the international procurement is impossible or when valid (exceptional)
operational reasons exist. However the policy doesn’t clarify what constitutes a “valid (exceptional) operational
reason”, which leaves the responsibility to decide to the medical directors with the technical advice of the section
pharmacists (SP) on a case-by-case basis.
Local purchase is the only aspect of supply that is considered and defined in the Procurement Policy; one-off
donation, loan, and mixed supply are not currently addressed in this definition.

The “MSF Policy on Donation of Medicines May 2012” (herein referred to as the Donation Policy) guides on how
to manage one-off donations and loans from a third party via a validation process for sources and distribution
chains.

The procedure for supply of specialized food (NFOS) validated by the Medical Director on 20 December 2013,
states that specialized food (NFOS) is the only medical product for which local purchase to validated suppliers
equals to international purchase in term of quality. As such, local purchase from validated manufacturers is
encouraged.

Challenges of mixed supply

In mixed supply, MSF receives systematic supply from MOH (thus relating to operational strategy) and this
dynamic poses potential challenges (beyond the necessary validation procedures for sources and distribution
chains):
Immediate challenges:


Creation of a double standard for MSF patients and MOH patients.

Managing / forecasting MSF buffer stocks to prevent shortage (including for MoH supply) (e.g. which may
/ may not be validated by MSF, thus leading to uncertain buffer targets).

Longer-term challenge:


Weakening or jeopardizing the MOH supply system when it is completely substituted with the MSF supply
chain in MOH projects where MSF operates.


PLANNING A MIXED SUPPLY PROJECT

A country risk assessment paper should be prepared and used to justify the position of MSF regarding the
MOH supply. The paper should be drafted by the field operations, reviewed by SP from technical and quality
standpoint, and validated by the Medical Directors who make the final decision and take the overall responsibility
regarding the mixed supply strategy.

Additionally, the paper can be used for advocacy in order to create awareness inside MOH / National Programme
/ Donor / External Actor level regarding the quality of MOH procurement and impact regarding patient safety, and
other public health concerns like treatment outcomes or drug resistance.

A Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) with MOH is needed to guide and regulate the mixed supply, including
defining the quality of the medical supply that MSF can accept (if any) as supply from MOH. Involving the SP and
intersectional / MSF country pharmacists (where they exist) in drafting the MoU will help ensure compliance with
the recommendations in the MSF Procurement policies and this mixed supply guidance. A suggestion of
paragraph to be included in the MoU is annexed, and could be used by project explos team when discussing
MOH project agreements.

The below points should be taken into consideration during the drafting of the country risk assessment paper and
the MoU:

1. Quality requirements for Medical Products

MSF can endorse the responsibility of the distribution and use of medical products supplied by MOH only if in
accordance with MSF Procurement policies or, after Medical Directors exceptional approval.

products that MSF will accept from MOH. The list of products with the information of the sources
(manufacturer/country/manufacturing site) must be submitted each time to the SPs/Medical Device referent/Food
referent, prior to acceptance of the supply. This technical feedback should guide the negotiation with MOH
regarding the mixed supply, trying to limit the MOH procurement to the least risky products.

2. Supply chain

In mixed supply, MOH is considered as the supplier even when the products come from MOH’s donors (PEPFAR,
CHAI, UNICEF or other UN agencies such as WHO, etc.), unless an agreement exists with the donors that
surpasses the MOH supply chain.

Accountability along the supply chain should be clearly defined.

When MSF accepts supply from MOH, the sourcing, procurement, reception and initial storage is done by MOH.
After the control, validation and acceptance of the sources, these products and the responsibility for the good
storage and distribution practices fall under the responsibility of MSF.

Page 4 of 7

Transport and distribution can also be done by MOH if the supply is accepted at peripheral level instead of at
central level. In any case it is recommended to have clear visibility on each step of the supply chain and define
the level at which MSF accepts responsibility for the products.
A systematic control at reception should be performed to ensure that the products received are compliant with
what has been agreed upon in the MoU.

Good Distribution Practices (GDP) and Good Storage Practices (GSP) should also be checked at MOH level as
they impact the quality of the product reaching the patient, particularly for products with critical storage conditions
such as cold chain.

As a minimum, the national medical stores of the government procurement agency/ national programme must be
assessed and considered acceptable, through a technical visit by a section pharmacist. However in the case that
the mixed supply is proposed at district level, the supply chain and product quality should be assessed up to the
point of reception.

Separate supply chain procedures for MSF and MOH products are recommended until the dispensing to the end
user if possible. The separation offers some advantages that compensate the constraint of having an MSF system
running in parallel to the MOH:


to define along each stage of the supply chain who is the owner of (and thus responsible for) the medical
product

to facilitate the handover to MOH when MSF withdraws, since the MSF supply system can be dismantled
without affecting the MOH system

to avoid non-validated products (with poor or unknown quality) entering the MSF supply chain.

3. Traceability in case of quality problem

MSF and MOH must ensure the traceability throughout the supply chain of the products for which they are
responsible. The responsibility to contact the end user as well as the supplier and/or the National Regulatory
Authority in case of any quality problem, alerts or batch recall must be set out. This is possible only when
responsibilities throughout the whole distribution system are clearly defined. In the case that MSF supplies
products to MOH, it is MSF who is responsible to ensure notification/ traceability in case of any quality problems.


CRITERIA AND STRATEGY FOR ACCEPTANCE OF MOH SUPPLY

The evaluation of the risk/benefit of the MOH supply allows advice to be given regarding (a) the strategy to follow
regarding the supply as well as (b) conditioning the definition of “valid operational reason.”

A risk analysis of each family of medical products should be taken into consideration during the discussion with
the MOH for the mixed supply in an integrated project. This will allow evaluation of all the procurement
mechanisms involved (MOH funds, Global Funds, GDF, etc.)

Programme products
Programme products cover the medical products distributed by the specific national programme (ARV, anti-TB
and anti-malarial medicines, medicines for neglected tropical diseases (NTD), medicines for the reproductive
health, vaccines, IVDs (reagents for machines or RDTs), condoms, etc... and may be procured with support of
institutional donors. The sources of the programme products are usually MSF-validated, because they have been
procured through GFATM / PEPFAR / UNICEF / WFP mechanisms according to their own procurement policy,
however this is not the always the case. For example, with procurement via World Bank or MOH own funds,
there may be local manufacturer preferences, or at least the absence of defined independent quality criteria. This
is why it is critical to check the procurement mechanisms applied to cover the total programme supply, as this
may also be mixed, especially in case of procurement gaps. MSF should be alert to the increased risk posed by
direct government funds procurement, due to the tendency to prioritize financial/domestic market considerations
ahead of quality.

Non-programme products include all the products (essential medicines, MD/IVD, medical equipment, laboratory
products and specialized food) not distributed by the national programmes and which are purchased primarily with
government funds. These are the most risky families of products to receive from MOH because, again, the

procurement route favors price and local market preference over quality. These sources are usually not MSF-
approved, either because they are unknown to MSF, or because MSF has evidence of non-compliance to
internationally recognized quality standards.

For both programme and non-programme products, a validation request should be sent to SP (cc the
intersectional pharmacist) for specific technical feedback, including reference to the procurement database if it
exists for that country, which eases control upon reception. If required, the validation request should be approved
by Medical Directors.

It is acknowledged that in some contexts, political reasons and/or a pragmatic approach (such as the proximity of
handover of the project to MOH) can favor the acceptance of the MOH supply for more risky classes of medical
products, and even from sources that have not been validated by MSF. Such exceptions and justifications must
be clearly defined in the country risk assessment paper.


SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS FOR MSF-MOH MIXED SUPPLY

1) A “country risk assessment paper” should be produced and:

a. outlines the overall context, contains an description of the MOH medical procurement and

supply chain, the list of medical products available from MOH supply with the sourcing, and
operational justifications for accepting (or not) the mixed supply.
b. should be drafted by field operations, reviewed by SP, supported by QA coordinators, and

validated by the Medical Directors taking the final decision based on the risk analysis and the
overall responsibility regarding the mixed supply strategy.
c. can be used for negotiations (including drafting of an MOU) with the MOH.

2) All MOH supply - regardless of families or sources - requires HQ approval via SP and Medical Directors if

required prior to acceptance. A thorough control at the reception should be performed with vigilance, with
reference to SP / IP in case any clarification/tech. support is required

3) It is recommended to operate with separate supply chain for MOH and MSF sourced products and to

designate supply as “only MOH” or “only MSF”

4.1 Selection of medical products for the mixed supply shall be based on the National Essential

4.2 International norms for the procurement of medical products shall be followed by MOH and

MSF, while respecting national medicines regulation.

Mixed supply shall be accepted for medical products procured by MOH (or for MOH e.g. via
donations or via third party procurement mechanisms) using the following criteria:

(a) WHO Prequalification List / selected other pre-qualified manufacturers with WHO

GMP certification (linked to specific manufacturing site and product specifications)
(b) USFDA approval
(c) Marketing authorization from a Stringent Regulatory Authority (SRA) with registration

in a Highly Regulated Country (HRC)

procurement centres by allowing medical imports while respecting national regulation and
rules, based on MSF’s recognized QA scheme.
