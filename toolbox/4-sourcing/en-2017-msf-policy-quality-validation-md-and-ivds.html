MSF intersectional policy for the quality validation of Medical Devices, including In Vitro Diagnostic Medical Devices

Background information

The MSF intersectional policy for the validation of Medical Devices (MDs), including In Vitro Diagnostic Medical Devices (IVDs), was first implemented in 2010. This paper incorporates the experience gained since then, mostly through the international validation of IVDs, and comes at a moment where even though the MSF movement has embraced the principle of quality assurance as a legal and moral obligation for medicines, it has not fully achieved it for MDs and IVDs at international and section level.

Introduction

As general principles:

  • As for the medicines, MDs/IVDs are imported into countries under the legal and moral responsibilities of MSF. It is therefore MSF general directors’ responsibility to ensure that these products fulfill equivalent standards in terms of safety and efficacy as products used in developed countries.

  • MDs/IVDs bought locally in countries where MSF operate must be of acceptable quality as well; even if the ultimate legal responsibility remains with the National Health Authorities, MSF still bears the moral responsibility to provide the best product quality possible to its operational teams in given contexts.

The definition of a consistent policy for the validation of MDs and IVDs sources of acceptable quality is challenging for the following reasons:

International regulatory framework for Medical Devices and In Vitro Diagnostics

The regulation of MDs/IVDs in Highly Regulated Countries (HRC) is not as harmonized, stringent and mature as it is for medicines.

The European system of CE marking relies on Third Parties (i.e. ‘Notified Bodies’) appointed by the manufacturers for the certification of their Quality Management System and the determination of the product conformity to CE marking requirements. This raises serious concerns of independence and transparency. It is also important to bear in mind that a CE mark is mandatory to market a MD/IVD within the EU but does not necessarily mean that the product is actually marketed and used in the EU. This weakness is counterbalanced in the EU by Post-Market Surveillance (PMS) measures supposed to detect major quality issues. Nevertheless CE marking is generally perceived as an unquestionable proof of quality, whereas it is used by a large number of the MDs/IVDs export-only companies based in the EU for business purposes only, without a PMS system able to detect and manage quality issues in the countries where MSF operates. This statement will be reviewed after the new Medical Device and IVD regulations come into effect in 2019. The US FDA approach, widely based on the demonstration of product equivalence (510k clearance regulatory pathway) does not fully address MSF’s concerns either.

Specific risks due to the context of the MSF missions

The diversity of MDs (i.e. sterile, invasive, sophisticated, thermo sensitive) has led regulatory bodies to adopt a risk-based approach to determine the appropriate level of scrutiny for a given type of MD. Unfortunately, the risk classification defined in HRC seldom corresponds to MSF’s reality of use. For example, the malaria Rapid Diagnostic Test classified by HRCs in the least stringent / lowest risk category permits self-certification by manufacturers. In addition, some specific conditions of use in MSF missions may not have been considered in the product design validation resulting in a higher risk of loss of performance and/or misuse with potential serious consequences to the patients’ and health workers’ safety. These include, but not limited to:

  • extreme environmental conditions (e.g. temperature, humidity, dust),

  • level of training of the end-users (health professionals and non-health professionals) for whom the Instructions For Use (IFU) might not be adapted,

  • higher risk of use without respecting the intended use recommended in the IFU, and

  • MSF patient’s particular clinical profile in specific epidemiological context.

Importation restrictions

New importation requirements regularly emerge in countries where MSF operate. These types of administrative requests are not necessarily linked to the intrinsic quality of the product. They should, however, be considered during the selection and quality validation of the product, since the supply capacities might be impacted.

Key principles of MSF intersectional policy for the quality validation of Medical Devices

Considering the above, the key principles of MSF quality validation policy for Medical Devices are meant to minimize any preventable devices-related risks for the patient health and to ensure health workers safety, while considering manufacturer capacity of continuous supply of acceptable and agreed quality of product:

  1. The end-user need (the patient or the health worker) is always the entry point for the validation of a MD (new product or reassessment of an existing product). The clinical relevance, the performance and user-friendliness of the product are assessed by MSF international working groups or technical referents at operational centers level and translated into technical specifications in the MSF ITC catalogs. Supply considerations (e.g. mono-sourced products, persistent supply issues, costs) can also lead to product (re)assessment since the patient’s quality of care might be indirectly impacted.

  2. The quality assessment of MDs/IVDs aims to collect sufficient proof ensuring the safe use of products in MSF context with an acceptable risk. This is primarily based on whether the product is used or not in Highly Regulated Countries, the manufacturer’s QMS compliance to ISO 13485, and the documentation demonstrating the safety and performance of the product such as risks analysis or stability studies, assessed by the desk audit and during an on-site audit as appropriate. History of use can only be taken into consideration if a sustainable and reliable PMS system is in place to collect quality complaints.

  3. In any circumstances, the Medical Directors are accountable, by delegation from the MSF General Directors, for the quality of Medical Devices used in MSF projects. They approve the strategies put in place to ensure the selection of MDs/IVDs of acceptable quality, delegate the product quality assessment process as described below, but make the final decision on the validation of products based on a risk/benefit analysis considering existing alternatives.

The above principles are applicable for:

  1. International supply1

  • For the most critical MDs (class II and III) and all IVDs, standard or non-standard requested by more than one operational section, the international Quality Validation scheme (QA-MD-P02) issued by the international Medical Device coordination applies. For IVDs MSF recognizes the WHO prequalification and ERPD processes, considering that it satisfies MSF requirements in terms of risk classification, level of quality assessment and PMS system. For MD other than IVDs, the primary criterion is the actual use of the product in Highly Regulated Countries.

  • For the less critical standard MD (class I, including sterile items), as well as for non-standard MDs or IVDs requested by only one operational section, the European Supply Centers (ESC) are responsible for the implementation of internal SOPs for the qualification of new MDs and IVDs and progressive reassessment of the existing portfolio in order to comply with the principles expressed in this document, specifically:

    • Upstream: Considering the patient and health workers safety, as well as the quality of care as the starting point of its product quality (re)validation processes.

    • Downstream: Install an appropriate and efficient PMS system with the capacity to collect the quality complaints from the field and implement Corrective and Preventive Actions2.

  • The product quality evaluation process is delegated by the Medical Directors to the International Medical Devices Coordinator and to the QA referents at the procurement centers, under his/her supervision. The Medical Directors make the final decision on the validation when applicable (low rating products).

  1. Local supply3

  • Similar to other medical products, international supply is the preferred option. Local purchase may only be approved when international supply is not possible (e.g. restrictions of importation), or for a valid operational reason according to a careful case-by-case evaluation.

  • The product quality evaluation for local purchase cannot be compared to the quality validation scheme applied for international purchase, and particular attention should be paid on counterfeiting and storage conditions at suppliers. The Medical Directors delegate the assessment of the product quality and suppliers to the section pharmacist or Medical Devices referent, supported by the International Medical Devices Coordinator and the Laboratory Advisors as appropriate, but all local purchase requests must be validated by the concerned Medical Director(s).
