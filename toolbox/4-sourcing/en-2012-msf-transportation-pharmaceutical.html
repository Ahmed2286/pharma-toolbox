Glossary


Controlled room temperature (CRT) as per IATA: Products to be maintained between +15°C to
+25°C (with some allowances for brief exposures to +15°C to + 30°C).

MKT as per USP: Mean Kinetic Temperature (MKT) is defined as the single calculated
temperature at which the total amount of degradation over a particular period is equal to
the sum of the individual degradations that would occur at various temperatures. Thus, MKT
may be considered as an isothermal storage temperature that simulates the no isothermal
effects of storage temperature variation. It is not a simple arithmetic mean.

Shelf life (also referred to as expiration dating period) as per ICH:
The time period during which a drug/product is expected to remain within the approved
shelf life specification, provided that it is stored under the conditions defined on the
container labelling.

Stability study Zone II conditions:

A long-term stability study specifies 25 ± 2 at 60 ± 5% relative humidity. Accelerated studies

are specified at 40 ± 2 and at 75 ± 5% relative humidity.

Stability study Zone IV conditions:

Long-term stability study specifies 30 ± 2 at 65 ± 5% relative humidity. Accelerated studies

are specified at 40 ± 2 and at 75 ± 5% relative humidity.

Time- and temperature-sensitive pharmaceutical product (TTSPP) (as per WHO): Any
pharmaceutical good or product which, when not stored or transported within predefined
environmental conditions and/or within predefined time limits, is degraded to the extent
that it no longer performs as originally intended.

Executive Summary

In order to harmonise rules and practices of medical products transport inside MSF while ensuring
quality all along the flow, an in depth analysis of concerned norms, past studies and MSF current
practices has been carried out. This analysis allowed formulating a proposal for a global MSF policy
for transport of pharmaceutical products.

The main principle common to all pharmaceutical norms is that transport conditions for
pharmaceutical products have to be conformed to the packaging instructions, which are defined,
based on stability studies, by the manufacturers and approved by the authorities. Short excursions of
temperature during transport are allowed provided data from the accelerated or intermediate
storage conditions can be used to evaluate the effect of short-term excursions outside the label
storage conditions. There is one practical norm in terms of permitted temperature excursions

described in the USP: excursions permitted between 15 C and 30 C (between 59 F and 86 F), brief

exposure to temperatures up to 40 C (104 F) may be tolerated provided the mean kinetic

temperature does not exceed 25 C (77 F). That will be the norm used to define the acceptable
temperature limits for transport of pharmaceutical products for MSF. For products to be stored
below 30°C, the authorised MKT could be extended to 30°C but as most of the time containers are a
mix of products to be stored below 25°C and 30°C, the worst case scenario will be considered and the
MKT of reference will be 25°C.

From the analysed studies mainly carried out by WHO in the nineties, it is difficult to deduct clear
recommendations, as conditions are not standardised, methodologies were different and some
results are contradictory. Moreover the range of the tested drugs is not exhaustive and the impact of
the difference sources for a same product has never been taken into account (indeed according to
sources the quality of API, the formulation can change influencing the stability of the product).

Today, rules and practices inside MSF differ from one European Supply Centre (ESC) to another. MSF
Logistique banned dry container for all D drug + SLAS. Reefer and airfreight are recommended and
Kit-In-Dry (dry container equipped with layers of isolation) is tolerated when the risk for temperature
excursions has been demonstrated to be limited based on previous Log tag results. Since January
2012, MSF Supply has forbidden the use of dry containers for all D Drug except infusions (still shipped
by dry containers). As far as APU is concerned, no specific measures have been taken yet to improve
temperature conditions for sea freight although study of temperature monitoring and investigation
for variation of temperatures mitigation material are in process.

Based on the analysis, a policy defining required transport conditions of medical products for MSF
has been proposed.

The concerned families are all controlled room temperature medical products from D category
(DORA, DINJ, DINF, DEXT, DEXO, DDIS, DDGT) and SLAS. DVAC as well as DDGT requiring being
stored below 8°C are not concerned by this procedure.

Recommended temperature conditions for transport are the specifications defined for
controlled room temperature products by the manufacturers meaning <25°C or <30°C
depending on the conditions of the stability studies used by the manufacturer. Excursions of
temperature is tolerated for short period of time during transportation, under the following

conditions: excursions permitted between 15 C and 30 C, brief exposure to temperatures up to

40 C may be tolerated provided the mean kinetic temperature does not exceed 25 C (worst
case scenario) .

The authorised means of transport and procedure to follow to guarantee appropriate conditions
of transport for medical products are as follow :

•
When Reefer is possible, the ESC will ship automatically products by Reefer container. OC’s
may request airfreight in case of emergency and should then address specific request to the
ESC. ESC may also propose airfreight when limited volumes are to be sent.
•
In case the destination is not reachable by Reefer, airfreight will be used for
DORA/DINJ/DEXT/DEXO/DDGT/SLAS/DDIS. However, there is a possibility for the OC’s to ask
for Kit-In-Dry shipment for DINF in order to reduce cost of freight while taking measured risk.

It is important to notice that dry container is not recommended anymore; Kit-In Dry can only be
used for DINF products and only when shipping by Reefer is not possible.

Temperature has to be monitored all along the transport. Therefore, Log tags need to be
provided in each consignment: three log tags per container. Log tags should be spread over the
container (Front, middle and back position) and fixed outside the carton boxes.

As per decision-making is concerned, the ESC chooses the transport mean among the authorised
ones.

•
If temperature deviation outside of permitted temperature excursion occurs during the
transport of medical products by air or Reefer, investigation will be conducted by the ESC
pharmacist and dispute with the carrier will be managed by the ESC pharmacist. The section
pharmacist will be informed about the deviation and will decide if the transported drugs can
be used despite the temperature deviation. Any potential financial lost will be discussed
between the ESC and the carrier.
•
When the OCs request transport by Kit-In-Dry for DINF products category, in case of
temperature deviation outside the permitted excursion temperature, the investigation will
be conducted by the section pharmacist of the concerned OC with the help of the ESC. It will
be the role of the section pharmacist of the OC to advice the medical director about the use
or not of the exposed drugs. Any financial lost will be supported by the OC.

The policy could be reviewed at any time to include new types of container. Any new mode of
transport can be authorised in the future if the conditions of transport have been validated
according to a validation process agreed at the international level.

Some gaps have still to be filled to ensure global good management of the transport of heat sensitive
products for MSF. Humidity seems to have very little impact on D products while sterile medical
devices could be more affected. Limits for humidity during transport is considered as not critical for D
products but should be considered for transport of sterile medical devices. For the upstream flow
(transport from suppliers to ESCs) as well as for the downstream flow after the delivery from ESCs to
the OCs (from dispatch address to missions), conditions of transport are not under control.
Responsibilities, limits and temperature monitoring should be better defined. For medical supplies
(especially sterile) and food items, only opinion of specialist have been collected at this stage, in
depth investigation is necessary to extend the proposed policy for those items. A deep cost analysis
will be necessary to estimate the financial impact of the implementation of the proposed policy.

Lastly, questions were raised on ownership and pharmaceutical responsibility between suppliers,
ESCs and OCs. Those responsibilities should be clearly defined in order to better ensure global good
management of the supply chain from suppliers to MSF missions and guarantee quality of medical
products during transport.

1. Introduction

a) Purpose and methodology

The final objective of this consultancy was to provide guidance to MSF for transport of medical
products and propose a policy to be endorsed by medical directors.

The work consisted of evaluating existing international guidelines and data related to the transport
of pharmaceutical products, reviewing the practices and measures taken by the European Supply
centres (ESCs) in order to master transport conditions of medical products.

The consultancy1 has been organised as per the following steps:
-
literature review,
-
interview of the 3 ESCs (pharmacist and transport manager),
-
working group to propose and discuss the new transport policy for medical products.

b) Scope

The evaluation and following recommendations concern transportation of medical products
(medicines, diagnostic tests, medical devices) from Europe to capitals.
Cold chain products are excluded from this study as well as food items.


2. Norms and guidelines review

a) Pharmaceutical responsibility

EU GDP state that it is the wholesale distributor responsibility that, during the supply of medicinal
products, the transport conditions are such as to maintain the quality of the product, to protect
against breakage, adulteration and theft, and to ensure appropriate environmental conditions are
maintained during transport.

According to the French code of public health (article R5124-36 and R5124-48) the responsible
pharmacist should ensure that transport conditions guarantee a good conservation, integrity and
security of the health products.

IATA PCR CH 17 Revised 20092

The International Air Transport Association (IATA) publishes the Perishable Cargo Regulations (PCR)
manual that gives some guidelines in temperature control and cold chain management for goods
from the health care and food sectors, including pharmaceutical products and no-hazardous
biological materials. It mentions that it is a regulatory requirement that pharmaceuticals have to
bear labels defining acceptable exposure conditions and have to be distributed within defined
environmental conditions. Drug manufacturer is responsible to fix the acceptable temperature
range.

In case of temperature excursions, stability data for the same range and duration of the temperature
excursions should be provided to determine if the quality of the product has been affected and to
decide if the product is suitable for consumption.

IATA PCR CH 17 Revised 2009 extract :

Regulatory agencies require that all healthcare products are labelled to define the acceptable range of
temperature exposure, and any other environmental conditions, such as light and humidity sensitivity. It
is a regulatory expectation that the product must be distributed within these same limits in order to
maintain product quality.

It is the responsibility of the drug /product manufacturer to define the acceptable temperature range,
based on product (quality) stability test data that determines the effects of external temperatures on the
product. The drug manufacturer often is required to show documentation to regulatory authorities
confirming that the product was kept within the specified temperature range.

When, and if a specific temperature range or limit is shown on the outside of the package or container
(such as on a Time & Temperature Sensitive label), that temperature range or limit always reflects the
(external) handling temperature allowed during transportation and distribution and not the actual
product (internal) temperature.

c) Conclusion following pharmaceutical norms and guidelines review on
transport conditions of medical products

The main principle for transport conditions for pharmaceutical products is to follow the packaging
instructions, which are defined by the manufacturers and approved by the authorities.
Temperature should be monitored and recorded periodically during transport. Records of
temperature should be reviewed regularly.
Short excursions of temperature during transport are allowed provided data’s from the accelerated
storage conditions and, if appropriate, from the intermediate storage conditions can be used to
evaluate the effect of short-term excursions outside the label storage conditions.
The only precise norms in terms of permitted temperature excursions are the USP ones: excursions

permitted between 15 C and 30 C (between 59 F and 86 F), brief exposure to temperatures up to

40 C (104 F) may be tolerated provided the mean kinetic temperature does not exceed 25 C (77 F).
The MKT of the products for which stability studies have been performed under Zone IV conditions
allowing storage up to 30°C could be extrapolated to 30°C. However, MSF shipments are most of the
time a mix of products with specifications to store below 25°C as well as below 30°C. Therefore, the
global proposal for shipment will be based on the worst case (based on product to be stored below
25°C°).
It is clearly the responsibility of the ESC to ensure proper conditions of transport for medical products
aiming to maintain integrity and quality of the transported products to the delivery point. It is a
pharmaceutical responsibility to guarantee good conditions of transport for medical products all
along the supply chain.

Conclusion on publications

Most of the studies are dating of the nineties and were mainly carried out by WHO. It is difficult to
deduct clear recommendations from these studies, as conditions are not standardised and
methodologies were different between studies. Another problem is that the range of the drugs to be
tested is not exhaustive. The widest study is the WHO Accelerated stability studies of widely used
pharmaceutical where 296 drugs were tested among which 110 were considered as degradable.
Moreover, these tests should be specific to each couple product/manufacturer as stability can
change from one source to another depending on the quality of the API and formulation of the FPP.
That makes more difficult the possibility to cover all the situations.

Except the consensus on instability of oxytocics and ergometrin, results are contradictory for the
other drugs. For instance, some studies demonstrate that acetylsalicylic acid tablets are no-
degradable when another one demonstrates that they are. The results depends on the conditions
employed but also on the method used to determinate the degradation.

Studies are limited as conclusions indicate if the product is deteriorated or not at time T, but do not
estimate the impact of temperature deviation on the stability of the product. In other word, it cannot
help to determine if the exposed product can still be used during the entire shelf life, if the shelf life
should be shorten or if the product should be destroyed.

b) Transport conditions - Temperature limits

As per pharmaceutical norms, pharmaceutical products must be stored and transported according to
manufacturer’s specifications coming from stability studies and described in the labelling. These
specifications for room temperature products are <25°C or <30°C according to the conditions of the
stability studies conducted by the manufacturer.

This being said excursions of temperature are tolerated for short period as it may occur during
shipping.

Because there is no study able to prove the robustness of all the couples product/source used by
MSF and because of logistic reasons (orders consolidation leading to a mix of products with various
levels of sensitivity to heat), a unique temperature limits has been defined which corresponds to the
worst case (products requiring storage bellow 25°C). These limits are based on the USP guidelines
that are the most precise ones among all international pharmaceutical norms for permitted
temperature excursions and is the one already used by MSF Logistique and MSF Supply.

Excursions permitted between 15 C and 30 C, brief exposure to temperatures up to 40 C may be

tolerated provided the mean kinetic temperature does not exceed 25 C.

7. Question raised

All along this exercise, two major questions were raised about the transport conditions management
all along the supply chain: ownership and pharmaceutical responsibility. Those questions are a bit off
topics of the defined terms of reference but need to be addressed in the shorter or longer terms.
Some recommendations have been formulated.


a) Ownership along the flow

Contrarily of what is commonly believed, the ownership is not fixed by Incoterms as Incoterms rules
are intended primarily to clearly communicate the tasks, costs and risks associated with the
transportation and delivery of goods.

For incoming, the ownership is depending on « Purchasing conditions » and is in principle at the time
and no later than the time of delivery.

For the downstream flow the transfer of owner ship between ESC and its “customers” (OC’s and third
parties) is in principle at the time and no later than the time of delivery unless otherwise agreed on
sales conditions. However, sales conditions of MSF Supply and MSF Logistique do not explained
precisely when the transfer of ownership is done. APU is not concerned, as OCA is the owner.

It is recommended to define Ownership transfer:
o For the Upstream, between Suppliers and MSF entities, the purchasing conditions has to be reviewed
MSF owns from the supplier depot / factory or the ownership transfer takes place at the delivery
point agreed with suppliers (ESC / regional depots / missions)
o For the downstream, between MSFL & MSFS (APU not concerned as OCA is the owner) and their
customers (internal and external), sales conditions has to be reviewed
ESCs own up to the dispatch address and OC afterwards.

b) Pharmaceutical responsibility

The legal responsibility is not obvious to determine as the MSF activities are incorporated in an
international framework and from one country to another, the regulations are different.
For instance, pharmaceutical responsibility is clearly defined in the French regulation: (code of public
health article R5124-36 and R5124-48) the responsible pharmacist should ensure that transport
conditions guarantee a good conservation, integrity and security of the health products. However, as
soon as goods are transferred in another country the regulation is not taking place anymore.
It is then not only the legal responsibility that is influencing the MSF policy aiming to guarantee
quality of medical products all along the supply chain but also the ethical responsibility of MSF
Pharmacists. It is important to ensure continuity of this responsibility all along the chain and define
clearly role, and scope of responsibility of ESC pharmacists or qualified person and section
pharmacists.

It is recommended to define pharmaceutical responsibility
o between Suppliers and MSF entities (ESC / regional depots / missions)
MSFL, MSFS responsible Pharmacists, APU qualified person (QP) endorse responsibility and should
ensure that transport conditions comply with MSF internal rule.
o between ESC and their customers (internal and external)
ESC Pharmacists (QP for APU) are responsible up to the dispatch address and OC (or customers)
Pharmacists (int & ext) endorse responsibility from dispatch address up to patient.

OF MEDICAL PRODUCTS FOR MSF

from European Supply Centres to capitals of the destination countries

Concerned family

All controlled room temperature medical products from D category (DORA, DINJ, DINF, DEXT,
DEXO, DDIS, DDGT) and SLAS

DVAC as well as DDGT requiring being stored below 8°C are not concerned by this procedure.

General principle

As per pharmaceutical norms, pharmaceutical products must be stored and transported according to
manufacturer’s specifications based on the stability studies performed by the manufacturer, and
described on the labelling of the product. These specifications for controlled room temperature
products are <25°C or <30°C depending on the conditions of the stability studies used by the
manufacturer.

Excursion of temperature is tolerated for short period of time during transportation, under the

following conditions: excursions permitted between 15 C and 30 C, brief exposure to temperatures

up to 40 C may be tolerated provided the mean kinetic temperature does not exceed 25 C.

Authorised means of transport and procedure

•
When Reefer is possible, the European Supply Centre (ESC) will ship automatically products by
Reefer container. Airfreight may be requested in case of emergency or when limited volumes
are to be sent.

DORA/DINJ/DEXT/DEXO/DDGT/SLAS/DDIS. However, there is a possibility for the OC’s to ask for
Kit-In-Dry shipment for DINF in order to reduce cost of freight while taking measured risk.

It is important to notice that dry container is not recommended anymore and that Kit-In Dry can only
be used for DINF products and only when shipping by Reefer is not possible.

The objective of this procedure is to provide guidance to MSF for transport of medical products

Scope

Those guidelines concern transportation of medical products (medicines, diagnostic tests, medical

Cold chain products are excluded from this study as well as food items.

Decision making

The ESC chooses the transport mean according to the decision tree fig. 1. OC’s can have specific
requests for airfreight and in this case ask ESC to send products by airfreight.

If temperature deviation outside of permitted temperature excursion occurs during the
transport of medical product by air or Reefer, investigation will be conducted by the ESC
pharmacist and dispute with the carrier will be managed by the ESC pharmacist. The
section pharmacist will be informed about the deviation and will decide if the
transported drugs can be used despite the temperature deviation. Any potential
financial lost will be discussed between the ESC and the carrier.

When the OCs request transport by Kit-In-Dry for DINF products category, in case of
temperature deviation outside the permitted excursion temperature, the investigation
will be conducted by the section pharmacist of the concerned OC with the help of the
ESC. It will be the role of the section pharmacist of the OC to advice the medical director
about the use or not of the exposed drugs. Any financial lost will be supported by the
OC.



Review of the decision tree

The decision tree could be reviewed at any time to include new type of container. Any new mode of
transport can be authorised in the future if the conditions of transport have been validated according
to a validation process agreed at the international level: 3 consecutive Log tag results should be
within defined permitted temperature excursion for three consecutive shipments for the concerned
routing. There should be a minimum of 3 Log tag. The worst case result will be taken into
consideration for the validation.
