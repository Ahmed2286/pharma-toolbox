Measurement of antibiotic use quantity

(‘How much are we prescribing?’):
Defined Daily Doses (DDD) Methodology

Version :

1.0

1.
What/Why

This SOP describes the process of measuring and expressing the quantity of antibiotic use through
use of a standardized measuring system: the Defined Daily Doses (DDD) Methodology.

The World Health Organization (WHO) has assigned DDDs to antibiotics. In simple terms, the DDD is
the amount of antibiotic that a typical adult patient will receive each day for treatment of an
infection. Antibiotic use expressed in DDDs is a commonly used standardized measure to track and
assess the quantity of antibiotic prescribing (‘how much are we prescribing?’).

DDDs are useful for monitoring trends of antibiotic use over time (e.g. ‘is use going up or down?’),
between locations and after improvement interventions, thereby identifying areas for further
investigation using audit and quality improvement methods.
DDDs are NOT useful for informing the clinical care of individual patients (‘how good are we
prescribing?’).

2.
When

Monthly DDD measurements may be collected and analyzed once or twice a year; or pre-and post-
implementation of an antibiotic use improvement intervention (not immediately after
implementation but 3 months after).

3.
Where

DDD Tool in annex is to be used in Hospital (IPD) setting.

Note:
A different tool should be used for OPD and IPD setting, as the denominator used will be different (i.e. number
of consultations for OPD; number of patient bed-days for IPD)
For exclusively pediatric setting (such as pediatric/neonatal ward; pediatric hospital) it is preferable to use
another methodology (DOT methodology), as the drug daily posology is a function of patient’s weight.

4.
Who

Pharmacy managers (field level) and/or Pharmacy Mission and Pharmacy Coordinator (if these
profiles don’t exist in the mission, other medical profile as clinician or nurse could do it).

5.
How

Use the DDD tool for hospital settings, (including working instructions) to obtain data on how much
antibiotics are used (expressed as DDDs per 100 patient-bed-days). Analyze and interpret results for
a set of sentinel antibiotics; provide timely feedback of data to drive improvement

3

In summary: What does the DDD tool do?

The tool lists all antibiotics available in MSF and will convert automatically the number of units
(capsule, vial...) consumed, to grams used. The number of grams from different formulations (eg
amoxicillin syrup, tabs, caps...) and dosage strengths (eg amoxicillin 250 mg, 500 mg...) from a same
antibiotic name will also be totaled.

To calculate the total DDDs for a period, the tool will divide the total number of grams of each
antibiotic used in a ward (or whole hospital) during a defined period by the WHO assigned DDD value
for that antibiotic. The quantitative measurement of antibiotic use is then usually presented as a rate
with the number of DDDs as the numerator and the size of the population of interest (hospitalized
during the same period) over the same period of time as the denominator. This indicator enables
comparison of antibiotic consumption over time, between structures or after corrective measures’
implementation.

In practice:
1. Collect monthly antibiotic consumption data for the time period and hospital ward/unit or other

setting that you would like to determine DDDs for. This can be outgoing stock data or real
consumption, according to data availability (electronic records) and quality.

Note: Ideally use ‘real consumption’ data; if no electronic records available or poor quality
data, outgoing stock data would be next best option.
IMPORTANT: Always document which source of data was used for the surveillance
2. Collect info on bed utilization (number of patient-bed-days)

IMPORTANT: make sure this information is collected for the same inpatient department,
hospitalization unit, time period... as for antibiotic consumption data
3. Enter these data in the DDD tool. The tool will automatically express antibiotic use as DDDs and

present it as a rate using every 100 patient-bed-days as denominator.
4. Define list of antibiotics to be included in the analysis (‘sentinel antibiotics’). E.g. focus on those

with concern for misuse, watch antibiotics, reserve (last resort) antibiotics, highly toxic ones,
highly expensive, high resistance potential, ...
5. Data visualization. Visualize the data to get an indication of whether antibiotic use is changing

over time. In simple terms plot the dots and see what is happening over time: is use increasing or
decreasing?
6. Data interpretation. Please see some guidance below for data interpretation
7. Timely feedback of data, to drive improvement. Share your data collection and analysis with

front-line clinicians to enable them to reflect on their practice and change their prescribing
behavior to improve patient outcomes and minimize resistance and other harm. It is important to
share data in as near real time as possible.

Note: Definition of a patient bed day.
The definition of a bed day may differ between hospitals or countries. A common definition is: A bed day is a day during
which a person is confined to a bed and in which the patient stays overnight in a hospital. Day cases (patients admitted for a
medical procedure or surgery in the morning and released before the evening) are sometimes included as one bed day and
sometimes excluded. The same definition of bed days should always be chosen when performing comparative studies.

WHICH ANTIBIOTICS TO BE INCLUDED IN THE ANALYSIS (‘SENTINEL ANTIBIOTICS’)?
Focus on those antibiotics with concern for misuse, watch antibiotics, reserve (last resort) antibiotics,
highly toxic ones, highly expensive, high resistance potential...

ACCESS, WATCH and RESERVE antibiotics as per WHO Essential Medicine List (2017) are listed below.
https://www.who.int/medicines/publications/essentialmedicines/EML_2017_ExecutiveSummary.pdf
Antibiotics belonging to the WATCH and RESERVE group should be particularly targeted by Antibiotic
stewardship programs, at national and international levels.


ACCESS – first and second choice options for common infections.

WATCH – includes antibiotic classes that should be prescribed only for specific indications,
since they are at higher risk of bacterial resistance.

RESERVE – antibiotics to be used mainly as ‘last resort’ treatment options, such as colistin.
Their use should be tailored to highly specific patients and settings, when all alternatives
have failed (e.g., serious, life threatening infections due to multidrug resistant bacteria).

IMPORTANT: Bear in mind that- following an improvement intervention- prescribers may have switched
to other drugs that are equally undesirable. These potential alternative drugs should be included in the
analysis to assess the full impact of the intervention. e.g. if use of a second choice drug is going down as
intended, but prescribers instead shift to a third choice alternative, the improvement interventions did
not have the desired impact.

HOW TO ‘READ’ TRENDS?
It doesn’t take long consider the way antibiotics are used in the clinical area where you work. What
do you see when visualizing the antibiotic use data?

Assessing trends may generate hypotheses that form the basis for further investigation. For example,
an increase in antibiotic use for surgical prophylaxis might trigger refresher training to reinforce
proper duration of prophylaxis.

IMPORTANT: There might be different reasons for changing trends in antibiotic use over time. It is
important to identify and explain any additional factor that might be responsible for the
increasing/decreasing trend, before linking this to the effect of improvement interventions only, or
to potential rational use.

If trends go up or down:

 Was there a change in admission criteria?
 Change in MSF treatment protocols? Eg has there been a change in the indication for which

an antibiotic is being used?
 Are there changes in the doses being used or in the duration of treatment?
 Is there an epidemic going on?
 Is there a change in patient population? Eg higher rate of patients unable to tolerate oral

treatment?
 Is there increased awareness of bacterial infections by prescribers resulting in a higher

proportion of patients with a bacterial infection being treated?
 If so, is this the result of training, or better diagnostics, or better adherence to treatment

protocols?
 Are there any Supply issues? (Stock outs, importation issues...)
 Switch to second choice/third choice dictated by expiry dates or other reasons
 Introduction of a new antibiotic in the Medical Standard List?
 If outgoing stock data were used for the analysis (instead of ‘real’ consumption data): were

donations etc... Excluded from the data?

In simple terms: what is going on i.e. is antibiotic use INCREASING or DECREASING?

6

 Is it an early signal of suboptimal use (this can be both under use and over use)? E.g. if the

observed patterns of antibiotic use is compared with the current treatment guidelines, can
you formulate a hypothesis whether discrepancies might represent less than optimal
practice, whether training on protocol adherence for example is required or whether
treatment guidelines should be reviewed in the light of current practice? ( You can link these
with PPS methodology )
 Can the positive/negative trend of antibiotic use be linked to the effect of improvement

interventions? In other words, did an intervention intended to improve antibiotic use (eg a
refresher training on proper duration of surgical prophylaxis, update of Medical Standard
List, implementation of stewardship rounds,...) had the desired impact?

HOW TO ‘READ’ THE DDD VALUES?
https://www.who.int/medicines/regulation/medicines-safety/toolkit_indicators/en/

DDD per 100 bed days: The figure 25 DDDs per 100 bed days of an antibiotic provides an estimate of
the therapeutic intensity and estimates that 25% of the inpatients receive one DDD of an antibiotic
every day.

Potential questions to ask are

 What is the class of antibiotics with the highest consumption? E.g fluoroquinolones, 3rd

generation cephalosporin’s... which antibiotic is consumed most?
 Is that coherent with the infectious syndromes seen and their treatment protocols?
 What is the therapeutic intensity? (How much % of inpatients receive a (specific) antibiotic?
 Is the level of antibiotic consumption justifiable by the rates of bacterial infections?
 What is the ratio of broad and narrow spectrum antibiotics?
 What is the ratio of oral to IV DDDs? E.g. this might be useful to measure impact of IV to PO

conversion programs
 What is the proportion of fluoroquinolones use (or any other antibiotic class) compared to

the total consumption of antibiotics for systemic use?

GENERAL GUIDANCE FOR BENCHMARKING :

WITH YOUR OWN WARD OVER TIME
WITH OTHER WARDS, HEALTH STRUCTURES, REGIONS, COUNTRIES

Benchmarking is comparing drug use data from different locations, or from the same location over
time. By doing this, it is often possible to detect substantial differences that require further
evaluation, which may then lead to the identification and promotion of best practice.

When comparing total antibiotic use within and between hospitals:


Bear in mind that total antibiotic use will be influenced by specialty mix within and between
hospitals: hospitals with specialist units such as intensive care units, burn patients units.. may
have a higher use of antibiotics than a non-specialized hospital (as majority of these units’
patients will be under critical and immune depressed conditions; the high complexity of
therapeutic and diagnostic procedures developed over there etc..)

Definitions

Defined Daily Dose= the assumed average maintenance dose per day for a drug used for its main
indications in adults.
It is a commonly used standardized metric for the surveillance of antibiotic use.
