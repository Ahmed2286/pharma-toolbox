The goal of this procedure is:

 to explain the different channels of procurement of specialized food,

 to give tools to prevent inventory shortage without stocking excessive quantities by:

o Calculating the quantities in stock by products, by warehouse (Stock statement)
o Forecasting the consumption (calculation of the theoretical consumption)
o Determining the warning level by product
o Knowing the supply lead time and buffer stock (security stock) for each product
o Calculating the Average Monthly Consumption (AMC)

 to order right quantity of product at the right moment.

1. Monitoring consumption: theoretical consumption

The theoretical consumption is based on the protocols and rations. Orders are always made based on
theoretical consumptions.
Base Formula for 1 product for 1 use only for a given time:

Ration (amount per day) * number of beneficiaries * number of days

 Global procedure:
For all the products used in many programmes for a given period:
– List all the specialized food products used in the programme (to be done in collaboration with the
Nutrition referent)
– List all the final destinations
– Know all the protocols and rations applied in each of these final destinations (contact the nutrition
referent of your section)
– Know the estimated number of beneficiaries per final destination:

 by comparing with previous weeks, months and/or years according to season variations, hunger

gaps, farming, epidemic cycles, nomadism, etc.

 through the relationship between estimated admissions and envisaged discharges

 by comparing with other programmes: Following a survey or census, or through information

coming from the authorities or private bodies (NGO, international agencies, religious institutions,
etc.)
– Calculate the consumption per product in each final destination using the base formula. By adding these
consumptions the theoretical consumption of a product out of all the activities carried out for a given period
of time is obtained.

2. Avoid stockouts: the warning level

This warning level is calculated for each product and corresponds to the minimum quantity that should be in
stock. If the product quantity is less than the warning level, there is a risk of stockout, an emergency order
shall be placed if there is no order in process (backorders = pipeline = orders on going)

 Calculate the warning level of each product:

Warning level = consumption during LT (lead time) + (SS) security stock

– Lead time (LT)
Lead time is the time between the preparation of an order and the reception of the ordered products. It
includes order processing (do not forget the time needed before the order is passed at field level),
manufacturing or stock picking, shipping, and for international supply, the customs formalities delay. Supply
lead time depends on the type of supply (for local purchases, attention to the supplier’s capacity), transport,
distances, infrastructures, security constraints, etc.

– Security stock (SS) (Buffer stock)
The buffer stock is a minimum amount that needs to remain in stock to be used only for unexpected events:
late or lost delivery or a sudden increase in consumption. A buffer stock that has been used shall
immediately be restocked and shall be re-evaluated on a regular basis.
A buffer stock should be calculated based on consumption and supply constraints (purchase, transport,
security, etc.). Most of the time for standard programmes, half of the consumption per order is taken as a
reference: 15 days for monthly orders and 3 to 4 days for weekly orders.

Application : calculation a the warning level of a warehouse.
- Lead time for this particular warehouse = 2 months
- Security stock = 1 month
- RUTF AMC (Average monthly consumption) = 3 240 sachets/month
 Warning level = 3240 x (2 + 1) = 9 720 sachets

 Compare the available stock to the warning level for each product
The warehouse manager should compare the stock available for each product to its warning level:
– for each movement registered on the stock card,
– when filling the NFOS stock monthly statement

3. Ordering

3.1. Choice of procurement channel

3.1.1. International/regional and local purchase

Specialized food (NFOS) is the only medical product for which local purchase to validated suppliers equals to
international purchase in term of quality. As such, local purchase from validated manufacturers is
encouraged.
There is a very strict procedure for the validation of all products/manufacturers. Regardless the location, the
criteria used for the validation are exactly the same. The validation is done by the International Office (see
procedure ref QA_NFOS-SOP1).

Local purchase to validated manufacturers is encouraged for specialized food

The list of validated products/manufacturers is available on request to your section pharmacist or the
nutrition referent.

3.1.2. Donation

Donation can only be accepted if the Product/manufacturer is validated by MSF (International Office), and
after a proper quality control upon receipt.

Acceptation of donation possible
ONLY IF the Product/Manufacturer is validated by MSF
AND after a proper quality control (see IV: quality control).

In some case, a validation by the Medical director is possible.

3.2. Parameters for preparing an order

The needs are dictated by estimates of consumption. This method given here has benefits in specialized food
procurement by taking into account actual needs. The downside of this method is that it includes protocol
and problems derived from poor inventory management.
Therefore it is important to:
– ensure the coherence of the consumption data,
– take into account project activities and variability over time– know the constraints specific to storage and
procurement of specialised nutritious food: lead time, product shelf life, storage conditions.

● Consumption ≠ outgoing stocks:

Not all outgoing stocks can be considered as consumption. Donations outside the programme, transfers,
loans and withdrawals due to expiry should not be considered for monthly consumption calculations.

● Reference period to calculate the AMC: the AMC should be calculated for the last three months of
consumption if the project activities vary or over longer periods of time if project activities are stable. To
determine the effects of time impact on the AMC, the AMC can be calculated and compared for both 3
months and 12 (or more) months periods.

Prospective evaluation of the AMC:
● Take into account seasonal activity (decrease or expansion)
It is important to take into account the seasonal variations of the programmes in order to adapt the
quantities during the time covered by the order. The AMC for should be estimated during the “pic” (high
AMC) and outside of this period (low AMC).

● Take into account a cessation of project
The AMC should be re-calculated to subtract consumptions of a project that has closed, otherwise there
would be an overestimation of needs.

● Take into account the variation in the number of New Beneficiaries (NB)
Consumption and activity are linked. Attendance is measured by the number of new beneficiaries = NB. The
AMC should be re-calculated according to the average number of NB expected during that period. The AMC
and the average NB should be calculated for the same period of time:

3.2.2. Calculate order quantity

Order quantity should cover consumption of the stock between 2 orders taken into account the lead time.
Anticipate a SS to avoid any stock rupture.
Likewise, stock on hand and stock on order (in inventory) should be taken into account.

Quantity to order = AMC x (OP + LT + SS) – stock available – stock on order

– AMC of each product [expressed in standard unit of distribution units]
– OP = Order Periodicity [expressed in months]
– LT = Lead Time [expressed in months]
– SS = Security Stock for the product [expressed in months]
– Stock available/in inventory [expressed in standard unit of distribution]
– Stock on order (or backorder) which has not yet been delivered [expressed in standard unit of distribution]

Caution: the result of this calculation is based on past consumption and should be put into perspective with
the evolution of the project, as shown in the “Prospective evaluation of the AMC” part.
