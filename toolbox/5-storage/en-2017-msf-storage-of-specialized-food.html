Scope and objectives
This document describes good storage practices and provides guidelines for stock management of
specialised food products (NFOS), in order to ensure optimal quality of product.

The stock management requires trained people, a rigorous organization with clear and accurate tasks
repartition, to ensure:
 Implementation of the warehouse: choice of the site, security and safety, layout
 Good storage conditions (temperature)
 Receipt of products and their control upon receipt
 Good stock management: organisation, systematic update of stock cards, follows up of
alert thresholds
 Regular quality controls
 Maintenance and cleaning

1. Storage conditions for specialised food products (NFOS)

1.1. Temperature

The shelf life and storage conditions of NFOS are established by the manufacturers based on stability
studies. Stability studies demonstrate that degradations of specialised food products start at temperatures
above 30°C. Specialised food shall thus be stored at temperature under 30°C.

30°C is the limit for an optimal preservation of NFOS

 Temperature >30°C
Storage at temperature higher to 30°C does not produce major deteriorations of the product, but can alter
the nutritional properties of the product as the vitamin content can start decreasing.

 Case of therapeutic products : F75, F100 and RUTF1
These products are used for the treatment of severe acute malnutrition. As the vitamins are an important
part of the treatment, their contents need to be conforming to the specifications.

The therapeutic products: F75, F100 and RUTF1 shall be stored at temperature < 30°C

 Case of supplementary products : (RUSF2, LNS2, super cereals)
These products are used as part of moderate acute malnutrition and for the prevention of severe acute
malnutrition. As their vitamins content is less critical, storage at temperature between 30° and 40°C is
tolerated.

Other NFOS products may exceptionally be stored at temperature between 30° and 40°C

 Temperature >40°C
At temperature above 40°C, the food safety of the product is still ensured (no microbiological degradation),
but its nutritional properties are not guaranteed anymore as the speed of degradation reactions increases
dramatically: products’ shelf live (best before date) is reduced.

Specialised food shall not be stored above 40°C.

 Temperature <0°C

1 RUTF: Ready to Use Therapeutic Food
2 RUSF = Ready to Use Supplementary = LNS: Lipid Based Nutrient Supplement

Temperatures below 0°C do NOT lead to any risk for the product. The only eventual consequence is the
change of the viscosity, which become “normal” again at positive temperature.

1.1.1. Temperature check and record

The warehouse temperature must be registered at least once a day, at the hottest hours of the day. It is
recommended to use a Log Tag (ITC code PCOLCONT5‐), which collects temperature and humidity, with a
possibility to download quickly the data into computer. If LogTag is not available, it is important to
implement a sheet for daily record of the temperature. The “Daily record of the temperature of the
warehouse” form can be used (ref. QA‐NFOS‐F2‐8).

Attention: It is not just a matter of recording temperatures, it is important to make sure that they are
conform to the required storage conditions and act if it is not the case.

1.1.2. What to do in case of storage at temperature >30°C?

In case of storage of specialised food above 30°C, an analyse in accredited laboratory may be necessary to
check the nutritional properties and establish the new BBD (Best Before Date). In case of continue storage
at those temperatures, analyses will need to be regularly done.
There is no rule that allows establishing the new BBD according to the storage temperature as the
degradations depend on duration of exposures and the intensity of peaks of temperature. The decision is
done on a case by case basis.

Decision tree to follow:

 How can the store temperature be reduced?
 Install air‐conditioning (remember to keep doors and windows closed and use indicative signs)
 Install ventilation system
 Insulate roof by adding thatch, a shade netting or a false ceiling.
 Insulate the walls.

1.2. Humidity

Sachets protect the product from humidity, but the cartons’ resistance characteristics might be decreased
by humidity. If cartons are damaged, their mechanical resistance is reduced and the products inside will be
under the strain of supporting the cartons above. This might, among other things, cause the sachets to
burst open.

Keep dry
Keep away from heat (solar radiation)

2. Laying out

2.1. Choice of the site

Specialised Food shall always be stored in sound structures.
o Tents should not be used for storage of NFOS.
o Containers should only be utilised if they have been adapted (e.g. direct sunlight protection and
after installation of air‐conditioning)
o Design with high roofs and ceilings improve ventilation.

 See paragraphs 5.1 (prevention for the pest control plans) and 5.3.1 (spraying)

 Security of the site:
The site shall be secure and access restricted. A guarding system should be put in place.

2.2. Dimensions and layout

2.2.1. Estimated surface area

The warehouse includes the space occupied by the food product themselves, the necessary traffic area
(aisles, space between the walls and pallets) and the work area.

Estimated storage surface area needed = estimated surface area of stock * 3

In doubt, it is advised to overestimate, in order to be able to cope with activity peaks periods. The estimated
volume of stock can be calculated with a table available by demand to your section pharmacist or nutrition
referent (ref. QA‐NFOS‐D2‐1).

2.2.2. Interior layout

Interior layout should be logical and correspond to handling activities: reception, storage and dispatch.

 Reception zone: for the received products before the control upon receipt
 Zone for the control upon receipt
 Storage zone: refer to the paragraph 3.1 for details on the organization of the stock
 Quarantine zone (indicated and closed or separated): non‐conforming products and products with passed
shelf life (see paragraph 8).
 Working zone: for the preparation of the orders, quality controls and eventual repacks
 Dispatch zone: for the orders prepared before the dispatching
Note: Reception and dispatch zones should be physically apart to avoid errors
 Office(s): for administration (sufficiently big and lit) where all the documents for the management shall
be kept and archived

3. Stock management

3.1. Organisation of the stock: on pallets

 Specialised food must always be stored on pallets (isolation from the floor & air circulation)
 The way pallets are placed shall make circulation easy for stock‐taking, inspections, cleaning, outgoing
and incoming stocks, inventories...
 Specialised food shall never be stored against the wall: a space of at least 30 cm should be left
 The way products are placed in the storage area shall depend on the item category, batch number, Best
Before Dates and outgoing frequencies. Reserve stock (e.g. prepositioned emergency rations) shall be
placed in the back of the warehouse.
 Opened cartons shall be correctly re‐closed and labeled « incomplete », so they are used first.

Each pallet shall bear only 1 product bearing the same batch number

Piling technique:

 Sweep the floor before placing the pallets.

 Check the pallets: solid, clean, no splinters or out coming ends.

 Place the items carefully, do not throw them.

 Be aware of the direction (items packed in cartons)

 Put the first row exactly at the edge of the pallet and regularly place the items in such a way that
the entire pallet area is covered.

 The other rows will be placed in the opposite direction, thus crossing the packing (self ‐stability).

 All the rows shall have the same number of items, except for the last row if the pile is not complete.

 Height of the piles: maximum 2 meters (for stability and to avoid crushing).

 Identify the pallets by means of a label showing the batch number and the expiry date (to be done
upon receipt of product)

 For sacks (super cereals): the first row shall not be closely ranged so as to ensure stability. Place
the openings of the sacks inside the pile. Pallets can be grouped in isles of 4 or 6 pallets (not advised
in humid climates), which is more stable for piling sacks (allowed only for sacks). For long storage:
re‐pile once a month, un‐piling and re‐piling aims at placing the sacks that are at the bottom, on
the top (to avoid crushing and also to run quality controls).

3.2. Stock card (ref. ITC ALSTSCAR4W)

Stock card aims to:
‐ Register and follow up all stock movements (incoming /outgoing), quality control, and inventories
‐ Know the situation of the stocks at any time.
It must be systematically updated after each stock movement, including quality controls (one line per stock
movement).

One stock card per product and per batch number

 Where does the stock card go?
There are two possible options:
 Each card is placed along with the product where it is stored (preferred)
 All stock cards can also be together inside a box using an alphabetic order system per family.
Note: The stock cards should be kept in the warehouse and not in the MedCo’s office!

 What to do when a stock card is full?
– Transfer on the new card the theoretical stock of the previous stock card
– Archive the previous one and keep only the latest stock card
– The stock cards have to be properly archived for 5 years

IMPORTANT NOTE:
Specialised food quantities shall always be recorded in standard unit of purchase on all the documents of
stock management.

1 stock card per batch number

3.4. Physical inventory (Stock‐taking)

Stock‐taking consists of physically counting all the items. The main objective is to know the exact situation
of the stocks and tally with the information in the stock cards. It also helps to analyze the quality of stock
management and identify eventual problems. Real consumption can be calculated and thus orders can be
adjusted accordingly. Physical inventories are also a time to verify the product arrangement according to
the BBD3. Inventories should be carried out regularly (at least every three months).

3.4.1. Prepare the physical inventory

• Choose the date: if possible a day without receiving or dispatching products.
• Check that stocks cards and Monthly statement of the NFOS stock are updated: all item movements
(in/out) should have been recorded.

3.4.2. During the physical inventory

• At least two persons should conduct the physical inventory. The exact number depends on the volume.

■ Counter(s): counting shall be done by product and batch number, blindly with only the list of
products (by ITC code) without indication of the number of products to be counted.

■ Controller(s): controller(s) must compare the figures with the stock cards
 in case of difference : make the stock recount by another person, search for reserve stocks in the
warehouse, verify the theoretical stock...

• The final figure shall be written in the stock card: date, “inventory” in the origin/destination column and
the quantity in the stock column.

• Products with passed BBD3 and damaged products shall be placed in the quarantine zone (do not forget
to fill in the stock card with the date and reasons, and the non‐conformity card, see paragraph 8).

3.4.3. After the physical inventory

1/ Compare theoretical and physical stocks for each card:
• When differences have been found, investigate the possible causes (recording mistake, robbery, etc) and
act accordingly.
 When there are many and/or important differences between the theoretical and physical stock,
inventories should be conducted more frequently and/or be more specific (focused on certain products
according to the problems encountered).
• Prepare a list for all products whith BBD3 less than 6 months
 Will these products be used before their BBD3?
Compare the quantities in stock with the Average Monthly Consumption (AMC).

3.5. Stock rotation: FEFO rule

Rapid rotation of the stock stored helps to limit the risks of food alteration: determined by best before
dates and storage conditions, storage time should be as short as possible.

Golden Rule: FEFO (First Expired First Out): food products with the closer best

before date and repacked products must be taken out of stock first.

Example: inventory therapeutic milk F100: stock = 700 sachets with BBD in 2 month time.
AMC = 240 sachets/month
From now till the end of shelf life, 2x240=480 sachets will be consumed.
Thus, 220 sachets will not be used (with same level of activity).
 Forecast a new order and consider making a donation of the products you will not use rather than letting them expire!

4. Quality control

4.1. Control upon receipt

Whatever the type of supply (local, regional, international and donation), a control needs to be done upon
receipt (4.4.1). In some cases, this control needs to be completed by a more detailed quality control, and
an analysis in a laboratory (4.4.2).

4.1.1. “Simple” control upon receipt: mandatory for each receipt of NFOS

 In every case, a control upon receipt must be done by filling in the check list for control upon receipt,
(ref. QA‐NFOS‐F2‐1 / ITC code: NSFSCRECOS+).
 You shall also create a new stock card and complete the amounts received, the batch number, the date
and quantity.

Control upon receipt whatever the type of supply: Use and fill
the Check list for control upon receipt (ref. QA‐NFOS‐F2‐1 / ITC code: NSFSCRECOS+)

In case of non‐compliance, you must inform the procurement centre. If the product has been purchased
directly to a local manufacturer you must inform the Coordinator for Food Quality Assurance.
The check list must be kept for the duration of the shelf life.

 Note on the BBD (Best Before Date):

• Pay attention to the different ways of expressing the best before dates in
different languages.
In French, MM/YY is normally used (e.g., 06/09 or June 2009) while in
American English, the YY/MM formulae is used (thus 09/06 for June 2009).
Since this can lead to confusion, it is advised to write years with four digits
YYYY. Ex.: 06/2009

• If the best before date is written in month/year (MM/YYYY) or in
year/month (YYYY/MM), it means that the product can be used till the last
day of the indicated month.
Ex: BBD = 06/2009 then the product can be used until the 30th June 2009.

4.1.2. Control at receipt of super cereal, or therapeutic food with transport/storage > 30°C

 In these cases, the control upon receipt MUST be completed by a more rigorous quality control, including
an organoleptic test, and an analysis in laboratory in case of storage condition > 30°C before the donation.

These analysis are coordinated by the Coordinator for Food Quality Assurance. The way to collect samples

section pharmacist and/or the nutrition referent and/or Medical Directors if necessary, makes the
appropriate decision.

5. Pest control

Pest includes rodents, birds, and insects ‐both flying and crawling. The presence of pests can present
infection hazards, contaminate and degrade food products: they may transmit micro‐organisms via their
excrement or saliva: Salmonella, Staphylococcus, Listeria... their control is capital for food safety. They can
also damages materials and structures or just be a nuisance.
Once established, pests can be difficult and costly to deal with. It is necessary to implement a pest control
plan and to maintain good hygiene conditions.

5.1. Prevention: protection of the access to the warehouse

Pest can enter in premises by very tiny holes (<10mm). It is important to look over all the access to isolate
the warehouse:
 Doors and windows must be hermetic (be careful with the bottom of the doors). It is advised to add strip
curtain at the entrance of the warehouse.

 Protect windows, air vents... with grilles and mesh.
 The warehouse shall be completely isolated: There shall not have any hole in the roof/false roof/wall
joints/floor/windows...

5.2. Subcontracting pest control

The pest control shall preferably be subcontracted to a specialised company (For example: Rentokil).

5.2.1. Inspections

The Contractor must carry out regular inspections. All insect monitor stations and all rodent bait stations
must be individually numbered, signalised by a sticker/poster and their location recorded on a map. On
each inspection visit, the contractor shall check all of them, together with a general inspection of high‐risk
areas, for signs of any pest activity.

5.2.2. Reporting

The Contractor must provide written report of every visit, detailing:

 the date and time of the visit
 the type of visit i.e. routine, emergency, follow‐up or job work
 the locations inspected and/or treated
 any evidence of infestation (if known, both the scientific name and general term for the pest shall be
included)
 the type of treatment carried out including the common name of the pesticide and the quantity used
 if possible: any recommendations to eliminate causes of infestation
 name and signature of technician

5.2.3. Review and signature of the report by MSF – continuous improvement

The reports must be reviewed and signed by the person responsible for the warehouse.
Key performance indicators shall be implemented to measure the efficacy of the pest control
procedure and improve it continuously.

5.3. Establishing a pest control plan when subcontract is not possible

5.3.1. Struggle against rodents

 Traps: Place the traps outside along the walls, next to the access of the warehouse and
inside along the walls and on the way of the rodents...

Baits: dry cereal grains crushed or not too finely ground, a little vegetable oil and sugar
can be added, making the bait more attractive even if it needs to be changed more often
(it can get rancid). Avoid touching them, rodent have a good nose

 Glue traps: it is a ready to use plate, to be positionned on the floor.
Note: a rodent caught on a glue trap dies of heart attack after few hours during them they are yelling. While
these cries are dissuasive for other rodents, they can also be annoying to the staff present in the
warehouse...

 Bait boxes: boxes made in carton, plastic or metal, in which the bait is placed. A system
of witness sometimes allows to check if rodent has passed. Some food grade quality
(approved for food industries) bait boxes are closed and avoid the rodent to go out once
entered in.

 Poisons: All types of poisons are strictly forbidden in the warehouses and the outside area (except in the
case of food grade bait closed boxes because the rodent can’t go out from the boxe) . A poisoned rodent
may contaminate the food.

 Cats: The use of cats is forbidden; They are relatively useless and pose hygiene problems.

5.3.2. Struggle against insects

 Preparing the warehouse: spraying with approved insecticide: ITC code: CWATIIRSSC‐/CWATIIRSWP‐:
 before putting for the first time products in stock
 when needed
It is important to insist on the cracks and holes (after having sealed them!)
Spraying can be performed by people observing minimum precautions: use gloves, a mask, avoid skin
contact, always read the instructions on the label. Wait for 2h before using the treated area.
The use of insecticide is regulated in many countries. Check legislation in place.

 Glue traps: Glue system is very simple to put in place to catch insects. They include a surface covered with
glue that permanently holds the insects trapped. They can be placed on the floor for crawling insects, or
hanged for flying insects. There are also more sophisticated devices with glue board killer.

 Electric insect killer: They are fitted with fluorescents (or UV) tubes, which
attracts the insects and an electrically charged grid to kill them (by electrocution).
They are very efficient. Most of them contain a tray to collect the dead insects,
allowing the count of trapped insects. It is advised to replace the UV tubes every 8
month (60% of UV emission after 8 months).

 Zones to protect
 Zone where there is daylight (competition between the device and the daylight)

3.3.3. Inspections and reporting

 Identify all the devices/traps (againt rodents and insects):
1/ Number all the devices/traps and fix a label in the proximity to point it out: you can use the document
ref QA‐NFOS‐P2‐1 as a template.
2/Indicate all the devices/traps on a detailed map of the warehouse.

 Inspections: All the devices (againt rodents and insects) must be checked at least once a week: fill the
Inspection form (ref. QA‐NFOS‐F2‐9), note your remark for each device/trap and note every time a
treatment is done.

 Continuous improvement: indicators shall be implemented to measure the performance of the pest
control plan.
Example of indicator: number of insects, rodents and birds detected durign a predefined period.
Note: you must have pest activity, at least at the exterior ! A pest control plan without pest activity detected
is not efficient...

5.4. In case of insects in Super Cereal (fortified blended flour)

Only secondary pest infests flours, and they cannot penetrate the plastic barrier. Fortified blended flour
shall be heat sealed, which prevents insects to enter into the bags. The two only reasons to have insects in
fortified blended flour is absence or presence of defective heat‐sealing and bags with hole due to bag
handling. Thus, the control upon receipt of super cereal is very important and contains a meticulous
inspection of the quality if the stitching and heat sealing (use the check list ref. QA‐NFOS‐F2‐4 / ITC:
NSFSQCCONFF+).

7. Handling non‐conforming products and destruction of NFOS

A non‐conforming product is a product that is out of specifications: nutritional properties, food safety
(bacteria, contaminants...), defective packaging, absence of marking of BBD3 and batch number... The non‐
conformity can be detected during the control upon receipt, a quality control, handling of product, or during
a tour of the warehouse... In every case, the following procedure applies:

1/ Identify the non‐conforming products:
 Isolate the non‐conforming products: place them in the quarantine area, or if the quantity is too big,
isolate them with remarkable ribbon.
 Stick a non‐conformity card on the pallet : see the template (ref. QA‐NFOS‐P2‐2)

2/ Report this action to the stock card, with the quantities. Check if the same products are also present in
other stocks (end‐user units, intermediary stocks, etc.). If this is the case, identify and isolate them.

3/ Follow the Intersection Harmonized SOP for medical products for quality complaint and batch recall (QA‐
MEDS‐P01), contact your nutrition referent and/or section pharmacist (or the Food Quality Assurance
Coordinator).

5/ Decision are done as described in QA‐MEDS‐P01, according to the degree of the non‐conformity.

 Destruction of NFOS:
‐.Do not let the non‐conforming products accumulate in the quarantine area. Proceed to destruction as
soon as possible once the decision is done.
‐ Destructions are under the responsibility of the coordination team, after endorsement from the head
quarter and according to the national legislation.

Specialised food is product with high value, the risk
of traffic (diversions, re‐sales...) is important.

 Products shall be burnt.
They can NOT be given even for animals (except exceptional authorisation).

 Guidance to ensure that NFOS is disposed according to laws and regulations and in a safe manner:
