Foreword

Hazardous waste regroups several kinds of potentially dangerous waste generated
within Health Structures and/or which are related to medical activities. It includes bio-
hazardous waste, pharmaceutical waste, laboratory waste, X-ray related waste,
expired or inappropriate insecticides / rodenticides and specific waste (e.g. radioactive
waste and heavy metals like mercury). This manual explains why these kinds of
wastes are potentially dangerous; what hazardous wastes are exactly; where, when
and by whom they should be dealt with and proposes how to manage them. The
protocols to eliminate hazardous waste can be found in the annexes.

1. Why is hazardous waste present in health structures?

Certain hazardous products used in health structures need to be renewed on a daily,
(two) weekly or even monthly basis, thus these products are considered as
recurrently generated hazardous waste. But most of the hazardous wastes are
batches of specific chemical products that are inappropriate for use or that have
expired at a certain moment. In the latter case, it is possible to distinguish three
scenarios:
•
A normal accumulation of inappropriate or expired products, just waiting for the
next hazardous waste elimination campaign to be organized.
•
An accumulation of expired products because the foreseen consumption period
was reduced by long custom procedures.
•
An abnormal accumulation due to bad management, being:
o an incorrect follow up of the warehouse or the pharmacy;
o bad estimations of the consumption rates;
o inaccurate forecast of the needs;
o (very) irregular hazardous waste elimination campaigns.

As accumulation of hazardous wastes only increases the difficulty of their storage and
even more so of their treatment and/or final disposal, they should be managed in an
appropriate manner from the start.

Some basic rules should be followed under all circumstances:

Expired products, certainly pharmaceuticals1, should NEVER be used.
Using pharmaceuticals beyond their expiring date would expose patients
to hazards going from "lower efficacy of the drug" to "toxic risks". For
vaccines and antibiotics, a "lower efficacy" is already a high risk as it can
give a false feeling of security and develop resistance to the drug.

Avoid incorrect storage, sorting and elimination of hazardous waste as it may
lead to even more serious environmental as well as public health risks.
Where most of the pharmaceutical waste doesn't represent a serious threat
for public health or the environment as such, it's its destruction or
improper mass disposal that generates indirect risks. Other hazardous
wastes might represent direct public health and environmental risks. Some
of these indirect and direct risks are gathered below:
• Contamination of potable and other water resources by toxic and
hazardous substances.
• Release of toxic pollutants (e.g. dioxins) in the air when certain
hazardous waste is combusted at low or medium temperatures; e.g. in
the open air, mono-chamber waste reducers and even batch (auto-
combustion) incinerators (e.g. De Montfort).
• Human scavenging on a dump, resulting in potential misuse of drugs or
diversion of drugs from an unguarded warehouse for resale to the
general public.
• Ecological perturbations like:
o killing of organisms needed for sewage treatment, or damaging
aquatic life in water resources.
o organisms developing resistance against certain molecules (e.g.
antibiotics,
non-biodegradable
insecticides,
antineoplastic
medicines, disinfectants).

1 Expired sterile medical materials shouldn’t be used either, but they fall under the general waste
categories of sharps and soft waste.

Therefore, the temporary storage, the treatment and/or the final disposal of
hazardous waste in low-income countries should be done with a minimum risk
for the environment and public health, and at the lowest price possible, so it can
be repeated even when the aid-agency has left. Let’s not forget that certain waste
products (e.g. expired drugs) that aren’t eliminated (properly) are always at the
potential risk of being sold (either re-labelled or not) on the black market.

2. What is considered as medical hazardous waste?

Medical hazardous waste includes certain potentially dangerous products which are
used within a health structure and/or which are directly or indirectly related to
medical activities, but don't find their place within the other Health Care Waste
categories as defined by Médecins Sans Frontières: excreta, wastewater, sharps, soft
and organic wastes.

Hazardous waste is often related to chemical products that are expired or
inappropriate, although that some biological waste is also included in this category.
Therefore, medical hazardous waste includes:
• bio-hazardous waste,
• pharmaceutical waste,
• laboratory waste,
• X-ray related waste,
• inappropriate or expired insecticides / rodenticides,
• specific hazardous waste (e.g. radioactive waste, medical equipment containing
hazardous products, batteries, engine oil of the ambulance).

On the other hand, old medical equipment that doesn’t contain dangerous products
isn’t hazardous waste. Most of the expired (non-used) medical material (e.g.
needles, syringes, bandages, empty blood bags) can be classified under sharps or
soft waste, and are thus not considered as part of the hazardous waste category.
However, the medical material used for preparing and administering antineoplastic
(cytotoxic) drugs becomes contaminated with dangerous products, and is thus
recurrently generated hazardous waste.

Where the other big waste categories (sharps, soft and organic waste) that are
generated within health structures of low-income countries are grouped in a simple
but pragmatic manner according their similar and standardized treatment and/or final
disposal methods, hazardous waste will have to be managed on a case-by-case basis.
Their treatment and/or final disposal method will depend on the actual substances of
the waste and the chemical or biological reactions that can be expected when
mismanaged. Their packaging can play a role in the choice of the elimination
procedure as well.

Bio-hazardous waste

Laboratories will have to dispose of urine, stools and/or other body fluid samples (e.g.
blood) coming from (potentially) infected persons. In some laboratories, cultures of
bacteria are grown (e.g. water quality analysis). Within health centres with
Tuberculosis wards, a high amount of sputum will be generated by the patients,
besides the small quantities needed for laboratory sampling. These potentially
contaminated samples, cultures and specific body fluids have to be considered as bio-
hazardous waste and thus treated with caution (see further).

Pharmaceutical waste

In the strict sense, pharmaceutical waste is composed of the drug itself and potentially
its primary packaging (e.g. the blister, but not the cardboard box around the blister, as
this secondary packaging is soft waste).

12

Following products are considered as pharmaceutical waste (according to WHO):

all expired or damaged pharmaceuticals (drugs);

all unsealed syrups or eye drops after the normal period of use*;

all Cold Chain damaged drugs*;

all bulk or loose tablets and capsules (often due to inappropriate drugs
donations)*;

all unsealed tubes of creams, ointments, etc. after the normal period of use*;

all disinfectants diluted since more than 1 week.
*: Expired or not

Besides the above-mentioned rules, pharmaceuticals are also considered as waste
when the drugs are refused because of:

coming from a supplier of unproven or unverified quality (risk of having a
substandard or counterfeit drug, potentially hazardous for the patients’ health);

belonging to a batch that has been recalled due to quality related problems;

their deterioration or alteration (e.g. opened vaccine vial with remaining doses
can't be used anymore after the normal period of use);

the non-respect of the donating directives.

Laboratory waste

Typical laboratory wastes are some of the chemicals and diagnostic test kits used for
analyses, and in some labs the generation of radioactive waste, although that the latter
should be a really big exception within MSF settings. Some diagnostic test kits are as
such not really hazardous, even so as some chemicals such as sugars, amino acids and
certain salts, certainly when they have to be disposed of in little quantities. Other
chemicals are considered hazardous if they have one or more of the following
properties:

Flammable

Corrosive

Toxic

Teratogens / foeto-toxic / genotoxic

Reactive: explosive, air and/or water reactive, shock sensitive.

Wastewater from laboratories will most probably contain some hazardous residues,
but their concentration in chemical substances or infectious organisms is low.
Nevertheless, attention will have to be given to its discharge in order to avoid the
contamination of the environment (e.g. groundwater).

Also other kinds of wastes are present within a laboratory, like sharps and soft waste.
But as they aren't part of the hazardous waste category, they can be managed
according the general recommendations for medical waste in low-income countries,
as described by MSF.

X-ray related waste

X-ray related activities don't generate that much (hazardous) waste. Most of the time,
it will be limited to:

developing liquid for the X-ray film (frequent replacement or when expired);

fixer liquid for the X-ray film (frequent replacement or when expired);

expired X-ray film;

old X-ray pictures (as they are part of the patient’s medical file, specific rules
have to be respected, which fall outside the scope of this manual).

13

Insecticides / rodenticides

Some control methods of vector related diseases in health structures require the use of
insecticides / rodenticides. Most insecticides have a rather long shelf life, thus an
unprepared (non-dissolved / non-diluted) product after a spraying session can often be
kept for a next time. It is also possible in specialized laboratories to test insecticide
samples for their concentration. This permits to verify if the stored / expired
insecticide is still valid for use and under which dilution. As these tests can be
expensive, it’s only worth to do them if the volume of remaining insecticides is high
enough. Contact your Head Quarter for more information. If the final decision is that
the expired insecticides are beyond use, specific management beyond simple burial or
batch incineration will be required.

However, leftovers of insecticides preparations (e.g. solutions) used during vector
control campaigns (e.g. Insecticide Residual Spraying) are always considered as
hazardous waste that needs to be managed correctly.

Inappropriate (e.g. locally bought) or expired insecticides / rodenticides are often
found in the warehouse of health structures in low-income countries. It's often not
known what chemicals the unrecognized insecticide / rodenticide brands are
composed of, what their toxicity is and thus their hazards are, and if resistance already
exists against some of the products. So in order to avoid having unwanted insecticides
/ rodenticides, it is strongly recommended not to buy from local suppliers, except if
they would sell genuine products of recognized manufacturers. Contact your Head
Quarter for more information.

Specific hazardous waste

Specific hazardous waste groups a lot of products that can be common as well as
rather particular for certain medical activities within a health structure. Some
examples of specific hazardous waste are:

all material that potentially has been in contact with antineoplastic drugs: e.g.
needles, syringes, drapes, infusion pouch, gauze;

radioactive waste: although the medical sector doesn't produce waste with a
high radioactivity, this type of refuse is dangerous and thus requires particular
disposal techniques. However, it will only be in very rare cases that MSF will
be confronted with real radioactive waste, and thus they fall out of the scope
of this manual;

(small) batteries of medical equipment;

old and broken equipment containing hazardous products: e.g. broken
appliances containing mercury such as thermometers and blood pressure
gauges;

UV-lamps and low energy light bulbs;

materials related to the ambulances: e.g. used engine oil, car battery.

3. Where, when and by whom?

Recurrently generated hazardous waste will mostly be found within the laboratory
and the X-ray room, but also in the consultation / treatment rooms or the wards (e.g.
all waste related to preparing and administering antineoplastic drugs, a broken
mercury thermometer, small batteries, insecticides left-over after spraying, opened
vaccine vials with doses remaining, all kinds of bio-hazardous waste).

The recurrently generated hazardous waste should be managed on a daily, sometimes
a (two) weekly or even a monthly basis. The management procedure might even
include pre-treatment (autoclaving) of some bio-hazardous waste within the
laboratory itself.

The segregation of the recurrently generated hazardous waste categories has to be
done by the (Para-) medical and laboratory staff, depending on the place where they
work. All lab technicians will also have to be trained in the correct discharge of small
amounts of certain chemicals and the potential pre-treatment of bio-hazardous waste.

The transport of recurrently generated hazardous waste in between the treatment
rooms / wards / laboratory and the waste zone should be done by the waste manager,
as well as its potential treatment and/or final disposal. The waste manager should
receive a specific training of how to deal with all kinds of hazardous waste.


The location of inappropriate or expired hazardous products depends on the
distribution system within the health structure. Where the products (mainly drugs) are
taken out on a needs basis, most of the expired waste will be found in the pharmacy or
warehouse. Where a weekly or monthly allowance is in place, it will be possible to
find expired products as well in the wards and consultation / treatment rooms
(including lab, maternity, operation theatre). They should be located and separated
from the rest during inventories.

As specialized elimination institutions with a high temperature incinerator or
recycling facilities are rather rare in low-income countries, it's often most apt to
manage inappropriate / expired hazardous products on a centralized place, which can
be onsite the health structure (e.g. waste zone), or offsite like for burial of
encapsulated waste in a controlled landfill. But it might sometimes be possible to find
good alternatives to specialized incinerators, which also can deal with hazardous
waste in a correct manner, such as cement factory kilns and/or blast furnaces for iron
and steel production.

Inappropriate and/or expired hazardous products have to be eliminated during special
campaigns, at least once every six months. Piling hazardous waste up for years can
lead to situations that are very difficult to manage.

As the treatment and/or final disposal of hazardous waste should normally be done
according to the national legislation (if existing), it is necessary to contact the
responsible authorities for more information and/or to ask for the necessary permits,
certainly if controlled substances need to be eliminated. Such authorities could be the:

Drug Regulatory Authority

Health Office

Authority of Regional or Local Health

Environmental Office

Hospital and/or Health Centre, etc.

16

The elimination of the inappropriate and expired hazardous waste should be managed
by a multi-disciplinary team:

The medical responsible / pharmacist / storekeeper of the project to separate
the hazardous waste from the pharmacy / warehouse and to prepare the list.

A logistician or “watsan” for the determination of a suitable elimination site.

A logistics team led by a logistician or a “watsan”, for the organisation of the
potential transport of the hazardous waste and for the actual elimination
activities. The elimination itself should only be done by male staff for safety
reasons. It is also strongly recommended that a medical person would be
present during the elimination procedures to give advice on pharmaceutical
and laboratory waste, and/or to intervene if an accident would occur.

The medical coordinator stays the final overall responsible for the whole
elimination process.
All people involved in hazardous waste elimination should be trained for their specific
job. Once trained, they should receive a certificate indicating that they are capable of
collaborating in correct hazardous waste elimination.

4. How should medical hazardous waste be managed?

Obviously, it is better to avoid as much as possible hazardous waste. This can partly
be achieved with better management of the warehouse / pharmacy by checking:
a. all the orders,
b. the quantities available in the warehouse / pharmacy,
c. the expiry dates of the products,
d. the consumption rates.

The refusal of all kinds of drug donations, except those authorised by the MSF
Medical Department, will also reduce the amount of hazardous waste. However, it
cannot be avoided that some hazardous waste will be generated such as products
expiring, even with the best possible management of the warehouse / pharmacy, and
not to forget the recurrently generated hazardous waste. In order to avoid
accumulation, it is important to eliminate the hazardous waste in a correct manner and
on a regular basis.


4.1. Recurrently generated hazardous waste

It is recommended that bio-hazardous waste would be autoclaved before it leaves the
laboratory, as it can be highly infectious. However, the autoclaving process is often
considered to be difficult to manage within the laboratory and is therefore not done.
This isn't a big problem when the waste is immediately collected, transported towards,
treated and/or disposed of in the waste zone on the premises itself. The treatment
however has to be done in an incinerator with a double combustion chamber, which
has a good combustion ongoing before the bio-hazardous waste is introduced. Bio-
hazardous waste like blood samples or sputum of Tuberculoses patients might require
additional combustibles to reduce the risk of temperature drop during the incineration
due to their high liquid content. Obviously, samples in glass containers should not be
incinerated as their recipient might explode, but they should be disposed of in a
completely sealed sharps pit. For some of the waste coming from the laboratory like
urine, the latrines / toilets can be used.

Although that the excreta, vomit and puncture liquids of patients treated with
antineoplastic drugs are contaminated for about 48 hours and thus be considered as
hazardous waste, they can be disposed of without splashing in latrines / toilets or a
sluice, which should be maintained regularly.


The few vaccine vials that have been opened during an EPI vaccination but still
contain some doses, or those that got cold chain damaged are part of the recurrently
generated pharmaceutical waste. These vials can be discarded in a completely sealed
sharps pit, without being crushed (as is normally recommended for empty vials and
ampoules). However, it is recommended to put the boxes with leftovers or complete
batches of cold chain damaged vials of mass vaccination in a separated and closed
stock in order to be eliminated during or shortly after the campaign. Their volumes
are most often too big to be disposed of in a normal sharps pit, and will therefore have
to be encapsulated.

Disinfectant solutions are considered to be “expired” after a consumption period of a
day (e.g. 0.05 % chlorine solution) or a week after their dilution. It is recommended to
check the consumption period for every disinfectant on a concentration-by-
concentration basis. These “expired” solutions can still be used for cleaning floors
within health structures, possibly after further dilution (e.g. for chlorine solutions of

18

1% and above). As it is always very difficult to know the solution's remaining
concentration, it is strongly recommended not to use it for critical surfaces that have
to be disinfected, such as operating theatre or delivery room floors.


The management of recurrently generated hazardous laboratory waste depends on its
substances. For liquids, the management is often limited by pouring them in the sink
after a good dilution with water (attention for some chemicals as incorrect dilution
procedures can cause serious accidents; the label of the product should always be
consulted). The amount of recurrently generated chemical waste that leaves the
laboratory via the sink is so low that it shouldn't cause environmental nor health
problems when a good evacuation system of the wastewater is foreseen. However, it
is prohibited to dispose of large quantities of laboratory chemicals (e.g. complete
bottle of 1 litre) immediately via the sink as this might cause groundwater pollution
with standard wastewater infiltration systems (soak away pit, infiltration trenches).
Large quantities should be eliminated during special campaigns.

Most solid hazardous laboratory waste (e.g. diagnostic test kits), as well as sharps and
soft waste recurrently generated within the laboratory can be managed according to
the general MSF recommendations for low-income countries:
• segregation,
• transport towards the waste zone with potential temporary storage,
• potential treatment (incineration) and/or
• final disposal (sharps, ash / refuse pit).


When the developing and fixer liquids for X-ray films have to be replaced, they can
be diluted with big quantities of water and disposed of in a completely closed sewer
system, or in a big and/or rapid flowing river (never in stagnant water like ponds or
lakes, nor into the ground(water) via soak away pits or infiltration trenches). Small
quantities of these liquids can also be evaporated.


Small quantities of leftover prepared insecticide after a spraying session within the
health structure can be used to spray the inner walls of the latrines. If this isn’t
possible, as a last resort it can be poured inside the pit latrines. The quantity and
concentration of the leftover insecticide are considered that low that they shouldn't
form a direct environmental, nor public health threat, as long as the standard rules for
latrines are respected (no water source closer than 30 m, the highest water table at
least 1.5 m under the bottom of the pit, all within a homogenous soil). The insecticide
residues could even bring a reduction of the fly population in and around the latrines.


Recurrently generated specific hazardous waste like (small) batteries of medical
equipment might be recycled if they can be brought to suitable facilities. Otherwise,
they will have to be disposed of in a completely sealed sharps pit or be encapsulated.
Similar elimination techniques can be used for small broken equipment containing a
hazardous product, like mercury thermometers. Radioactive waste, although rare in
MSF settings, requires particular disposal techniques. The Head Quarter will need to
be contacted for more information

All material that potentially has been in contact with antineoplastic drugs during their
preparation and administration should be discarded in a single use sharps container
(e.g. vials, syringes with needle) or a hazardous waste bin (e.g. infusion pouches
without the needle, drapes, gloves). The sharps container can be disposed of in a
completely sealed sharps pit, while the hazardous waste can be incinerated in a well
heated double combustion chamber (batch) incinerator.

4.2. Elimination campaigns of inappropriate / expired hazardous products

The elimination campaign of hazardous waste has to be planned rigorously, in order
to ease the implementation and to be safe, even for big quantities. This planning
consists of:
• Isolating the hazardous waste.
• Making the list of all the hazardous products that have to be eliminated.
• Choosing the most appropriate elimination procedure per product (it is
recommended to involve the Head Quarter in the choice).
• Preparing the elimination procedures.
• (Potentially) transporting the hazardous waste
• Implementing the elimination procedures.
• Keeping records of all relevant documents.

4.2.1. Isolating the hazardous waste

The elimination campaign should start by retrieving the hazardous waste from the
warehouse / pharmacy by its responsible and by putting it in a separate cool, dry,
well-ventilated and locked room, away from reactive, oxidizing and combustible
materials. This room should be in a well-identified location within the health
structure, preferably detached from the other buildings, but still easily accessible from
the warehouse / pharmacy and possibly also from the elimination area. For lab
chemicals, the isolating room should also be away from direct sunlight, sources of
heat or ignition, or any area where fire hazard may be acute, nor should it be too cold
inside and certainly not freezing. Obviously, the isolation room should be a non-
smoking area.

It is recommended to keep the same products together and to group them according
the above described hazardous waste categories. Similar products can be put in closed
recipients (e.g. solid cardboard boxes, clean plastic drums) which should be labelled
indicating clearly their content (generic name, quantities, form, dosage, expiry date)
and if it is fragile (e.g. glass bottles). However, incompatible laboratory reagents

All the hazardous waste that is retrieved from the warehouse / pharmacy needs to be
registered on the “stock cards”, mentioning their elimination and the reason why. In
order to recognize these products for correct elimination afterwards, it's important to
leave them in their original packaging.

In case that the hazardous waste packaging would be damaged, the staff performing
the work should wear protective equipment (gloves, boots, overall, dust or chemical
mask and possibly goggles). This job requires trained and supervised male staff.


All inappropriate / expired pharmaceuticals will have to be managed during
elimination campaigns. A first sorting can be made by putting the antineoplastic drugs
and the controlled substances (e.g. narcotics, psychotropic drugs) separately from the
rest of the pharmaceutical waste. All missions need to be up to date on the current
national legislation on controlled substances. The authorities most probably will have
to be informed about the elimination (reason, quantities, procedure), often already in
advance. This latter practice is advisable for all pharmaceutical waste. In case of
doubts, always check the country legislation, together with the section pharmacist if
needed.

If there is a quality alert or a batch recall on medicines you have in your mission,
please contact your section pharmacist. Approved pharmaceuticals that haven’t
expired, but are not complying with appropriate quality standards (e.g. deterioration
as physical changes, decreased efficacy) should be put apart. It’s possible that samples
will have to be sent in sufficient numbers to the section pharmacist for an appropriate
follow up. In case of accountability of the manufacturer, a decision will be taken with
them if the elimination of the pharmaceuticals will take place on the spot or if they
will be sent back to its origin on their expenses. Normally, the manufacturer should
replace these deteriorated drugs for free.


The majority of inappropriate or expired hazardous laboratory products will be
chemicals and diagnostic test kits.


The expired X-ray film containing silver, as well as its developing and fixer liquids
should be put aside, certainly if suitable recycling facilities would be available.


Inappropriate insecticides / rodenticides (e.g. bought locally) should be put
separately according to the different products for the inevitable future encapsulation
or high temperature incineration. Before thinking of eliminating big quantities of
expired insecticides, contact your “watsan” technical referent to verify if sending
samples to Europe for testing is worthwhile, in order to validate if they still can be
used.


Piled up specific hazardous waste needs to be managed on a case-by-case basis.
Before isolation, it should be verified if some products can be recycled, as this can be
an interesting elimination alternative in certain cases. For further information, seek
advice at your Head Quarter.

Remark: Most of the expired medical material can be considered as sharp and soft
waste, and is not part of the hazardous waste category. Therefore, all expired material
that can cause stick injuries or cuts, thus to be disposed of in a sharps pit or
encapsulated, should be separated from non-sharp waste that doesn't contain
hazardous substances, which in turn will have to be burnt or incinerated.

4.2.2. Making the list of all the hazardous products that have to be eliminated

For correct elimination of the inappropriate / expired hazardous products, a list should
be made by the person responsible for the warehouse / pharmacy in the following
manner:
1. Rank the items according to the MSF Medical, Surgical & Laboratory Order
Sheets2, or the MSF catalogues (e.g. oral drugs together, preferably in
alphabetical order).
2. Provide the International MSF code (if you have it). This allows the exact
identification of the product.
3. Give also the quantity per product (per unit; e.g. tablet, bottle, ampoule).
4. Include for the non-standard pharmaceuticals (no MSF-code):
•
Generic name (INN), even if it’s a brand named drug.
•
Administration route and formulation.
Potentially the reason for elimination and the value of the expired products can be
added, although this is more for your own information.

4.2.3. Choosing the most appropriate elimination procedure per product

If a national legislation on the disposal of hazardous waste exists, it must be followed.
However, it is recommended to verify the elimination procedures proposed in the
legislation. When they are substandard, the MSF alternatives can be proposed and/or
lobbying by WHO / MSF can be done to improve the elimination procedures within

4.2.3.1. "Internal" management

Encapsulation

Encapsulation is a method based on imbedding the hazardous waste within a lime,
cement and water mixture that solidifies within a clean steel or plastic drum that will
be sealed off once full. This method immobilizes the hazardous waste before it is
buried in a safe place. Attention has to be paid that different hazardous waste products
can't come in direct contact with each other, as this might lead to uncontrollable
chemical reactions. Thus, the hazardous waste has to stay in its primary packaging
(e.g. drugs pot, blister). For the same reason should laboratory reagents not be
encapsulated in steel drums.

☺ Advantages:
• Method usable for all inappropriate / expired hazardous products (even the only
possibility for aerosols if specialized elimination facilities aren’t available).
• Inexpensive method.
• Not so labour intensive as the hazardous waste can stay in its primary packaging.
• No specific material required (although that a cement mixer facilitates the job
enormously for very large quantities of hazardous waste).
• Simple method that permits the implementation by MSF itself (after appropriate
training of all the staff involved).
• Method can be implemented within the health structure, with potential burial
offsite in case of lack of space.

Disadvantages:
• Hazardous waste isn't destroyed and could potentially be recovered (although this
would be difficult).
• High quantities of encapsulated hazardous waste demand a lot of burial space
(possibly offsite burial in a controlled landfill).
• Special criteria for burial site (located far from water sources to avoid potential
contamination of ground and surface water, no access for unauthorised personnel).

In case the drums with encapsulated hazardous waste have to be buried offsite:
• Need for transport means to move the heavy drums.
• Potential need for special authorisations for burial within a controlled landfill.
• No further control on the encapsulated hazardous waste afterwards.

24

In case of antineoplastic drugs:
• Need for double encapsulation: small quantities of the same drug are encapsulated
within a small recipient (special container or a plastic bucket with well fitting lid),
which in turn will be encapsulated in the normal steel or plastic drum.
• Need for an impermeable layer (e.g. clay) underneath the buried drums.
• Takes some time: solidification of the mixtures takes 7 to 28 days before the
burial can take place.

Inertization

Inertization is a variant on waste immobilisation that is mainly used for some (semi-)
solid and powdered pharmaceutical waste. It differs from encapsulation because the
pharmaceutical waste is removed from its primary packaging and potentially crushed
before it is blended under powder form with the lime, cement and water mixture, to
finally be decanted in the ground. Attention has to be paid that different hazardous
waste products can't come in direct contact with each other as this might lead to
uncontrollable chemical reactions. Therefore should inertization be done for each
hazardous product separately.

☺ Advantages:
• Besides the waste being immobilized, it isn't accessible anymore because it has
been integrated within the mixture that eventually solidifies.
• Relatively inexpensive method.
• No specific material required (although that a cement mixer and a grinder or road
roller facilitate the job enormously for very large quantities of hazardous waste).
• Simple method that permits the implementation by MSF itself (after appropriate
training of all the staff involved).
• Method can be implemented within the health structure, with potential burial
offsite in case of lack of space.
• Method requires less space for burial in comparison with encapsulation.

Disadvantages:
• Method only usable for a limited number of pharmaceutical wastes: not for
liquids, hormones, psychotropic drugs, narcotics, antineoplastic medicines,
vaccines, ointments, aerosols and disinfectants.
• Labour intensive, as the hazardous waste has to be removed from its primary
packaging and potentially needs to be crushed.
• The opening of the packaging and the (potential) crushing of the pharmaceutical
waste create dust, thus extra attention has to be paid to protect the male staff
performing the inertization (e.g. ventilation, Personal Protective Equipment).

In case of inertization with offsite disposal:
• Need for transport:
o
Concrete mixer truck if mixture is made onsite (health structure).
o
Truck / pick up for moving the hazardous waste to the landfill where the
mixture will be prepared and disposed of.
• Potential need for special authorisations for hazardous waste disposal on a landfill.

25

Dissolving

Dissolving is a method where (semi-) solid and powdered hazardous waste is mixed
in a plastic drum with a minimum amount of clean non-salty water to the formers
dissolvability point before the mixture is discharged with preferably additional water
into a good closed sewer system or into a river with a high flow rate. To avoid
uncontrollable chemical reactions, every hazardous product should be treated
separately. Never discharge the mixture into slow moving or stagnant surface waters,
nor into groundwater.

☺ Advantages:
• The waste isn't accessible anymore because it has been dissolved, diluted and
discharged.
• Inexpensive method.
• No specific material required.
• Simple method that permits the implementation by MSF itself (after appropriate
training of all the staff involved).
• Method can be implemented within the health structure, with potential offsite
discharge in a closed sewer system, or a big and/or rapid flowing river.
• Method requires no space for residues as they are discharged.

Disadvantages:
• Method only usable for some inappropriate / expired hazardous products (not for
liquids, anti-infective drugs, (potential) foeto-toxics, hormones, psychotropic
drugs, narcotics, antineoplastic medicines, vaccines, ointments and disinfectants).
• Relatively labour intensive, as the hazardous waste has to be removed from its
primary packaging and stirred to be dissolved.
• Need for high quantities of clean non-salty water.
• Need for a good completely closed sewer system (also outside the dissolving site)
with preferably a water treatment plant, or a river with a good flow rate (never in
stagnant water such as ponds or lakes, nor into the ground(water) via soak away
pits or infiltration trenches).
• Potential need for transport of the mixture towards the sewer / river.
• Potential need for special authorisations to discharge hazardous waste in the sewer
or the river.
• Potential risk of pollution of water sources (surface and groundwater) if basic
rules aren't respected.

Attention:
• The minimum water amount needed to reach the dissolvability point of the (semi-)
solid or powdered hazardous waste should be respected as indicated per product.

Dilution

Dilution is a method where liquid hazardous waste is mixed in a plastic drum with a
minimum of clean non-salty water to avoid the latter being saturated, before the
mixture is discharged with preferably additional water to a good closed sewer system
or to a river with a high flow rate. Dilution is also often directly done at laboratory
level for small amounts of recurrently generated hazardous waste. To avoid
uncontrollable chemical reactions, every hazardous product should be treated
separately. Never discharge the mixture into slow moving or stagnant surface waters,
nor into groundwater.

26

☺ Advantages:
• The waste isn't accessible anymore because it has been diluted and discharged.
• Inexpensive method.
• No specific material required.
• Simple method that permits the implementation by MSF itself (after appropriate
training of all the staff involved).
• Method can be implemented within the health structure, with potential offsite
discharge in a closed sewer system or a big and/or rapid flowing river.
• Method requires no space for residues as they are discharged.

Disadvantages:
• Method only usable for some inappropriate / expired hazardous products (not for
(semi-) solids / powders, anti-infective drugs, (potential) foeto-toxics, hormones,
psychotropic drugs, narcotics, antineoplastic medicines, vaccines, ointments and
disinfectants).
• Relatively labour intensive, as the hazardous waste has to be removed from its
primary packaging and diluted (stirred) within the water.
• Need for high quantities of clean non-salty water.
• Need for a good completely closed sewer system (also outside the dilution site)
with preferably a water treatment plant, or a river with a good flow rate (never in
stagnant water such as ponds or lakes, nor into the ground(water) via soak away
pits or infiltration trenches).
• Potential need for transport of the mixture towards the sewer / river.
• Potential need for special authorisations to discharge hazardous waste in the sewer
or the river.
• Potential risk of pollution of water sources (surface and groundwater) if basic
rules aren't respected.
• Potential risk of accidents (certainly with acids) if specific rules aren't respected
(explicit training!).

Attention:
• The amount of water needed to avoid its saturation with liquid hazardous waste
should be respected as indicated per product.
• It is important to pour acid into the water and not the other way around as this
might create explosions. Acids should also be neutralised with lime before they
are discharged.

Batch incineration (e.g. De Montfort incinerator)

Batch incinerators, even made with double combustion chambers and heat resistant
materials are NOT an option to eliminate the majority of hazardous wastes (e.g.
laboratory chemicals, pharmaceutical waste) because they will emit very toxic gasses
due to the:
• potentially too low temperatures generated inside.
• big temperature drop each time the loading door is opened to introduce a new
batch of waste.
• too short retention time of the combustion gasses inside the furnace (should be
at least 2 seconds at 850 / 1200 °C).
Some products could even explode when heated, causing potentially serious injuries
when the loading or ash door is opened.

27

However, batch incineration can be done for "hazardous" wastes that aren't really that
harmful, even when incinerated at temperatures lower than 1200 °C and with a short
retention time of the gasses. This is the case for most laboratory diagnostic test kits
and some bio-hazardous wastes. Nevertheless, these wastes should only be introduced
into the batch incinerator when a good combustion is taking place. If needed,
additional combustibles will have to be added to reach a good combustion.

Empty combustible packaging of hazardous waste (e.g. cartons, blisters) can also be
batch incinerated. Packaging that is sharp and/or can potentially explode (vials,
ampoules, aerosols) should NEVER be batch incinerated in order to avoid accidents.

☺ Advantages:
• Potential "hazardous" waste is destroyed and thus can't be reused.
• Volume reduction of the waste.
• Relatively inexpensive method, if a batch incinerator is available.
• Relatively simple method that permits the implementation by MSF itself (after
appropriate training of all the staff involved).
• Method can be implemented within the health structure, if a batch incinerator and
ash / refuse pits are available.

Disadvantages:
• Very few "hazardous" waste products can be batch incinerated (mainly diagnostic
test kits, some bio-hazardous waste).
• Potential need for special authorisations to incinerate (hazardous) waste.
• Air pollution, certainly if basic rules aren't respected.
• Potential risk of accidents if basic rules aren't respected (training!).

Remark: Diagnostic test kits and empty combustible hazardous waste packaging
could be burnt in a well functioning drum volume reducer, if nothing else is available.

Evaporation

Evaporation can be an alternative to eliminate some laboratory liquids or X-ray
related development and fixer fluids, but only for limited quantities. It will work best
where the ambient air temperature is high and the relative humidity low. An
extremely well-ventilated area, potentially covered, far away and isolated from all
other activities (no access for unauthorized persons), should be available. The
evaporation will go faster if the liquid is poured in an open recipient having a big
contact area with the surrounding air. The material of the recipient should be
compatible with the liquid to be evaporated, so there isn’t a dangerous chemical
reaction (e.g. melting of the recipient, an exothermic reaction or even an explosion).

☺ Advantages:
• Inexpensive method if a correct area for evaporation already exists.
• No specific material required, except for a suited evaporation recipient.
• Simple method that permits the implementation by MSF itself (after appropriate
training of all the staff involved).
• Method can be implemented within the health structure.
• No space for burial required, nor a sewer system, river or incinerator.

28

Disadvantages:
• Very few hazardous waste products can be evaporated.
• Need for a well-ventilated area, potentially covered, where unauthorized people
can’t enter.
• Potential risk of accidents (e.g. with acids) if basic rules aren't respected
(training!).

“Recycling”

“Recycling” is a method where a waste product, which is potentially hazardous when
disposed of incorrectly, is reused for another less critical purpose, for which no real
hazards are involved. “Recycling” will mainly be done for inappropriate / expired
disinfectants, but also for disinfectant dilutions of more than a week old and leftovers
from prepared insecticides (both recurrently generated hazardous waste). The
preparation of the disinfectants will be according to the normal procedures, but in case
of doubt, it is recommended to contact the Head Quarter.

☺ Advantages:
• Potential hazardous waste is still put to good use.
• Money saving.
• No specific material required, except for recipients to store the liquid disinfectant.
• Simple method that permits the implementation by MSF itself (after appropriate
training of all the staff involved).
• Method can be implemented within the health structure.

Disadvantages:
• Very few hazardous waste products can be “recycled” (mainly disinfectants).
• Relatively labour intensive, as the hazardous waste has to be removed from its
primary packaging and dissolved / diluted.
• Need for high quantities of clean non-salty water.
• Potential risk of accidents if basic rules aren't respected (training!).

Remark: The silver of expired X-ray film (and pictures), as well as batteries and
some other hazardous waste can be recycled as well, but this has to be done by
specialized institutions / firms that are often not available in low-income countries.
Nevertheless, it’s recommended to check if such facilities exist in the region.

4.2.3.2. External management

Incineration

A distinction has to be made in between high and medium temperature incineration.
High temperature incineration can be used to eliminate most hazardous wastes (with
limitations related to the packaging), whereas medium temperature incineration is
much more restricted due to environmental and health risks.

It is easy to find out if there are industries that have high / medium temperature
incineration, but it is important to know the exact temperature the incinerator can
reach and the internal retention time for its combustion gasses. But before starting to
negociate with the industry, verify if high / medium temperature incineration in
industrial facilities strokes with the national legislation, if the latter is existing.

29

Administrative issues (described further) should be resolved before the actual
incineration can take place. Also practical details should be determined; for instance,
solid hazardous waste and/or the packaging might have to be grinded before being
introduced into the incinerator, because they could block its feeding system.

Incineration at high temperatures

To speak about high temperature incineration, the temperature in the furnace should
rise above 1200 °C and the combustion gasses should stay at this temperature for a
retention time of minimum 2 seconds. Under these conditions, the formation of
hazardous (toxic) by-products like dioxins (due to the combustion of halogens) and
acid rain (mainly because of sulphurs) stays limited.

Even in high income countries, only very few incinerators of class 1, specially
designed for the incineration of hazardous waste, with facilities for washing and
scrubbing the fumes in several steps, are existing. Even with such sophisticated
equipment, specific rules have to be followed for the incineration of ampoules and
vials, because the massive introduction of these items could lead to serious damages.

Certain industries like cement factories and blast furnaces have ovens that reach
temperatures above 1200 °C and have long retention times of the gasses at these high
temperatures. Therefore, they can be an interesting alternative for the elimination of
hazardous waste, also in low-income countries where these facilities are present.
Particularly cement factories are suited for the destruction of most of the
pharmaceutical waste, but also chemicals, used oils and tires as they reach
temperatures of at least 1400 °C, up to 2000 °C for the combustion gasses, with
retention times of several seconds. Nevertheless, attention should be paid for certain
hazardous wastes and more precisely their packaging. It will have to be checked if the
cement factory's manager will accept to incinerate ampoules and vials, due to the risks
of blockages of the feeding mechanism or explosions that might damage the
incinerator / kiln seriously. Aerosols should never be incinerated! For the elimination
itself, it is recommended that the ratio of hazardous waste doesn't exceed 5 % of the
total combustibles used.

☺Advantages:
• High temperature incineration is a destruction method.
• The technique can be used for most of the hazardous wastes (but never for
aerosols and possibly not for glass vials and ampoules either).
• The environmental impact and public health risks are less in comparison with
other elimination procedures.

Disadvantages:
• High temperature incineration equipment isn't available everywhere, and is
certainly not part of MSF's equipment.
• As quite some administrative work might be needed, it's not a method that can be
done quickly for a small amount of hazardous waste (except maybe if there is
already a clear Memorandum of Understanding between MSF and the industry).
• The hazardous waste has to be transported from the health structures to the
industry possessing high temperature facilities, which requires transport means.
• As the hazardous waste is eliminated offsite, the risk of fraude (e.g. drugs
disappearing and being resold) increases, thus good follow up of MSF is required.
• High temperature incineration might proove to be expensive.
• A good contract, at least with the industry manager, is essential.

30

Incineration at medium temperatures

To speak about medium temperature incineration, the temperature in the furnace
should rise certainly above 850 °C and the combustion gasses should stay at this
temperature for a retention time of minimum 2 seconds. These kinds of conditions can
be reached by some incineration plants designed to eliminate domestic waste. Such
incinerators can sometimes be found in the neighbourhood of big cities.

Most drugs don't possess halogens or sulphur, or only in very faint quantities (e.g.
approximately 0.1% of halogens). Thus when incinerated at medium temperatures, the
risk of forming lots of hazardous by-products due to halogens and sulphurs is limited,
reducing as such the impact on the environment and public health. Nevertheless, those
hazardous wastes that are allowed to be incinerated at medium temperature will still
have to be mixed with domestic waste at a weight ratio of 1/1000.

☺Advantages:
• Medium temperature incineration is a destruction method.
• In some big hospitals, there might be an incinerator installed, with double
combustion chamber, refractory bricks, fuel burners, and preferably an automatic
feeding system, which can reach the conditions of medium temperature
incineration, but this is very rare. Before introducing hazardous wastes in such an
incinerator, its working conditions should be verified. These specifications can be
found in the incinerator’s (operator’s) manual, or via the supplier / manufacturer.
In case of doubt, it's better to refraim from using it for hazardous wastes.

Disadvantages:
• Medium temperature incineration equipment isn't available everywhere, and is
certainly not part of MSF's equipment.
• Medium temperature incineration is not suited for hazardous wastes:
o
such as (potentially) foeto-toxic drugs, hormones, antineoplastic drugs,
vaccines, ointments and disinfectants.
o
having packaging that can potentially block the automatic feeding system
and/or explode (e.g. vials, ampoules, and certainly not aerosols).
• As quite some administrative work might be needed, it's not a method that can be
done quickly for a small amount of hazardous waste (except maybe if there is
already a clear Memorandum of Understanding existing in between MSF and the
incineration plant).
• The hazardous waste has to be transported from the health structures to the
incinerator plant, which requires transport means.
• As the hazardous waste is eliminated offsite, the risk of fraude (e.g. drugs
disappearing and being resold) increases, thus good follow up of MSF is required.
• Medium temperature incineration might proove to be expensive.
• A good contract, at least with the incinerator manager, is essential.

Attention:
•
Even when crushed, glass ampoules, bottles and vials shouldn't be incinerated at
medium temperature because the glass (molten or not) could introduce blockages
in the incineration system. Therefore, medium temperature incineration hasn't
been proposed in the list of potential elimination options for hazardous wastes in
these kinds of packaging.
•
The preliminary work (finding a good medium temperature incinerator) and the
administrative work needed are similar to high temperature incineration.
•
Rather small commercial incinerators (e.g. Medi Burn, Strongman) are not suited
for medium temperature incineration (weight ratio of 1/1000 of domestic waste!).

Return to manufacturer

In each situation, the possibility of returning hazardous products (waste) to the
manufacturer (or donor) that will take care of the destruction or safe storage should be
studied. However, hazardous wastes are mostly considered as (very) dangerous
products, and thus it is often difficult, if not impossible to get the authorisations for
their transport across the different country borders. In case of a quality problem due to
and/or a batch recall from a drugs manufacturer, it should be their responsibility to get
the drugs back and to replace them for free. However, the return of the drugs towards
the manufacturer is often a logistical nightmare. It is recommended in these situations
to involve your section pharmacist.

☺Advantages:
• The manufacturer (or donor) becomes potentially responsible for the hazardous
waste (e.g. pharmaceuticals), but this doesn't guarantee their correct elimination
(certainly not if hazardous products have been purchased locally / regionally).

Disadvantages:
• Special authorisations are needed to cross the borders (difficult, if not impossible
to obtain).
• Needs a lot of administrative work, thus it is very time consuming.
• Very expensive method as it requires the transport of dangerous goods (if
accepted anyway by the transporters).

For all these reasons, it is doubtful that returning the hazardous waste to the
manufacturer (or donor) is feasible as a common solution for the field.

4.2.4. Preparing the elimination procedures

The following steps will have to be done before the elimination procedures can start.
A distinction has been made in between onsite elimination (“internal” management)
and offsite elimination in a specialized institution or industry (external management):

• Locate an appropriate site:
o
In case of “internal” management, the treatment and final disposal will
mostly be done onsite the health structure, at least if the needed disposal
options (e.g. long-term waste zone in a hospital, completely closed sewer
system) are available. But offsite treatment and/or disposal at an appropriate
site (e.g. controlled landfill for encapsulation) will sometimes be inevitable.
o
When the site of the elimination institution or industry is verified and
approved, it should still be investigated which and how many hazardous
wastes they are willing to take (e.g. refusal to incinerate vials and ampoules

negociations (e.g. price, possibility to obtain an elimination certificate) start.
• Sort out the administrative issues of hazardous waste elimination:
o
Official authorizations might be necessary for the elimination of hazardous
waste; probably when they have to be done offsite, and certainly when it
concerns controlled substances (as well for onsite and offsite elimination).
o
If the preliminary contacts with the elimination institution or industry are
positive, it is highly recommended to ask for a written permission at the
authorities for the elimination of the hazardous waste by the proposed
procedure. MSF's preference is for a general authorisation, but it’s possible
that only a permission for a specific list of hazardous wastes can be obtained.
o
Once the authorisations obtained, a contract with the elimination institution
or industry can be signed.
• Organize the right human resources:
o
Trustworthy staff will have to be identified, trained for the job and provided
with the appropriate protective equipment (see annexes). Be aware that the

to avoid all risks with (potentially) foeto-toxic drugs, or other toxicities.
o
Elimination institutions or industries should have their own specialized
personnel and safety procedures.
• Arrange the right equipment and consumables:
o
The MSF “internal” elimination procedures demand only simple means such
as 200 l drums, considerable amounts of water, cement and lime (see the
different elimination protocols within the annexes). In case large quantities
of hazardous waste need to be eliminated, it can be handy to have a cement
mixer, a grinder / road roller and transport facilities available.
o
Elimination institutions or industries may use high-tech solutions, but these
are their responsibility.

4.2.5. (Potential) transport of the hazardous waste

Whatever the scenario, it might be necessary to transport the hazardous waste from
one location to another (e.g. from different health structures to one that is equipped
and has trained staff to deal with this specific problem). The decision to transport the
hazardous waste towards another MSF supervised health structure or even completely
offsite (e.g. towards an external facility) depends on constraints such as the national
legislation, the health structure being located in an urban zone or the big amount of
hazardous waste that has been generated; or opportunities like the existence of an
elimination institute (e.g. recycling facilities) or a cement factory in the region. It’s
very important that the hazardous waste transport happens under strict conditions (e.g.
correct packaging of the hazardous waste, trustworthy staff doing the transport, filling
out waybills).

33

Special precautions should be taken into consideration when transporting hazardous
waste:
o All products will have to be protected against physical damage:
They should stay within their original packaging and transported inside
solid containers (e.g. cardboard boxes, clean plastic drums) with clear
labelling as prepared during the isolation of the hazardous waste. In case
the outer packaging of a laboratory reagent is missing, its recipient should
be placed in a solid plastic container and surrounded preferably with
vermiculite. This material has a shock-absorbing effect and will absorb the
reagent if its primary packaging would be leaking. In case vermiculite
would not be available, coarse sand could be an alternative.
All containers should be well bonded within the vehicle.
o All containers should be kept away from direct sunlight, heat, water and
combustibles, whilst some laboratory reagents should be protected from
freezing.
o Incompatible laboratory reagents (see table in 4.2.1. Isolation) should
preferably not be transported together, and certainly not in the same container.

Each transport of hazardous waste should be accompanied by a waybill and a packing
list because:
o it is imperative with such products to know what is on board of the vehicle,
potentially for legal matters and certainly in case of an accident;
o a correct follow up reduces the risk of potential fraud (e.g. certain products
disappearing en route).

4.2.6. Implementing the elimination procedures

Once the preparation and potential transport are finished, the actual elimination
procedure should start soon afterwards.

Before the actual elimination, the hazardous wastes should be sorted according to
their preferred elimination option:
o Once the sorting according the different options done and just before the
chosen elimination procedure starts, the hazardous waste should be removed
from their outer packaging. For the inertization, dissolving and dilution
elimination methods, the hazardous waste should also be removed from their
primary packaging (e.g. blister, ampoule, plastic container). This should be
done with one kind of product at the time during the elimination procedure
itself to avoid contact in between different molecules, which could lead to
uncontrollable chemical reactions. For encapsulation, the hazardous waste
should remain in their primary packaging to avoid contact in between
different molecules when they are encapsulated together. Be aware that
certain laboratory reagents are incompatible (see 4.2.1.) and should never be
encapsulated together. Antineoplastic drugs should be double encapsulated
separately per product.
o
In case the procedures take place at an elimination institution or industry, the
potential sorting of the hazardous waste will have to be done according to the
agreements made with them. Thus it is possible that the sorting will have to
be performed by MSF beforehand.

The outer packaging, and for some procedures (e.g. inertization, dissolving, dilution)
also the empty primary packaging are often remaining after the elimination of the

o Sturdy drugs containers with a well fitting lid and of a good volume can be
kept to be modified into single use sharps containers, after the removal of their
label and having been rinsed inside.
o Cardboard boxes and other combustible material that are non-hazardous can
be (batch) incinerated.
o Empty glass vials and ampoules can be thrown in a sharps pit (after potential

o Aerosol canisters should be encapsulated.
o Containers of laboratory reagents / insecticides / rodenticides / disinfectants
should be encapsulated or destroyed by high temperature incineration. Even
when empty, they can still be hazardous since they retain product residues
(vapours, liquid). They should never be washed and/or used for other purposes
either.

Remarks:
o Although the internal management is most often done by logistics staff, it’s
strongly recommended to have a medical person present during the
elimination procedures to give advice on the pharmaceutical / laboratory
products in case of doubt, and/or to intervene if an accident would occur.
o During all handling, special protective equipment (gloves, boots, overall,
respirator or at least a dust mask, goggles / face shield for certain products)
should be worn. They should be removed at the elimination site when the
work with the hazardous waste is finished. Do avoid cross-contamination with
personal clothing.
o Hands should be washed before eating.
o Hands, face and neck should be washed when exiting the elimination site, and
having shower facilities on the premises would be a great asset.
o Eating, drinking and smoking at the elimination site should be prohibited.

4.2.7. Keeping records of all relevant documents

After the elimination, a copy of the list of the inappropriate / expired hazardous
products (waste) should safely be filed because it can serve as justification when
needed (e.g. for audits of donors or authorities). The potential authorisations, and in
case of offsite management, the contract, the transport waybill, the industry's or
institution’s certificate of elimination (if possible, signed by the authorities as well)
should all be filed together.

5. Conclusions

• Manage orders according to consumption rates and available goods in the
warehouse / pharmacy because this will reduce the amount of hazardous waste.

• Eliminate the recurrently generated hazardous waste on a daily, (two) weekly or
monthly basis.

• Eliminate inappropriate / expired hazardous products at least once every six
months during special campaigns, by:
o Isolating the hazardous waste.
o Making the list of all the hazardous products that need to be
eliminated.
o Choosing the most appropriate elimination procedure per product (it is
recommended to involve the Head Quarter in the choice).
o Preparing the procedures.
o (Potentially) transporting the hazardous waste.
o Implementing the elimination procedures.
o Keeping records of all relevant documents.

• In case a national legislation concerning hazardous waste is existing:
o Verify the persistence of this national legislation.
o Verify the existence of institutions which are specialized in the elimination
of hazardous waste (recycling included).

• In case the MSF onsite strategy is chosen, follow strictly the elimination rules to
avoid as much as possible environmental and health risks:
o Choose and prepare the site well for the elimination of the hazardous waste.
o Check whether administrative requirements are to be fulfilled (e.g. informing
the authorities, etc.).
o Make sure that the staff performing the elimination procedures is well trained
and protected for the job.
o Make sure to have all the equipment and consumables ready before the
implementation starts.
o Follow the protocols strictly as described in the annexes of this manual.

• In case the (MSF) offsite strategy is chosen:
o Verify the facilities and procedures by asking for a visit of the elimination
institution or industry. In case they are unsatisfactory, check if correct
facilities and procedures can be set up (according the legislation or
international regulations).
o Check whether administrative requirements are to be fulfilled (e.g.
informing the authorities, taxes, etc.).
o Make a good contract with the elimination institution or industry, and
potentially as well with the transport company.
o Do the follow up of the hazardous waste until their final elimination.

Encapsulation

Equipment needed:

• Metal or preferably plastic drum(s) of about 200 l
with lid.
The drums have to be clean inside and
should not have contained dangerous
and/or explosive components.
It is strongly recommended NOT to use
metallic drums for lab reagents as violent
reactions might occur due to potential
corrosion, or due to the heat created during
the closure of the drum by welding.
• Portland cement, lime and clean non-salty water.
• Trowel.
• Measuring recipient / shovel.
• Mixing equipment (e.g. masons' bucket / cement
mixer for very big quantities of hazardous waste).
• In case of a closed metallic drum: a chisel and
hammer / a puncturing device and electrical saw.
• Welding equipment and its accessories (rods,
brush, picking hammer, protective clothing).
• Waste zone / (protected) landfill.

Encapsulation: Step 1

Open the drum.

• In case of a closed metallic drum, cut open its top
part (cover) over 75% of its circumference (with
hammer and chisel, or saw) and bend the metal
towards the outside, or take the cover completely
off. This is just to have a good accessibility inside
the drum.

Encapsulation: Step 2

Prepare a small amount of cement – lime – water
mixture in a mixing recipient.

• The mixture is made with a 3 - 3 - 1 proportion (by
weight) of cement, lime and water.

42

Encapsulation: Step 3

Put before anything else a thin layer of this
mixture on the bottom of the drum.

• The layer should be 2 – 3 cm thick.
• Before any of the hazardous wastes are
introduced, the thin layer should somewhat have
solidified.

Encapsulation: Step 4

Put the hazardous wastes inside the drum until
it has reached 3⁄4 of its volume (height).

• The hazardous wastes have to stay in their own
closed primary packaging; containers, ampoules,
vials, bottles, blisters and/or tubes. Containers
partly full can be topped up with strictly the same
product. Incompatible (laboratory) chemicals
should even be encapsulated in separate drums
(see further in Attention). If these rules are not
respected, different products can start to have
chemical reactions with each other, which may
become a (public health) risk. The cardboard
boxes around the blisters, ampoules, tubes, etc.
can be removed however (and incinerated / burnt
if wanted) to gain some space.
• Antineoplastic (cytotoxic) drugs should be
collected and encapsulated separately from other
products (see further in Important).
• The hazardous wastes have to be put inside the
drum in such a way that they take the least space
possible without touching the side of the drum
(gap of at least 2 cm). Some openings should be
left between the primary packaging, in order for
the mixture that will be poured in afterwards to fill
up the gaps in between the products, and thus to
form the secondary barrier.
Encapsulation: Step 5

Top up the drum completely with the cement –
lime – water mixture.

• The same 3 – 3 – 1 mixture can be used to top
up the drum, but some extra water can be added
in order to make it more liquid and thus to
infiltrate better in between the gaps of the
hazardous wastes' packaging. Adding more
water means that it will take longer for the
mixture to solidify however.

43

Encapsulation: Step 6

Close the drum with its lid once completely
topped up with mixture, and seal it off.

• The sealing of a plastic drum can be done with
glue.
• The lid of a metallic drum should be welded
(preferably seam or spot welding). In case the
cover was cut open and bended back, it should
be refolded towards the inside first.
• The welding should not be done when flammable
or explosive products are encapsulated.

Encapsulation: Step 7

Bury the closed drum at a safe location, far
away from water resources (wells, springs,
rivers, etc.) and well above the groundwater
table (also during the rainy season).

• Safe locations might be:
the controlled waste zone of a long-term
health structure;
a controlled landfill designed for this purpose,
but this might require a specific authorization.
• The best is to bury the drums once their mixture
has solidified.

Remarks:

- Encapsulation has the big advantage that it is a solution that can be used for all kinds of
hazardous wastes. However, when and where possible, the other WHO compliant
solutions should be preferred because they really eliminate the hazardous waste, whilst
encapsulation is only an immobilization of the products before safe burial.
- Before the implementation of the encapsulation technique, it is important to collect all
relevant information on the final disposal possibilities for the drums.
- If burial is momentarily impossible, the drums can be stocked in the treatment area of the
health structure’s waste zone until an appropriate place is found to bury them safely. But
a final solution will have to be found before MSF leaves the project, mission or country.
- Take security precautions that the final disposal site can’t be accessed by unauthorized
persons or even animals.
- Know that once the mixture is inside, the drum becomes very heavy. So the procedure
should preferably be done closely to the final disposal site, or facilities should be
available to move the filled drum.
- When the amount of hazardous waste is insufficient to fill the drum for 3⁄4 of its height, the
encapsulation can be done by filling the drum with the cement - lime - water mixture up
to 5 cm above the waste products. This drum can then be set apart until a next
encapsulation takes place (the normal filling procedures are to be followed at that
moment).

Inertization

Equipment needed:

• Grinding pot / grinder / road or garden roller.
• Portland cement, lime and clean non-salty water.
• Trowel.
• Measuring recipient / shovel.
• Mixing equipment (e.g. a masons' bucket or a
concrete mixer for big quantities of hazardous
waste).
• Waste zone / (protected) landfill.

• If (a big quantity of) inertization mixture has to be
transported: a concrete mixer truck.

Inertization: Step 1

Make sure that all hazardous wastes are well
segregated.

• Inertization has to be done for one hazardous
waste at the time, because adding further
molecules (e.g. from other drugs) into the mixture
increases the risk of creating toxic compounds.

Inertization: Step 2

Remove the hazardous waste completely from
its packaging, even from blisters, vials, or
ampoules.

• The product must be removed from its packaging
(different
than
for
encapsulation).
As
the
inertization takes place for one hazardous waste
at the time, there isn't any risk of getting in
contact with other molecules.
• When opening the recipients of the hazardous
waste, be careful for injuries as some of them
might be sharp (e.g. the edges of an opened
ampoule).
• The operators should always wear a mask to
avoid inhaling hazardous waste dust.
• The cardboard or plastic packaging and blisters
can be burnt / incinerated; glass packaging
should be disposed of in a sharps pit (with
crushing facilities), or encapsulated and buried if
their amount is big.

46

Inertization: Step 3

Pulverize the hazardous waste into powder, if
required.

• The pulverization can be done in a grinding pot,
by a grinder, or by a garden / road roller in case of
big quantities of hazardous waste. In the latter
case, a smooth flat surface should be available to
be able to crush all the hazardous waste correctly.
• Whatever pulverization system is chosen, it
should always be done in a place that is protected
from wind and rain, to avoid that the hazardous
waste dust would be blown or washed away. On
the other hand, there should
be enough
ventilation and the operators should always wear
a mask to avoid inhaling hazardous waste dust.

Inertization: Step 4

Mix the hazardous waste powder, cement, lime
and water until a homogenous paste is formed.

• The mixture should be made according the
following weight ratio:
Hazardous waste: 65 %
Lime: 15 %
Cement: 15 %
Water: 5 % (or more) to form a liquid
consistency that can easily
be poured.
• This mixture can be made manually or preferably
in a concrete mixer when big quantities of
hazardous waste have to be inertizised.

Inertization: Step 5

Disperse the mixture at a safe area.

• The disposal area should be away from water
sources (wells, springs, river, etc.). The disposal
area should preferably be done within or nearby
the waste zone of the health structure. In order to
gain space, the mixture could be poured in a hole
/ trench, as long as the water table isn't too high.
• An alternative is a landfill where the mixture can
be decanted into normal domestic waste where it
will settle. If the inertization procedure isn't done
at the landfill itself, a concrete mixer truck will be
needed to transport the mixture under liquid form
to the site.
• For a better environmental protection, it is
advised to pour the mixture into recipients (until it
has solidified) instead of directly on the ground.

47

Remarks:

- Inertization can only be done for a limited number of (semi-) solid and powdered
pharmaceuticals.
- Once the inertization of one hazardous waste is completed, the procedure for the
next product can start.
- The mixture of a second product being inertizised can only be put on a previous
layer when the latter is already sufficiently solidified (interval of at least one week).
- The mixture should NEVER be used for construction because the chemicals of the
hazardous waste can affect the cement binding.

Attention:

It is possible to dissolve acids under powder form, but before discharging the solution
into a closed sewer system or a big / fast flowing river, it should be neutralized first.
As dissolving and neutralizing acids can be dangerous practices, they should be
performed in a very well ventilated location by a person (laboratory technician,
chemist) who understands very well the risks involved and the procedures described
below. All specific protective clothing should be available and worn. If these
conditions can’t be met, the powdered acid should NOT be dissolved and
neutralized, BUT encapsulated or potentially incinerated at high temperature (if
acceptable by the manager)!

Specific protective clothing is required: chemical resistant apron, splash-proof
goggles or even better a full-face shield, nitrile gloves and preferably also long gloves
or gauntlets.

- Prepare separately a 6N base solution by adding 336 g of CaO per litre of clean,
non-salty water. The quantity of 6N base solution to be prepared will have to
correspond with the amount of acid powder to dissolve, which can be deducted
from the ratio indicated in the MSF-list. Having too much of this 6N solution would
create the problem of its safe disposal afterwards, for which an additional acid
would be required.
- Fill a Poly Ethylene (PE) recipient (e.g. food quality 20 l bucket or 125 l container)
with 10 l of clean, non-salty water.
- Add the acid powder (only 1 product at the time) little by little to the water according
to the ratio given in the MSF-list and stir. Do NOT add the water to the acid as this
might create very violent chemical reactions which can cause serious injuries. As
the temperature of the dissolved mixture might rise considerably, some ice might
have to be added.
- Add the 6N base solution, small quantities at a time, to the recipient with the
dissolved mixture and stir, whilst measuring the pH of the liquid with pH paper /
strips or a pH meter. Keep on adding the 6N base solution until a correct pH is
reached (ideally around 7, certainly above 6 and not above 9).
- Wash the whole solution down the closed sewer or big / fast flowing river, after an
additional dilution of 1 part of solution to 20 parts of clean, non-salty water.

protective clothing should be available and worn. If these conditions can’t be met, the
acid should NOT be diluted and neutralized, BUT encapsulated or potentially
incinerated at high temperature (if acceptable by the manager)!

Specific protective clothing is required: chemical resistant apron, splash-proof
goggles or even better a full-face shield, nitrile gloves and preferably also long gloves
or gauntlets.

- Prepare separately a 6N base solution by adding 336 g of CaO per litre of clean,
non-salty water. The quantity of 6N base solution to be prepared will have to
correspond with the amount of acid to dilute, which can be deducted from the ratio
indicated in the MSF-list. Having too much of this 6N solution would create the
problem of its safe disposal afterwards, for which an additional acid would be
required.
- Fill a Poly Ethylene (PE) recipient (e.g. food quality 20 l bucket or 125 l container)
with 10 l of clean, non-salty water.
- Add the acid (only 1 product at the time) little by little to the water according to the
ratio given in the MSF-list and stir. Do NOT add the water to the acid as this might
create very violent chemical reactions which can cause serious injuries. As the
temperature of the dilution might rise considerably, some ice might have to be
added.
- Add the 6N base solution, small quantities at a time, to the recipient with the diluted
mixture and stir, whilst measuring the pH of the liquid with pH paper / strips or a pH
meter. Keep on adding the 6N base solution until a correct pH is reached (ideally
around 7, and certainly above 6 and not above 9).
- Wash the whole solution down the closed sewer or big / fast flowing river, after an
additional dilution of 1 part of solution to 20 parts of clean, non-salty water.

Remarks:
- Chemicals (e.g. pharmaceutical waste, laboratory waste, disinfectants) should
NEVER be eliminated with batch incineration. Most laboratory diagnostic test kits
can be incinerated in a (batch) double combustion incinerator however.
- Some bio-hazardous waste (e.g. sputum of Tuberculosis patients, blood samples)
contained in non-sharp / non exploding containers can be treated with a batch
incinerator, but a very good combustion should be ongoing. This will require most
of the time additional combustibles to keep high combustion temperatures, despite
the high liquid content of the bio-hazardous waste. Auto-combustion of bio-
hazardous waste together with soft waste from the wards will most probably be
insufficient, certainly when rather big amounts of sputum from the Tuberculosis
wards need to be incinerated.
- Burnable (non-sharp / non-exploding) empty packaging of hazardous waste can be
considered as soft waste, and can thus also be burned in a (drum) volume reducer,
if a double combustion incinerator wouldn't be available.
- In places where kerosene or (natural) gas is used, do contact your technical referral
person as special safety and operating measures will be required!
- Once all the waste bins are emptied, they can be moved towards the washing area
of the waste zone for cleaning and disinfection.
