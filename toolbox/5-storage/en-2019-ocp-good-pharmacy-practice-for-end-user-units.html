Good Pharmacy Practice for End-User Unit

All the resources involved in having the medicine reach the patient will come to naught if the patient does not receive
the right medicine, at the right form, at the right dose and at the posology prescribed, along with clear instructions
and a packaging assuring its quality (same rule for medical devices).

SELECTION AND STORAGE

1. Medical Standard List ( MSL)

It consists on the list of medical articles needed for the correct running of the unit that has to be validated and that
should contain both consumable and not consumable medical articles.

Activities and related protocols in each End User Unit should have been already defined


Medical protocols in use.

For the same drug: avoid multiple strengths / forms as much as possible.

Oral route should be first option as parenteral / injectable ones are only recommended in severe
cases or when oral route is not suitable (digestive intolerance, ill-absorption, etc.).

Preferably restrict use of syrups and suspensions for hospital pediatric wards, nutritional programs
and/or specific programs.

For the same indication: avoid therapeutic duplicates as much as possible(If further information
needed, refer to the document located in Mymsf->Medical Toolbox-> Pharmacy-> 04 Pharmacy Management in
units-> OCP Therapeutic List)

If further information is needed, refer to Essential Drugs guideline - Part two – Chapters “Organization and management of a
pharmacy” & “Prescription, cost, compliance”. http://refbooks.msf.org/

2. Responsibility for the pharmacy (at End User Unit)

The End User Unit needs to have a 'pharmacy responsible' and the keys of the cupboard have to be kept with the
pharmacy responsible (usually this responsible is the Supervisor of the Unit.)
This responsibility task should be mentioned in the Job Description.

4. Storing medical items

Drugs and medical material should be organized in a place suitable for their storage. It is important to consider
preservation conditions: light, humidity, temperature.

Within a health unit, determine storing space for products needing cold chain, psychotropic and narcotics and ensure
all staff receives specific training (important to always consider HR needs);

Refer to Pharma Toolbox for any kind of document you could use for training purposes;

For countries where temperature reaches 30°C, do not have a stock in the unit superior to 2-3 weeks consumption.

4.1 Arranging cupboards

Its location should be:

-
Close to the nurse’s working area
-
Well illuminated to avert errors

Intermediate levels of pharmacy are not recommended, as more human resources are needed, as well as the number
of inventories and the increase of chances of making mistakes.

Interior organization:

– enables to follow the products organization rules (see further on)
– use a storing cupboard which offers good visibility and ease of access to each product
– Washable

4.2 Organization of medicines and medical supplies

Effective organization will facilitate management and reduce risks of errors during dispensing. A Medical Standard List
(MSL) should be visible at the ward. All medicines should be identified by their description (INN), dosage and
strength.
In case of local purchase: Brand names may be added next to the INN.

Product organization has to observe the basic rules cited for stock management: MSF family (DORA, DINJ, DEXT, etc.)
and alphabetical order.

If drugs are repacked in a different container, keep in mind that plastic bags cannot be used longer than 7 days, and
appropriate containers need to be cleaned and disinfected accordingly: DRUGS CONTAINER, plastic, screw cover, 250
ml ( MSF code: SDDCCONT250L); Labels need to be removed and relabeled;

Particular consideration should be given for proper labelling, storage and /or segregation of high-risk medical items
(*antimicrobials, anti-viral, potassium, insulins, etc.) as well as those ones that look very similar: i.e. atropine and
adrenaline...



4.3 Storage for opening suspensions

Refer to manufacturer recommendation for shelf life when bottle opened: some oral suspensions must be kept
refrigerated; their storage at room temperature is limited to a few days and with syrups there is a risk of
fermentation.

5. Management of particular stocks

5.1 Narcotics

Narcotics are kept in a safe locked (separate from the rest of the stock) with a responsible for it, who is the supervisor
of the unit.
Control of reception and delivery its mandatory: double check for the different steps (reception, packing, dispatch...)
Delivery “hand to hand” in the wards from pharmacy manager to End user unit supervisor should be done.

A register for narcotic and psychotropic drugs is mandatory in each ward.

Verify with Coordination the applicable measures to follow in your country concerning the return of empty vials.

5.2 Dangerous goods


Follow manufacturer's instructions

Keep original protective packaging

Always sheltered away from heat, light and moisture: in a dry, well ventilated area, protected from solar
radiation

Store products classified as explosive, oxidizing or extremely flammable in specific areas.

If possible, store toxic products in a lockable (metal) cabinet

For safety reasons, the bottles should be stored on the lower shelves (lower part). Watch out for leaks

Beware of "Good / Bad neighbors": incompatibilities between certain products-> information available in
Mymsf->Medical Toolbox-> Pharmacy-> 3. Main Pharmacy Management->34.Products with specific management

5.3 Cytotoxic drugs

There are products like bleomycin, vincristine or doxorubicine... and all oncology products in general.

They show the following sign in the catalogue:


No risk during storage in the pharmacy.
However: measure as a precaution = no manipulation by pregnant women.

Dangerous medicines for the personnel handling them (preparation). Therefore there should be preventive protective
measures for preparation: gloves (nitrile), gown, mask, etc. as well as written procedures displayed => What to do in
case of broken bottles/vials, contact the product on the skin or contact of the product on eyes.

If further information needed, refer to the MSF catalogue, volume 1: drugs->introduction on drugs->cytotoxic drugs

GOOD DISPENSING PRACTICES

A. Out Patient Department (OPD)

1. Factors contributing to effective dispensing practices

The dispenser has to issue the correct medicine in the form and dose prescribed, and in quantity corresponding to
the duration mentioned on the prescription.
Ideally, he has to be able to detect an error done at the time of the prescription, a misunderstanding from the patient
about his illness and his treatment.

He has to be rigorous in registering all drug movements in the management record book.

The dispensing room must allow the correct storage of drugs: a lot of medicines are sensitive to heat, humidity and
light. The room must have a large counter, easily washable, enabling the proper preparation of the medicines, and an
office to keep all management files. The room has to be equipped with a water sink and a filter (administration of first
doses to the patients, hygiene for dispensing, use of gloves: these ones need to be changed regularly and avoid keep
the same pair of gloves the whole journey).

2. Dispensing of products

Before reaching the pharmacy, the patients have usually waited for the consultation.
They are often tired and in a hurry to go home and therefore not always listening to information. It is therefore
important that the dispensing rooms are comfortable and confidential.

Prescriptions are written on the patient’s health card, on a prescription form or on the hospitalisation card.
In these documents, must be included:
– The prescriber’s name, ward or health centre,
– The prescriber’s signature,
– The patient’s name
– The age/weight for children, for adults as well depending on the pathology,
– The prescription date.

The setup of the prescription must observe the following rules:

– Description of drugs using INN, dosage (mg, etc.) form (tab., inj., etc.) and administration route
– Number of times per day and total number of days to complete treatment,
– Using well-known acronyms: « g » for gram, « ml » for millilitre,
– Clear and legible writing to avoid mistakes when dispensing,
– Information must be written in a language dispensers understand.

3. Preparation of products

It is recommended to double-check prescriptions when possible. If two people work in the pharmacy, treatment
should be prepared by one of them and checked by the second. This reduces the risk of mistakes with the
prescription. (Things to avoid: reconditioning cream /ointment in a plastic bag)

3.1 Preparing tablets and capsules

Medicines pre-packed in blister (ACT, contraceptives) should not be unpacked.

Important note: plastic bags (MSF code: SDDCBAGP06) can be used for treatments no longer than 7 days. They are
neither opaque nor water-resistant. The quality of the products is not assured beyond that time.

For chronic treatments or for treatments longer than a week, an opaque and hermetically closed container can be
ordered: DRUGS CONTAINER, plastic, amber (MSF code: SDDCCONT150AL, SDDCCONT30AL, SDDCCONT60AL)

Using a ballpoint-pen or a marker, write legibly on each plastic bag before packing the drugs:
• Drug description (INN) and strength
• Dose under each pictogram indicating the time of intake,
• Treatment duration
• Expiry date/Dispensing date

The dispenser has to:

-Have clean hands.
-Calculate the number of tablets necessary for the whole treatment.
-Use a spoon or a tablet-counter. Never use fingers to count tablets (MSF code: EDDC).

-Clean regularly the tablet counter (or use different spoons and different tablet counters) to prevent any cross
contamination, some patients being allergic to very small quantities of medicines (e.g. penicillin)

Avoid errors in preparation:
– Confirm that the label of the product packaging corresponds to the prescription.
– Close the container. Do not leave drug containers open on the working table.

3.2 Preparing standardised treatments

For drugs that are not in blister, it can be prepared in bags in advance (maximum the day before to prevent any
degradation of the product). Adequate labelling of such bags is crucial.

Pre-packing allows safer and faster dispensing with less risks of error. Keep the bags from humidity and light.

3.3 Preparing oral liquids

As good storage conditions are not guaranteed at home, syrups and oral solutions should neither be prescribed nor
dispensed to outpatients except for particular cases.

It is recommended to limit their use for numerous reasons:

– Risk of incorrect usage
Outside of hospitals, determining the correct dosage is hazardous: spoons never contain standard volumes (soup
spoons, dessert spoons, tea spoons). Oral suspensions should be prepared with a specified amount of clean water,
and well shaken prior to administration. There is therefore a risk of overdose or giving an insufficient dosage.
Some oral suspensions must be kept refrigerated; their storage at room temperature is limited to a few days, and with
syrups there is a risk of fermentation.
In numerous countries syrups are thought of as "cough medicine". Confusion between cough mixtures and
antibacterial suspensions or syrups is common.

– Economic considerations
Compared to the price of tablets or capsules, the price of syrups and oral suspensions is considerably higher. Even
using a powder for subsequent reconstitution, the costs may be 2 to 7 times higher than an equivalent dose due to
the cost of the bottle itself and higher transportation costs due to weight and volume.

Refer to the Essential drugs book->Part two -> Prescription, cost, compliance
