1. Objectives / Background

Stock management of medical products (pharmaceutical products, medical devices, in vitro
diagnostics, and specialized food) is challenging for many reasons in MSF contexts including
forecasting challenges, delivery lead-time, storage space and among which limited shelf-life of
products remains a major constraint.

The expiry date of a medical product reflects the period during which a product can be used if it
is stored according to its labeled storage conditions. It is determined using stability studies
performed by the manufacturer. Stability studies are required for pharmaceutical products,
specialized food, IVDs and the most critical medical devices.

Pharmaceutical products may degrade and lose potency or become toxic over time. Physical
changes may lead to reduced absorption, rendering the products less effective and impurities
may develop that could affect the product’s safety profile. Therefore, once the expiry date has
passed there is no guarantee that the medicine will be safe and effective.

Medical devices and in vitro diagnostics performance are also affected over time, and their
integrity, usability, sensitivity, specificity, and sterility may be compromised as relevant, with a
potential impact on the patient and/or end-user safety.

Nutritional properties of specialized food also degrade after the expiry date. For fortified food,
(i.e., all NFOS products used in MSF programs), the micronutrient content decreases over the
span of the shelf-life and cannot be claimed after the ‘best before date’. Therefore, the
therapeutic properties of the products are lost, and expired products constitute an immediate
danger to human health after a short period.

In any case, the manufacturer cannot be held responsible for any harmful effects, adverse
events or lack of efficacy if the product is used after its expiry date.

Destroying expired medical products is costly, negatively impacts the environment, requires
paperwork, and can damage MSF image if being seen as a wasteful organization. Therefore, it is
tempting to extend the product shelf-life for potential cost and environmental motivations, and/or
reserve them for non-medical use. Even if it is acknowledged that the health risk is minimal in
some circumstances, it is important to be aware of the following aspects:

1. Reputational risk: Whatever the type of medical products, using a product beyond its

expiry date can damage MSF’s image from the perspective of the patient, caregivers, our
own staff, and be seen by the local authorities as a negligence of basic quality assurance
principles regarding patient safety, and therefore jeopardize the guarantee of MSF’s
presence in a country.

2. Legal accountability: As a distributor of medical products but also as healthcare service

provider, MSF is bound by legal and regulatory obligations with respect to the
management and the disposal of expired products. Moreover, as explained above, a
manufacturer/supplier can no longer be held responsible for the quality of the goods if
used after the expiry date, meaning MSF would not have any possible recourse against
the supplier in case of any events related to the use of the expired goods, including if
such damage might not be related to the fact that the goods are expired.

For these reasons, what can be perceived as a beneficial in terms of cost and environmental
impact can in fact lead to immediate and long-term detrimental consequences for MSF
operations.

2. Instruction

It is NOT RECOMMENDED to use expired medical products of any kind

in all countries of intervention*.

*It is acknowledged that in some specific cases, a decision to overrule the above statement can be made
for operational reasons. This should be based on a risk/benefit assessment, taking into account the
medical department’s recommendations regarding product quality and clinical considerations among
others. Such a decision must be fully endorsed by the operations department because it can affect the
professional registration of the MSF staff with national authorities.
