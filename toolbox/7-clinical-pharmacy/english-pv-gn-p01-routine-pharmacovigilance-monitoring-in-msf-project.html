SOP Title:
Routine pharmacovigilance monitoring in MSF projects

Project/
study:

MSF projects excluding clinical trials and programs for which a
dedicated pharmacovigilance guideline has been developed.

1
Scope and application

This procedure is applicable to adverse events (AEs) noticed or learned by a clinician and occurring in a
patient following the administration of one or more medicinal product(s). Focus should be on serious AEs
(SAEs) related to the use of a medicinal product, i.e. for which there is at least a reasonable possibility that the
medicinal product has caused the event.

SAEs are defined as:

•
Fatal events,

•
Events that directly or immediately threatened the life of the patient,

•
Events leading to patient’s admission into hospital or requiring prolonged hospital stay (for a patient
already in hospital),

•
Events leading to significant/permanent disability or incapacity,

•
Congenital anomaly in a baby or birth defect related to the exposure of the mother/mother-to-be, or

•
Any other event judged as serious for which immediate actions/intervention were required to prevent
one of the above listed outcomes.

This procedure aims at clarifying which tools are available, describing the flow of information and the
responsibilities of each function.

This procedure is not applicable to AEs occurring in all settings for which an approved pharmacovigilance
(PV) guideline is in place and in clinical trial settings. In these situations, pharmacovigilance activities should be
performed in accordance with the PV guideline and/or the clinical trial protocol.

Additionally, in settings where a medical incident reporting system exists and encompasses reporting of AEs,
the medical incident reporting guideline apply. The current flow and tools can be used to complete the
information if needed and upon request of the Section Pharmacists.

3
Procedure

3.1
Routine PV Package

The routine PV package is a set of tools aiming at facilitating the capture and reporting of AEs by clinicians. It
is composed of:

•
An introduction document,

•
A standard Case Report Form – for anonymised AE data capture and reporting – along with guideline

for Case Report Form completion,

•
The current procedure.

The routine PV package can be completed by additional supportive documents authored by the PV unit and/or
experts, including:

•
Summary information sheet(s) on identified or potential risks of drugs; applicable for medicines

identified by the SP and/or the Medical Referents as requiring specific follow-up and/or carrying
important or unknown risks, e.g., new drugs or novel combinations.

•
Other important documents pertaining to the safety of patients (e.g., summary of drug-drug

interactions).

The PVO is responsible for maintaining the PV Package up-to-date including the summary information sheets
and the other related documents. The IPC is responsible to plan and organize the distribution of these
documents along with the SP.

3.2
Training on the routine PV Package

The PVO is responsible to prepare and update training material on routine PV as well as provide support upon
request (e.g., webinar, e-learning, participation in training weeks). The IPC and the SP are responsible to
organize training.

A simplified training scheme is recommended as follows:

It is up to the SP to decide in the light of the cultural context or other constraints who is the best
person/function to train the local personnel and the appropriate format of training (e.g., e-learning, webinar)
accounting for the resources and the personnel turnover.

Each trainer receives the same training material and should ensure proper translation into local languages if
appropriate. Group training sessions via tele- or videoconference are acceptable. E-learning will be available in
the future for the same.

The PVO collects training certificates and archives them in the MSF electronic document repository system
(MSF International Pharmacy Network SharePoint). It is the responsibility of each trainer to transmit the
training certificates to the PV unit. Ideally, new personnel should be trained before starting on a mission, but it
is up to each trainer to decide the best time for the training. The IPC can be consulted any time regarding
training organization.

3.3
Routine pharmacovigilance process

3.3.1
Foreword

This section aims at describing the general flow of pharmacovigilance information. Each project/mission is
different, and the PV focal point in the project/mission is free to liaise with their SP and agree on adding more
steps to this process, more functions in cc- of the communications, etc.

It is up to each project/mission to attribute the role of PV focal point to the appropriate function (e.g.,
Copharmacist, Mission Pharmacist) in consultation with their SP.

An approach of care as proactive as possible applies in the limits of each project/mission:

•
Clinicians are expected to actively prevent adverse events and other drug-related harms by taking a

baseline medical history with the patient or their relatives (e.g., of previous drug allergy, pregnancy,
comorbidities), reviewing their risk factors and current treatments to ensure the prescriptions are
compatible, and/or performing clinical and laboratory assessments as needed and as possible.

•
Clinicians are expected to look for adverse events by informing patients to report/consult upon

appearance of potential reactions and to perform follow-up clinical and laboratory assessments as
needed and as possible.

•
Upon detection of drug-induced adverse events, clinical management is the absolute priority

and should never be delayed. Management includes all the required interventions to stabilise and
improve the patient’s health (e.g., stop the offending agent, provide alternative therapy).

o
Would there be an urgent need for input from the SP, a medical referent or the PV unit

regarding management: the clinician, the Medco and the PV focal point shall send urgently
their request via email or call.

In settings where a medical incident reporting system exists and encompasses reporting of AEs, the PV focal
person can inform their SP that the AEs were reported in the system and discuss their questions and the need
for further follow-up through the process described in the current SOP. In principle, duplicate reporting should
be avoided as much as possible, and in case of doubt regarding the best reporting practices the SP shall be
consulted.

3.3.2
Data collection by the local clinician

If a medical occurrence matches the definition of a serious AE and is judged by the clinician as having a
reasonable causal relationship with one or more drugs, the clinician is expected to complete a Report Form.

Support from the local PV focal point (e.g. Copharmacist, Mission Pharmacist) or the PV unit can be requested
at any time. The SP should be copied in email communications.

Once the Report Form is complete, the clinician should send it to the local PV focal point and inform the
MedCo.

3.3.3
Transmission by the local PV focal point

The local PV focal point (e.g. Copharmacist, mission pharmacist) should verify the completeness of the report
before transmitting it further. Minimally, each report must include:

1.
Name or any identifier of a reporter, i.e. the person who initially collected the information (e.g. a
function such as ‘nurse’ or ‘physician’ is acceptable),

2.
Any anonymized identifier of the patient (e.g. patient number, initials, date of birth, gender),

3.
A description of at least one serious adverse event deemed as having at least a reasonable causal
relationship to a drug,

4.
At least one drug suspected to be involved in the occurrence of the adverse event.

Support from the SP or the PV unit can be requested at any time. The SP should be copied in email
communications to the PV unit.

Complete reports are sent by the PV focal point to the SP (copy to the PV unit and the MedCo).

In case of incomplete reports, the SP should be consulted and generally the local PV focal point is expected to
contact the local clinician to get complete information. The MedCo should be informed and involved as
needed.

3.3.4
Reporting by the Section Pharmacist

The Section Pharmacist is responsible to verify the origin of the culprit drug product(s) with which the AE was
observed.

For culprit drug products supplied internationally by the MSF ESCs, the SP should transmit the Report Form
to the ESC Pharmacist for reporting to manufacturers. The ESC Pharmacist is responsible for transmission but
also for maintaining an up-to-date list of manufacturers’ contacts as per their contracts/agreements with
manufacturers. The PV unit should be copied in communication with MSF ESCs.

For culprit drug products supplied locally (via local purchase, donation, loan, etc.), the SP or delegate (e.g.,
the local PV focal point, the MedCo, the intersection pharmacists) should transmit the Report Form to the
manufacturer if identifiable and to the National Regulatory Authority or Ministry of Health PV Department, if
existing in the country and as discussed and agreed with Operations and according to the setting (e.g.,
National hospital may manage internally such reporting). Translation should be performed locally as needed.

The PV unit should receive all reports and be copied on external communications or receive a proof of
reporting for tracking purpose from the SP or the ESC Pharmacist.

Support from the PV unit can be requested at any time. The PV unit should never be involved in direct
communication/discussion with the concerned National Regulatory Authority or Ministry of Health without the
prior approval of the SP and the Operations.

If no recipient for important information can be found, the PVO is responsible to transmit the information
directly to WHO PV Department keeping track of proper Report Form receipt, in agreement with the SP (who
should have obtained the green light from their Operations).

Check list of SP sequential actions upon case receipt:

1
Report form receipt acknowledgement.

2
Transmission to Medical Referent (as applicable) and PV unit (as needed) for feedbacks, consideration
on follow-up of the patient’s case and/or on potential risk minimization action(s) as appropriate.

3
Verification of the source of the culprit drug product(s).

4
Information to Operations and strategic discussion on reporting to Ministries of Health or Regulatory
Authorities, if applicable.

5

Reporting to ESC Pharmacist (ESC supplied product) or to pharmacovigilance contact at Ministries of
Health or Regulatory Authorities & manufacturers (locally purchased product) – as agreed with
Operations, or consideration for reporting to WHO via PV unit – as agreed with Operations. In any case,
PV unit copied for tracking purpose.

6
Initiate investigations if appropriate (e.g. in case of AE associated with a quality complaint).

7
Coordinate feedbacks to missions/projects, follow-up, and communication on risk minimisation actions.

3.3.5
Assessment and feedback by the Section Pharmacist

It is up to the SP, Medical Referent, and Operations with the support of the PV unit to investigate and analyse
the causes of the reported adverse events and to:

-
Provide feedbacks (e.g., on management, on results of quality investigations, on other causes

identified or to be investigated) to the PV focal point and MedCo for transmission to the local
clinicians,

-
Follow-up case reports with the support of the PV focal point (e.g., follow-up on pending tests,

evolution),

-
Decide and inform on risk minimization actions if appropriate.

If a safety issue is identified as impacting more than one operational centre it is up to the respective SPs and to
the IPC to coordinate follow-up and/or risk minimization actions across operational centres. The PV unit
provides technical support to intersectional efforts.

When a new case modifies the risk-benefit balance of a medicinal product, the SP alerts the IPC and the
concerned functions in their medical department to discuss the risk and risk minimisation actions – as
appropriate (e.g., changing the treatment protocols, stopping the purchase of a source). This in accordance
with the applicable processes in each section and intersection.

3.3.6
Tracking by the PV unit

Upon receipt of a Report Form, the PVO confirms receipt and tracks cases in a database along with
assessments and actions taken or planned (excel database reference PV-GN-DB01).

The PVO is responsible to archive in MSF electronic document repository system (MSF International
Pharmacy Network SharePoint), an electronic copy of the Report Form along with a proof of reporting to
manufacturers and/or National PV Agencies and/or WHO (e.g., justificatory emails, formal receipt
acknowledgements).

The IPC and the SPs can access the database and the documents without restriction, directly on the
document repository system. Each SP is responsible to share the information with Medical Referents and/or
Operations, if/when they judge it is appropriate.

3.3.7
Timelines

Each function is expected to achieve its tasks at the earliest opportunity. To achieve risk minimization actions
that appropriately reach patient(s)/policy maker(s) on time, information on drug-related serious adverse events
should be escalated shortly after first detection.

3.3.8
Back-up and coverage

Each function is responsible to have a back-up as planned per each section and/or department policy.

Definitions

An Adverse Event (AE) is any untoward medical occurrence in a patient administered a pharmaceutical
product and that does not necessarily have a causal relationship with this treatment. An AE can therefore be
any unfavorable and unintended sign (including an abnormal laboratory finding), symptom, or disease
temporally associated with the use of a medicinal product, whether or not related to this medicinal product.

An Adverse Drug Reaction is a response to a medicinal product, which is noxious and unintended and that
may arise from use of the product within or outside the terms of the marketing authorisation or from
occupational exposure (e.g. nurse pricking herself with the needle while preparing the injection). Conditions of
use outside the marketing authorisation include off-label use, overdose, misuse, abuse and medication errors.
In case of an adverse drug reaction, a causal relationship between a medicinal product and an adverse event
is at least a reasonable possibility.

The term clinician is used throughout the document and refers to the responsible health care practitioner at
the country or project level (e.g. physician, nurse).

Risk-benefit balance is the evaluation of the positive therapeutic effects of the medicinal product in relation to
the risks, i.e. any risk relating to the quality, safety or efficacy of the medicinal product as regards patients’
health or public health.

Risk minimization action: An intervention intended to prevent or reduce the probability of the occurrence of
an adverse drug reaction associated with the exposure to a medicine, or to reduce its severity should it occur.

Serious Adverse Event (SAE): Any untoward medical occurrence that at any dose:

•
results in death,

•
is life-threatening (defined as an event in which the subject was at risk of death at the time of the
event; it does not refer to an event which hypothetically might have caused death if it were more
severe),

•
requires inpatient hospitalization or causes prolongation of existing hospitalization,

•
results in persistent or significant disability/incapacity,

•
is a congenital anomaly/birth defect,

•
is an important medical event (defined as a medical event(s) that may not be immediately life-
threatening or result in death or hospitalization but, based upon appropriate medical and scientific
judgment, may jeopardize the subject or may require intervention [e.g. medical, surgical] to prevent
one of the other serious outcomes listed in the definition above. Examples of such events include, but
are not limited to, intensive treatment in an emergency room or at home for allergic bronchospasm;
blood dyscrasias or convulsions that do not result in hospitalization.)
