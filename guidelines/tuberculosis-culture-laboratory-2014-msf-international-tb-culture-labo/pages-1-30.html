Intoduction

Microscopy can provide fast preliminary diagnosis of tuberculosis, but only genotypic testing
or isolation of Mycobacterium tuberculosis in culture provides a definitive diagnosis for
tuberculosis.

Compared to microscopy, culture can increase case detection by up to 20-40%1 of
confirmed cases compared to the number of TB patients identified by microscopy, and its
specificity for diagnosis of TB is greater than 99%. This technique is also more sensitive than
microscopy for diagnosis of extra-pulmonary tuberculosis. The disadvantage of culturing
mycobacteria is usually the long time necessary to recover mycobacteria, as most of them,
including M. tuberculosis complex, proliferate extremely slowly, with a replication time of
18-24 hours compared to the few minutes for many other bacteria.

In December 2010, the World Health Organisation (WHO) endorsed the use of GeneXpert
(Cepheid), an automated nucleic acid amplification techniques (NAAT) that detects presence
of M.tuberculosis complex in pulmonary and extra-pulmonary samples the resistance to
rifampicin. This new technology has demonstrated to increase case detection compared to
microscopy, however genotypic testing does not eliminate the need for conventional
culture, which are indispensible both to perform phenotypic susceptibility testing for other
drugs and to monitor patients during treatment2.

Solid media, either egg-based (e.g. Löwenstein Jensen) or agar-based (e.g. Middlebrook
7H10 and 7H11) require on average at least 3-4 weeks to allow detection of growth on
primary isolation. Drug susceptibility testing (DST) on solid media takes 4 weeks. On liquid
media, growth is detectable on average after 1-2 weeks, and DST results on liquid media on
average are available after 1 week.

Use of liquid media has been recommended by WHO in 2007 as the reference standard for
culture and DST for both first line drugs (FLD) and second line drugs (SLD), recognizing its
high sensitivity and specificity and significant shortening of time to positive results3.

The purpose of this guideline is to describe the procedures for the performance of culture,
FLD and SLD with MGIT followed by rapid identification on isolates with chromatographic
lateral flow assays (rapid tests) and the p-Nitrobenzoic acid (PNB) biochemical test.

The purpose of this guideline is not to replace the manufacturer’s manual4, but to give an
overview of these tests, to help to interpret them not independently but in the framework
of a laboratory routine diagnostic algorithm and to provide reference for other documents
to which the laboratory staff should refer.

This guideline is comprised of five sections that will cover procedures for the above
mentioned tests, sample flow in the laboratory, and detailed description of principles for
each test, including quality control. In the last section the guideline will provide a list of the
check lists to be used in the laboratory.

The manual does not contain procedures for microscopy investigation. For this procedure
please refer to international guideline5 and to Médecins sans Frontières-Holland.
Tuberculosis Microscopy Manual. Version 4 [2012].

Safety procedures are described in the guideline “Safety culture guideline for tuberculosis
laboratory” Institute of Tropical Medicine 2007, and WHO guideline6.

The present guideline does not contain templates, register forms and recording sheets, or
Monitoring and Evaluation tools for culture activity.

1. Laboratory culture activity

This section will describe an algorithm proposed for culture laboratories that implement the
tests proposed, and the principle for sample collection and sample shipment to higher level
laboratories for further testing.

Procedures are described in details in the standard operational procedures, which are to be
followed carefully, to provide correct test results and to ensure safe performance of the
techniques. However, as manipulation of mycobacteria may accidentally create spillage of
infectious material, the laboratory should always be provided with a clear planning for
emergency measures in case of accidents in the laboratory. For emergency intervention in
case of spillage, see “Check list for disinfection in case of accidental spills” page 36.

1.1 Culture laboratory algorithm

The figure 1.1 describes the algorithm proposed for testing with, MGIT and LJ for culture,
identification of M. tuberculosis complex with an chromatographic rapid test and PNB-LJ,
then 1st line and 2nd line DST with MGIT.

Figure 1.1: Laboratory diagnostic algorithm

Laboratories should examine two samples per patient, which is considered the best strategy
to provide diagnosis of tuberculosis without unnecessary increase the laboratory workload7.
Moreover two cultures increase the chance of isolating mycobacteria, mainly from smear
negative samples, and in case one culture is contaminated, the result can be obtained from
the second one, avoiding the problem of collecting another samples and delaying the result.
For this reason samples must be processed separately and never pooled together.

The two samples will be decontaminated with N-acetyl-L-cysteine (NALC)-NaOH and then
inoculated respectively on MGIT and on LJ.

Use of LJ solid medium in parallel with MGIT for the same sample is recommended during
implementation of MGIT as new technique, and if the laboratory experiences high
contamination rates, to increase the positivity rate. Inoculation of a solid medium alongside
MGIT for each sample during routine activity is the choice of the laboratory, adopted for
example to increase positivity rate and to obviate loss of results due to high contamination
rates.

However, for well implemented laboratories the usual practice is to inoculate each sample
only on one medium, algorithm also described in this guideline.

The culture on MGIT is expected to give positive results within an average 10-12 days, while
negative results can be given out only after 42 days.

Culture on LJ will start to show visible growth after 3-6 weeks. Negative results will be given
out only after 8 weeks of incubation.

Positive cultures on MGIT must be confirmed for the presence of mycobacteria and absence
of contamination with other bacteria or fungi with Ziehl-Neelsen and blood agar (BA).

Only broth positive for acid fast bacilli (AFB) on microscopy and negative on BA and
confirmed positive for M. tuberculosis complex by rapid test will be used for DST.

If the MGIT medium result is contaminated, the liquid will be decontaminated again with
NALC-Na-OH and culture growth confirmed once more. Decontamination should not be
repeated more than twice. If the MGIT tube is negative while LJ is positive, colonies from LJ
can be inoculated in MGIT to obtain a positive MGIT culture that will be used to perform
DST 1st line on MGIT (SIRE).

Once presence of mycobacteria is confirmed by microscopy, the laboratory confirms
presence of M. tuberculosis. Many other mycobacteria, defined as NTM (non-tuberculous
mycobacteria), can be isolated in cultures. NTM are commonly present in the environment
and their culture recovery can be due to contamination of the specimen, colonization or
infection of the patient. Identification of the species prior performance of drug susceptibility
testing is crucial, in order to provide correct results. Moreover NTM can be either
pathogenic or asymptomatic, distinction from M. tuberculosis and their identification is
necessary to provide correct treatment8.

Preliminary distinction between M. tuberculosis complex and NTM is provided by visual check
of LJ tubes and for MGIT by microscopy through detection on the slide of mycobacteria in thick
red cord formations, which are caused by a cord factor, typically produced by M. tuberculosis
complex. However other mycobacteria, like M. marinum and others, can appear as cords or
pseudo-cords9, for this reason this preliminary identification must be confirmed by other tests
like later flow assays3. This guideline describes use of SD-Bioline TB Ag MPT64 RAPID®, but
other tests with equivalent characteristics are available on the market.

These test can rapidly identify presence of M. tuberculosis complex but not simultaneous
presence of this mycobacterium and NTM growing in the culture. For this reason, in parallel
with 1st line DST on MGIT, the laboratory performs also the PNB test, inoculating in parallel
either a suspension of LJ positive culture or the liquid suspension from MGIT culture on one
LJ tube containing PNB and on one PNB-free tube, for comparison. Any non-tuberculous
mycobacterium present will grow on the PNB tube (for details see Section 3).

1.2 Sample collection, storage and registration

The number of samples collected by the laboratory for microscopy investigation depends on
the TB program of each country, two or 3 samples are usually collected. The collection of
specimens should be organized in a way that causes the least inconvenience to both patient
and laboratory.

Samples for cultures must be selected according to their quality (salivary samples should be
avoided) and time of collection (between samples of equal quality, the most recently-
collected should be used).

The longer it takes for a sample to reach the laboratory and be treated, the more the
bacteria present in the sputum will proliferate and compete with mycobacteria for growth
in the medium. For this reason, if possible samples should be kept in the fridge, and
processed within 4 days. Delay exceeding one week, even for storage at cool temperature,
will interfere with microscopy results and will significantly affect culture yield by increasing
contamination in the sample. If transport requires more than 4 days for 10% or more of the
samples, or transport requires air shipment, a preservative can be added10. One of the most
common is cetylpyridinium chloride (CPC), a quaternary ammonium compound. This
reagent is not compatible with the MGIT culture technique, so samples containing CPC
should be inoculated only on LJ11, and the request form must clearly state that CPC has
been added. Culturing samples with CPC are not covered by this guideline.

With the exception of samples containing CPC, specimens should be stored in cold chain
prior processing for cultures. All samples to be cultured are to be stored protected from
sunlight to avoid killing of mycobacteria.

For procedures of sample shipment, refer to WHO guideline12 and Médecins sans Frontières
catalogue.

Samples must be collected in a clean, if possible sterile container and preferably be
manipulated (e.g. for smear production) with aseptic material, for example with disposable
loops (for safety reasons reusable metal loops, which are flamed for sterilisation, should not
be allowed in the laboratory due to production of aerosols during flaming). Wooden sticks
should be avoided. Wooden tongue depressors may be used.

Extra-pulmonary samples have different collection requirements, according to the type of
specimen. The major criteria for collection are listed in table 1.1 (page 10).

Swabs are not recommended for mycobacterial isolation. All samples require refrigeration
between collection and transport, except for blood, which must be stored at room
temperature.

Blood is not suitable for culture on MGIT, and should be inoculated only on LJ.

All samples transported to the laboratory should be accompanied by a request form clearly
indicating patient identification code, date of collection and type of sample and possible
presence of preservative, and any other detail requested by the recipient laboratory.

2. Primary isolation with MGIT and LJ

This section describes culturing of samples on MGIT and LJ for primary isolation. These tests
will all be performed from sample sediment obtained after sample decontamination

2.1 Sample decontamination

The probability of recovering mycobacteria with culture depends on many factors, such as
the number of bacilli present in the sample, the time the sample takes to arrive in the
laboratory, also the proportion of still viable mycobacteria and the procedure used for
digestion and decontamination, simply referred to as “decontamination”.

This procedure is required for all samples prior inoculation on media. The purpose of
digestion is to free bacilli from mucus and cells. The purpose of decontamination is to
reduce competing organisms present in the sample. These two steps are covered by a
procedure commonly referred to as decontamination. Several methods are recommended,
between them decontamination with NaOH. Nevertheless, use of this decontaminant alone
can kill up to 60% of mycobacteria13 during the process, for this reason NaOH is usually
combined with NALC, which can decrease this proportion to 30%. The disadvantage of this
method, however, is the very short shelf life of NALC– NaOH solution, which, once
reconstituted, must be used within 24 hours.

A decontaminant often used for samples that require long transport prior inoculation is CPC
(cetylpyridinium chloride), but this preservative is only compatible with egg based medium
like LJ and cannot be applied to MGIT960 system. The Petroff method (2% NaOH final
concentration neutralized with HCl) can be used, but the higher concentration of NaOH used
for this method may affect the growth rate of cultures.

This guideline describes the decontamination with NALC-NaOH, the preferable method
applied to MGIT. The guideline does not describe procedures for use of CPC, which are
introduced in Annex 1.

Samples decontamination is a critical step for culturing, the following three steps are critical
crucial for this process:

– Concentration of the decontamination solution: the recommended concentration of
Nalc-NaOH is 1%13, which can be increased or lowered from 0.5 to 2.0% according to the
contamination rate observed in the laboratory14. While increasing or lowering the final
NaOH concentration, it should be taken into account that too harsh decontamination will
not only kill the contaminating bacteria, but also decrease significantly the number of
mycobacteria, therefore decreasing the possibility of recovering them on culture. Too
light a procedure will fail to kill competitive contamination. For both MGIT and LJ
methods, 3-5% of cultures contaminated is an acceptable value if the laboratory is
receiving ‘fresh’ samples. The percentage can increase to 5-10% if samples take a few
days to arrive at the laboratory. Absence of contamination is not an indication of a good
procedure, but a sign that the samples are most probably too strongly decontaminated.
The NALC-NaOH method is recommended when culturing on MGIT liquid medium. This
technique is also used for LJ. Cysteine is added to NaOH because of its capacity to digest
and liquefy mucus.

– Time to exposure to the decontamination solution: time should not exceed 20-25 min, to
avoid killing mycobacteria present in the sample.
– Time of centrifugation: Mycobacteria have a low specific gravity and centrifugation needs
a centrifugal force of 3000 x g for 15 minutes. If the laboratory is not equipped with a
refrigerated centrifuge, increasing of time of centrifugation should be avoided, as it will
lead to an increase in temperature which will kill the mycobacteria. If a refrigerated
centrifuge is not available, neutralize samples with refrigerated phosphate buffer that will
help to keep the temperature down during centrifugation.

2.1.1 Reagent preparation

The decontamination solution consists of sodium citrate dehydrate solution (solution A)
mixed with an equal amount of 4% NaOH solution (solution B), to reach a final concentration
of 2% NaOH sodium citrate solution.
NALC must be weighed and added to 2% NaOH just before use.
The mixed NaOH sodium citrate solution can be stored at room temperature (but preferably
in refrigerator) for a maximum of 1 month, while the NALC-NaOH working solution can be
stored for 24 hours, and then must be discarded.
See “Check list for preparation of NALC-NaOH”, page 37.

Phosphate buffer (PBS) pH 6.8 is used at the end of the decontamination procedure to
neutralize the NaOH. After preparation, phosphate buffer may be stores at room temperature
for a maximum of 1 year , but refrigeration is preferable.
See “Check list for phosphate buffer preparation”, page 38.

2.1.2 Sputum decontamination procedure

Samples can be sent to the laboratory in a standard sample container or a Falcon tube. In
the first case, before starting with the decontamination procedure, record the patient
identification number on the Falcon tube that will be used during the procedure.
Samples are decontaminated preferably with 2 or 3% Nalc-NaOH decontamination working
solution, to be added to the sample in a 1:1 proportion, so to reach a final concentration of
1-1.5% NaOH.
It is very important to respect the concentration of the decontamination solution and the
correct time for decontamination (for this protocol 20 min), to be set up from the start of
the procedure, when the decontamination solution is poured into the sample.

For correct decontamination, invert the tube to allow the decontamination solution to get in
contact with the whole surface of the tube, and continue to invert regularly the tubes during
the decontamination incubation period, to obtain homogeneous digestion and
decontamination.
Keep the pH as close as possible to neutral by checking NaOH pH. High pH can affect the
recovery rate and increase time to detection.
Do not vortex or shake samples for too long, as too much aeration inhibits the performance
of NALC.
NALC is inhibited in the presence of blood. For samples containing high amounts of blood,
use 3-4% NaOH alone (initial concentration).
The NaOH working solution concentration can vary between 2% (if used for the first
decontamination) and 4% (if decontamination of the sample must be repeated). The
concentration can vary according to the contamination frequency in the laboratory; if higher
than 5%, the concentration of the first decontamination solution can be increased up to 4%.

This value should not been exceeded, stronger decontamination of samples could kill too
many mycobacteria and the possibility to obtain growth could, therefore, be compromised.
In the present protocol we recommend the use of 4% NaOH, thus 2% for the working
solution. Because the proportion of decontamination solution and sample is 1:1, the final
concentration of the decontamination solution will be 1%.
If the MGIT culture shows contamination, the liquid can be decontaminated again with 4%
NaOH alone. In this case the final concentration will be 2%.
For details of the decontamination procedure, see “Check list for sputum decontamination
procedure”, page 39.

The percentage of acceptable contamination with liquid media should remain between
3-8%. Too low a contamination rate is often sign of overly harsh decontamination.
There are several factors that can lead to an increase of contamination:
– no mouth rinsing before sputum collection;
– long time between sample collection and processing;
– very mucous samples that do not liquefy properly during decontamination;
– laboratory procedures carried out in non-sterile conditions.
In LJ tubes, growth of contaminating bacteria or mould can be observed in the first few
days; usually if it is not visible within the first 48 hours, it is unlikely to grow later.
On MGIT, growth of contaminants can be observed after 72 hours. This is because the
growth of contaminants is slowed by the presence of antibiotics and anti-mycotics in the
media.

High contamination can be controlled by:
– reviewing the sample collection and shipment procedures;
– reviewing manipulation procedures;
– check for sterility of the reagents increasing NaOH concentration if 2% is used (final
concentration should not exceed 1.5%);
– increase of PANTA concentration during inoculation of MGIT tubes (reconstitute PANTA
with only 10 ml of OADC).

Always introduce one change at the time, and record the date and detailed protocols of the
measure taken.

2.1.3 Decontamination of other specimens

Non-sputum samples can be processed with the MGIT technique.

Pus and other mucopurulent samples not exceeding 10 ml can be digested and
decontaminated with NALC-NaOH as described for sputum, or with 2-4% NaOH.
If the sample exceeds 10 ml, process only 10 ml. If the sample is very thick, liquefy it by
adding 50-100 mg of NALC powder, mix well, then centrifuge and re-suspend with 5 ml
distilled sterile water.
Decontaminate with NaOH and proceed as for sputum.

Gastric aspirates must be processed a maximum of 4 hours after collection. The sample
must be concentrated by centrifugation then re-suspended in 5 ml of sterile distilled water,
then decontaminated with NALC-NaOH or 2-4% NaOH following the same procedure as for
sputum.

Bronchial washing is to be processed like sputum. If the sample amount exceeds 10 ml,
concentrate the sample by centrifugation, If too thick, add NALC powder (50-100 mg) then
resuspend in 5 ml sterile water and decontaminate following the same procedure for
sputum.

MGIT tube contains 7 ml of modified
Middlebrook 7H9 broth base, used for the
detection and recovery of mycobacteria
from clinical specimens and body fluids,
except urine and blood.
The principle behind this procedure is based
on the presence of a fluorescent compound
embedded in silicone at the bottom of the
MGIT tube.
Figure 2.1: MGIT tubes illuminated by UV light

This fluorescent compound is sensitive to the presence of oxygen in the broth.
Initially the high quantity of oxygen inhibits the fluorescence then, when the bacteria start
to grow, the concentration of oxygen will decrease, with a subsequent increase in
fluorescence that can be observed using a reader at 365 nm UV light (Figure 2.1). Growth is
also observed through the presence of turbidity, or small grains, in the tube.
Before inoculation, the tubes must be supplemented with BACTEC MGIT960Supplement Kit
which contains 6 x 15 ml vials of growth supplement (or OADC) containing oleic acid, bovine
albumin, dextrose, catalase, which are enrichments essential for the growth of mycobacteria
and 6 vials of PANTA, which contains polymixin B, amphotericin B, nalidixic acid, trimethoprim
and azlocillin: an antibiotic mixture that reduces contamination in the tube.

LJ is the most commonly used solid medium in culture laboratories. This medium, presents
several advantages compared to MGIT: it is cheap, it is easy to prepare and can be stored in
the fridge for one month; due to the presence of glycerol is considered a selective medium
facilitating isolation of M. tuberculosis compared to other mycobacteria, plus malachite
green reduces growth of contaminant bacteria.
However, if on one hand LJ often presents lower contamination rates than MGIT, liquid
media have demonstrated to be superior to solid for isolation of mycobacteria, for this
reason many laboratories combine these two media for culturing of diagnostic samples.
Moreover, compared to liquid media, LJ presents a significantly slower time to positivity.

2.2.1 Procedures for primary isolation with MGIT and LJ liquid media

The following procedures describe inoculation of sediment obtained from one sample in
MGIT and from the second sample in LJ.

MGIT liquid medium

MGIT tubes are prone to contamination more than other media, mainly caused by
environmental mycobacteria; for this reason particular caution must be used during their
manipulation.
Always open tubes in the biosafety cabinet in sterile conditions, and do not leave them open
between the different steps of the inoculation procedure. Do not open more than one tube
at a time.
Prior to inoculation add 0.8 ml of PANTA reconstituted with MGIT supplement to the tube,
and then inoculate 0.5 ml of well-mixed sediment (a greater amount will increase the
probability of contamination). Do not add reconstituted PANTA after inoculation. Tubes to
which PANTA has been added cannot be stored. After inoculation, invert the tubes few
times to mix the content and leave them standing at room temperature for 30 minutes
before incubation. Always incubate the tubes when tightly closed, as loose caps can affect
detection of fluorescence. Do not shake the MGIT tubes during incubation.

LJ solid medium

After inoculation and during incubation of the LJ medium, avoid inverting the tube, as
contact between the liquid contained in the slant and the cap can increase risk of
contamination.
LJ tubes after inoculation are left overnight in a slanted position, with the caps slightly
opened to allow the liquid at the bottom edge of the slant to evaporate. From the following
day, the tubes are incubated in a standing position, with the cap tightly closed.
However, if the medium does not contain too much liquid, it is preferable to close tightly
the cap immediately after inoculation, to decrease risk of contamination.
See “Check list for inoculation on MGIT and on LJ” page 41.

Storage conditions

MGIT tubes must be stored at 2-25°C. They should never be frozen or exposed to higher
temperatures. They should not be exposed to light and must be used only after their
appearance is checked (i.e. tubes must be clear and colourless.) Always check the expiry
date.
MGIT Growth Supplement must be stored in the dark at 2-8°C. It should never be frozen or
overheated. It should only be opened just before use.
MGIT PANTA lyophilized vials must be stored at 2-8°C. Once reconstituted, the PANTA
stored at 2-8°C may be used within 72 h, or within 6 months if stored at -20°C. Once
thawed, the PANTA mixture must be used immediately and unused mixture discarded.
LJ tubes can be stored in the fridge for on average one month.

according to different factors, such as the mycobacterial load present in the specimen, the

can kill a considerable number of mycobacteria.

2.4 Quality control for culture

Quality control for culture includes control of all material and equipment used during the
procedure, control of the media preparation, and quality control for the decontamination
step, which is all part of the evaluation of the laboratory’s performance.

Each new reagent or batch of media has to be recorded on a QC sheet, which reports the
batch number assigned, date of preparation and number of tubes or flasks prepared. Results
from QC should be regularly reported in this sheet.

2.4.1 Internal Quality Control for equipment and reagents

The main parameters to be checked are the temperature and fluorescence detector.
Temperature is monitored daily by manual reading of the temperature control tubes of each
drawer, and by controlling the automatic temperature reading.
The control lights should be checked weekly.
Calibration of the fluorescence detector is done automatically. If an error occurs, replace the
calibration tubes (Becton Dickinson).

Quality control of equipment and media/reagents

– temperature and sterility of the incubator
– temperature of the fridge during storage
– regular calibration of the MGIT960 machine
– expiry date of all reagents
– correct storage of the reagents (temperature and humidity)
– sterility of the reagents (if prepared on-site)
– each new batch of medium prepared on site must checked for sterility
– each new batch of MGIT tubes must be tested with a reference strain to check it supports
correctly growth.
– a laboratory facing contamination problems should check for reagent sterility by
inoculating one drop of the new batch prepared on a blood agar plate.

Quality Control of MGIT tubes

Each new batch of MGIT-broth tubes should be tested with a reference strain. If correct
storage and cold chain are not assured during shipment of the new tubes, QC could also be
performed for all shipments.
Two tubes must be used: one to be incubated without inoculum and any supplement, to
check for sterility (negative control), and one inoculated with a known strain, following
standard procedures (positive control).
The strain recommended is M. fortuitum. In the absence of M. fortuitum, M. tuberculosis
H37Rv can be used, the disadvantage being the delay of the QC result, as M. fortuitum
grows more quickly than M. tuberculosis. QC is performed using fresh colonies (3-4 weeks
old) grown on solid medium, by inoculating 0.5 ml of a 1 mg/ml suspension of colonies into
the MGIT-broth, according to the procedure explained in “Check list for preparation of
1 mg/ml bacterial suspension” page 47. See also Annex 2 “Inoculation of reference strains
for MGIT liquid medium QC according to BD procedures”. However, due to its complexity,
the procedure from Becton Dickinson is not recommended in this manual.
With M. fortuitum, a positive result should be obtained after between 1 to 3 days, while for
H37Rv, this should take about 1 week.
The batch control results and release of the batch are registered in the MGIT culture QC
sheet.
In case an incorrect result is obtained, the test should be repeated. In case of a second
incorrect result, discard the batch of tubes and contact Becton Dickinson.

2.4.2 Quality control for culture procedures

Evaluation of laboratory performance relies on regular monitoring of indicators established
in the laboratory. Indicators should always be accompanied by a regular laboratory report
which includes statistics and describes the indicators and any possible event in the
laboratory that may have influenced the indicators values.
Any relevant information which can contribute to the interpretation of the results, and
explanation of the indicators (for example reasons that can explain high contamination, or
changes in procedures that could justify variation in positivity rate) should be described in
the monthly report, which is then integrated in for quality control process.

Description of the indicators

The following indicators are internationally recommended for evaluation of culture
laboratory activity15. Indicators are calculated retrospectively, using as denominator the
total amount of culture inoculated in a month and as numerator the results obtained, so
that for e.g. in March the laboratory would calculate indicators referred to cultures
inoculated in January, for which results are already available.

1. Percentage of positive cultures reported as M. tuberculosis
Percentage of positive cultures reported as M. tuberculosis in 1 month/total number of
cultures reported as positive, negative, NTM or contaminated.
This indicator helps to monitor possible false positive and false negative results. Even if
recovery rate is linked to different factors related to the population, or seasonal changes, it
should not show considerable changes. If on one hand variations in percentage of positivity
rate always occur in routine testing, big variations of positivity rates between months,
exceeding 2SD, could indicate for example cross-contamination, while 2SD below this value
could be related for example to problems of procedures or reagent quality.

2. Percentage of cultures reported as contaminated
Percentage of cultures reported as contaminated in 1 month/ total number of cultures

Monitoring contamination rate is essential in laboratory evaluation, as this value
indicates good performance of the laboratory.
Contamination should not exceed 5% for both MGIT and LJ techniques, as too high or too

Number of smear positive samples reported as culture positive in 1 month/number of
smear positive samples inoculated in 1 month
Even if it is reasonable to expect that all smear positive samples result in positive culture,
many factors can have an impact on this indicator. Follow up patients for example can
submit samples that contain dead bacilli, which are still detected by microscopy, but that
will not be recovered by culture. Another factor could the load of bacilli in the specimen,
and procedures for sample shipment to the TB culture laboratory: scanty positive
samples sent with preservatives like CPC can result in negative cultures.

4. Correlation between smear negative samples and positive cultures
Number of smear negative samples reported as culture positive in 1 month/number of
smear negative samples inoculated in 1 month
As the culture technique is more sensitive than microscopy, it is to be expected that
culture detects positive patients, who were negative on smear examination; recovery of
liquid media could be equal or even higher than when using solid media. In general
around 30% of smear negative samples should give culture positive results. Values much
higher or lower can indicate problems in reporting smear results or in the identification
of suspected cases eligible for culture investigation.

5. Percentage of positive cultures reported as NTM
Number of cultures reported as NTM in 1 month/total number of cultures reported as
positive, negative, NTM or contaminated
The proportion of NTM recovered depends on the population and the type of media used
by the laboratory, for example liquid media can increase recovering of NTM. The
laboratory can consider reporting only clinically significant mycobacteria, but increased
recovery of environmental mycobacteria can indicate contamination of reagents and
cultures.

6. Turnaround time
The average number of days required for each technique to give positive results
This indicator must be established by each laboratory according to the technique used.
Turnaround time is to be calculated from sample collection to giving out of the result. If
the turnaround observed is much longer or shorter, all steps of the laboratory activity
should be checked.

3. Identification of Mycobacteria

This section describes the criteria to identify mycobacteria from positive cultures, by
morphological observation, the p-Nitrobenzoic acid (PNB) biochemical test, and lateral flow
chromatographic assay. Other tests as niacin or nitrate test, which are positive for
M. tuberculosis, are considered obsolete, and not recommended in this guideline.

3.1 Morphological identification

Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex (MTC) contains different species: M. tuberculosis,
M. bovis, M. africanum, M. microti and the strain for vaccine Calmette-Guérin, (BCG)16.
M. tuberculosis is the first cause of disease, while M. bovis is more common in cattle.
M. africanum is common in Africa with pathogenicity between Mycobacterium tuberculosis
and M. bovis, while M. microti is a rare pathogen of individuals immunocompromised17.
Newly accepted species are M. caprae, M. pinnipedii, and M. canetti.

From the clinical point of view it is very important to identify Mycobacterium tuberculosis
complex from non-tuberculous mycobacteria. Distinction between species within the
complex is not essential.

Each culture should be sub-cultured in a new LJ tube and observed for the following
characteristics, typical of M. tuberculosis (figure 3.1)
– slow growth (more than 7 days)
– growth at temperature only between 35- 37°C
– rough, dry, friable morphology
– absence of pigment
– no growth on LJ with p- Nitrobenzoic acid

3.2 Identification tests

3.2.1 PNB test

This test is used to identify M. tuberculosis, as Mycobacterium tuberculosis complex (MTC) is
inhibited by ρ-Nitrobenzoic acid (PNB), whereas non-tuberculous mycobacteria (NTM) are
resistant.

PNB is stored in solution (at 2-8°C for up to one year) and added to LJ during medium
preparation.

The medium is inoculated using the same procedures of growth comparison followed for
1st line on LJ (see Section 4).

As M. tuberculosis should not grow on PNB, LJ+PNB should appear negative, while the
control tube should show growth.

The reading of the test is performed following the proportion method, so the comparison
between LJ PNB-containing medium and the control is based on the assumption that on the
PNB-containing medium we should expect no growth, or colonies should not be more than
1% of the number of colonies that appear on the control.

This method can be performed either starting from solid or liquid positive cultures. In the
first case the test performed using two dilutions 10-2 and 10-4, following the proportion
method.

If the test is performed from a positive MGIT liquid medium, the suspension, is directly
inoculated on a PNB-LJ and on a standard LJ tube. In this case the proportion method cannot
be applied, therefor the test is interpreted by direct by comparison of the growth.

Tubes for both procedures are to be read at 7, 14 and 29 days.

See “Check list for preparation of LJ-PNB tubes (40 tubes)” page 48, “Check list for
inoculation on LJ + PNB from MGIT” page 48, “Check list for inoculation on LJ + PNB from LJ”
page 49 and “Check list for reading a PNB test” page 50.

3.2.2 Lateral-flow chromatographic rapid test

3.2.2.1 Test performance

This consists of a one step, rapid, immunochromatoghraphic test for the detection of MPT64
antigen, which is specific for M. tuberculosis complex.

This test can be used for identification of M. tuberculosis complex on positive, contaminant-
free MGIT/LJ cultures.

Results are available in 15 min instead of the 21 days required for PNB, with the advantage
of not delaying SIRE inoculation and not performing DSTs unnecessarily.

4. MGIT for drug susceptibility testing

4.1 Principle of MGIT drug susceptibility testing

Drug susceptibility testing is necessary to guide the physicians in choosing the most
appropriate line of drugs to be prescribed for the patient.
Susceptibility and resistance of mycobacteria to anti-tuberculosis drugs can be tested using
different methods: the proportion method, absolute concentration method, resistance ratio
method.
The most commonly-used is the proportion method, which uses solid media. The test is
based on the empirical proposition that, if in a population of mycobacteria, more than 1% of
the mycobacteria are resistant to a certain drug, the population are to be considered
resistant. This percentage, in fact, will quickly grow because of a selective pressure exerted
by the drug that will kill the susceptible mycobacteria but will allow the resistant ones to
multiply.
This protocol describes the MGIT SIRE (streptomycin, isoniazid, rifampicin, ethambutol)
system, and MGIT PZA (pyrazinamide) system.
The SIRE system provides qualitative results 3 to 14 days after incubation.
The MGIT tubes used for SIRE are the same as for primary culture; they contain 7 ml
modified Middlebrook 7H9 broth and a fluorescent compound quenched by the presence of
oxygen in the broth. The appearance of fluorescence reveals the growth of bacteria as the
oxygen in the media is consumed. When two tubes are inoculated, one drug-containing tube
and one drug-free tube (control tube), the absence of fluorescence in a drug-containing
tube two days after the appearance of fluorescence in the control tube is indicative of
susceptibility of the mycobacteria to that drug. Fluorescence of the drug-containing tube on
or within two days of the fluorescence of the control tube indicates resistance.
The MGIT SIRE system is routinely performed using cultures obtained from MGIT tubes.
Susceptibility testing can be performed by using a direct or indirect procedure:
– In the direct test, a decontaminated and concentrated specimen is inoculated directly into
drug-containing media and drug-free media (control).
– In the indirect test, pure growth from primary isolation medium is tested.

Generally, results of the direct test are available 3 or 4 weeks earlier than the results of the
indirect test, but the indirect test is more reliable since the inoculum size is more uniform
and the bacteria are metabolically active. The indirect test is currently recommended.
The following protocols describe the method for testing resistance to 1st line drugs:
streptomycin, isoniazid, rifampin and ethambutol.
The MGIT PZA assay is performed with specific MGIT tubes, containing a medium at a
reduced pH of 6, the level at which pyrazinamide is active against M. tuberculosis (or lower).
Most M. tuberculosis strains do not adapt to grow at this pH level, which is why testing for
resistance to this drug is particularly challenging.

The second line drug susceptibility testing (SLD) on the BACTEC-MGIT960 system is a rapid
qualitative procedure for testing of Mycobacterium tuberculosis for resistance to ofloxacin
(OFLO), kanamycin (KAN), capreomycin (CAP), ethionamide (ETH) & para-aminosalicylic acid
(PAS).
The test is performed from MGIT liquid cultures, or from the growth control of the SIRE if
available, or alternatively from subcultures prepared from growth on solid media. MGIT
tubes beyond 5 days after positivity should be subcultured to avoid over-inoculation.
