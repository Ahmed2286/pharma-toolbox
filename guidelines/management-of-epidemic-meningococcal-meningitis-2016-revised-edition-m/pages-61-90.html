62

Skin necrosis

– May be a complication of purpura.
– Treat as for burns with sterile dressings.

Septic shock

Meningococcal sepsis is less frequent in the meningitis belt.

– Management in the Intensive Care unit.
– Oxygen
– IV fluids and intravascular resuscitation with gelatine, Ringer’s lactate or 0.9%
NaCl (see Clinical Guidelines).

Isolation

Patients present a small proportion of the reservoir and most transmission occurs through
asymptomatic carriers. Isolation of patients is not necessary.

Prophylaxis and vaccination of contacts

Antibiotic prophylaxis for close contacts is recommended by the WHO during non-
epidemic situations, but not during a declared epidemic of meningococcal meningitis. The
current body of evidence does not allow conclusions to be drawn about the potential
effectiveness of prophylaxis during an epidemic, particularly due to the role of
asymptomatic carriers in transmission. Further research is required. It may be useful to
use prophylaxis in closed structures such as prisons or schools.

Single dose oral ciprofloxacin is the best choice for antibiotic prophylaxis, including in
children. For pregnant women, the treatment of choice is ceftriaxone 250 mg IM as a
single dose. Rifampicin is not recommended due to the risk of provoking mycobacterial
tuberculosis resistance.

Vaccination of contacts is not recommended outside of mass vaccination campaigns.

Frequency of supply

First supply

When the alert threshold is reached, kits should be delivered by the quickest means
possible. Supply all the health facilities in the affected region. Supply by order of priority:

– Hospitals.
– Temporary meningitis hospitalisation units.
– Health centres with the largest number of cases, with priority to those with the
highest CFR.
– All facilities which have declared cases.
– Centres yet to declare a case.

First provide the minimum stocks.
For hospitals and temporary hospitalisation units:

– Kits of 5 treatments uncomplicated meningitis
– Kits of 1 treatment meningitis 7 days for children under 2 months
– Kits of 10 treatments complicated meningitis
For peripheral health centres:

– At least 1 kit 5 treatments uncomplicated meningitis
– 1 kit 1 treatment meningitis 7 days for children under 2 months, if staff are
competent to care for children.

Subsequent supply

Set up a system to track supply and estimate needs.

– For each health facility, estimate needs according to the number of cases
treated each week and the number of cases expected from the course of the
epidemiological curve in the district.
– Use an expected admission rate of 15-20% in order to calculate initial needs in
hospitals and temporary hospitalisation units.
– The frequency of supply will depend on the distances, access difficulties, and
means of transport available (see Appendix 15).

At the end of the outbreak

– Remaining medication and equipment from peripheral health centres may be
returned to district level, then to regional level.

Key points

■ A strategy of decentralised care allows rapid treatment to be offered to patients,
while avoiding the overloading of health facilities during a significant outbreak of
meningitis.

■ Standard treatment of epidemic meningitis is 5 days ceftriaxone for adults and
children over 2 months and 7 days ceftriaxone for children under 2 months.

■ Since 2014, oily chloramphenicol is no longer advised and the single dose
ceftriaxone protocol should be reserved for specific situations.

■ Negotiate in advance the possibility of using the single dose ceftriaxone protocol
depending on the development of the epidemic and of setting up treatment for the
5 day protocol on an outpatient basis.

■ Prepare treatment kits in order to simplify supply to health facilities.

■ Treatment in all health facilities should be free of charge and any stock rupture
must be avoided.

■ In order to estimate treatment needs, it is necessary to calculate the number of
estimated cases, antibiotic needs and bed capacity needs.

■ Isolation of patients is not necessary.

■ Antibiotic prophylaxis of close contacts is not recommended.

Characteristics of meningococcal
vaccines

Available vaccines

Several vaccines targeting the capsule of the meningococcus are available, either
monovalent, active against a single serogroup, or polyvalent, active against two or more
serogroups.

Polysaccharide vaccines

Polysaccharide vaccines are cheap and provoke a strong immune response in adults and
children above 2 years of age. These vaccines are generally used in reactive vaccination
campaigns during epidemics. Their disadvantages are low efficacy among children under
2 and a short length of immunity, generally 2 to 3 years, which makes them unsuitable for
preventative vaccination. The lack of long lasting immunity leaves the community
vulnerable to the next epidemic cycle and means that repeated mass vaccination
campaigns are needed in the meningitis belt. Polysaccharide vaccines have no effect on
meningococcal carriage.

Conjugate vaccines

Conjugate vaccines are synthesised from a polysaccharide antigen attached to a carrier
protein, such as diphtheria or tetanus toxin, which increases the immune response.
Conjugate vaccines provoke a longer lasting immunity, at least 10 years, which confers
herd immunity for the community and allows their use in preventative vaccination
programmes. These vaccines are effective in children under 2 years and thus protect
across age groups. Contrary to polysaccharide vaccines, conjugate vaccines reduce nasal
carriage of meningococci and have a role in reducing the transmission of meningitis.
However, their major disadvantage is their cost, typically $15-20, which may be as high as
$80-100, compared to $0.5-5 for polysaccharide vaccines. A mass vaccination campaign
with a conjugate vaccine is therefore currently not feasible. The only affordable conjugate
vaccine is MenAfriVacTM, which covers only serogroup A.

against NmX exists. A low cost pentavalent conjugate vaccine against Nm A,C,W135,X,Y
which is heat stable for use under CTC conditions is in development but will not be ready

Age and response to vaccination

The immune response to a vaccine depends on the age of the person vaccinated and the
type of vaccine used.

In adults, polysaccharide vaccines produce a humoral immunity (antibody-mediated)
involving B lymphocytes. Antibodies appear 5-8 days after a single injection of vaccine.
Protection lasts for 3-5 years in adults and the vaccine efficiency (the proportion of
individuals protected among those vaccinated) is between 85-90%. This protection falls
over the course of time. Studies have shown that after 3 years only 65% of the population
vaccinated is still protected.

In children under 3 years, the bone marrow is still immature and has not reached full
production capacity for B lymphocytes. Polysaccharide vaccines therefore produce limited
immunity before the age of 2 and weak immunity before the age of 4-5 years. In children
this immunity is lost more quickly than in adults. In practice, polysaccharide meningitis
vaccines should not be given before the age of 2. Furthermore, the immune response is
specific to each serogroup. Vaccines against NmA produce a stronger immune reaction
than those against NmC and W.

73

The phenomenon of immune hyporeactivity has been observed for polysaccharide
vaccines against NmC and W. An individual who has received a second dose of vaccine
produces a lower antibody titre than an individual who has received only a single dose.
This reduction in the immune response following repeated vaccination could be linked to
depletion of memory B lymphocytes and has been seen in all age groups The practical or
clinical significance is not known.

Conjugate vaccines evoke a cellular immune reaction involving T lymphocytes, which
confers a stronger and longer lasting immunity than the humoral immunity evoked by
polysaccharide vaccines. Cellular immunity is active in children under 2 years, which
permits the use of conjugate vaccines in this age group.

Dose and route of administration

Check the product description for the dose.

– Children under 2 years: in the anterolateral thigh
– Children over 2 years and adults: in the deltoid muscle (outer upper-arm)

Contraindications

There is no absolute contraindication to meningococcal vaccination except
hypersensitivity to a component of the vaccine. Vaccination should be postponed in
patients suffering from acute severe infection, but a minor infection is not regarded as
contraindication. There is no risk in vaccinating an individual in the incubation phase of
meningitis.

Adverse events following immunisation (AEFI)

Adverse effects are infrequent (< 5%) and generally mild:

– Erythema at the injection site, lasting 48 hours < 1%).
– Fever of 38°5 C for 24 to 48 hours (0.4 to 2%).

Simultaneous vaccination

A mass vaccination campaign against meningitis is rarely combined
with other vaccination, due to the emergency nature of reactive
vaccination and the added organisational complexity.

In theory, vaccination against meningitis is possible at the same time as
other vaccines, such as yellow fever and measles. Two different
vaccines should never be mixed in the same syringe and the two
injections must be given in two different sites. Always read the
manufacturer’s instructions before considering giving two simultaneous
vaccinations.

From 2016, MenAfriVacTMis due to be incorporated into national immunisation
programmes. Studies to date have not detected any immunological incompatibility with
standard EPI vaccines.

Vaccine storage and stability

Lyophilised vaccines

Store between +2°C and + 8°C. Freezing at –20°C is not recommended.

Solvents

Solvent are kept at room temperature, but should be put in the refrigerator 12 hours
before a vaccination session to bring them to the same temperature as the dried
powder at the time of reconstitution. Do not freeze.

Always use the solvent supplied with the vaccine by the manufacturer. Solvent
composition and volume may vary. Always read the manufacturer’s instructions before
reconstituting. In case of loss of the solvent, contact the MSF pharmacist who will contact
the manufacturer for instructions.

Reconstituted vaccines

Store between + 2 and + 8°C. An open vial should not be kept for more than 6 hours and
any remaining vials must be destroyed at the end of the immunisation session.

Vaccination strategies

Meningitis Vaccine Campaign: MenAfriVacTM

The initial MVP campaigns vaccinated a broad section of the population, with a target age
of between 1 and 30 years. 5 years after the initial campaign, each country should
undertake catch-up campaigns for children born since the date of the first campaign.
From 2016, MenAfriVacTMwill be incorporated into the NPI, with a single injection of 5 μg
for children between 9 and 18 months of age. MenAfriVacTMis the only vaccine to be used
for preventative vaccination against meningitis in the meningitis belt, given that
polysaccharide vaccines produce too short an immune response and that other conjugate
vaccines are too expensive and not available in sufficient quantities.

Mass vaccination campaign

In order to be effective, a mass vaccination campaign undertaken during an epidemic
should be conducted as early and as rapidly as possible. Vaccination campaigns require
substantial staffing and carry a significant overall cost. It is important to ensure that
vaccination does not take place to the detriment of regular health system activities. The
crisis committee should define the epidemic response strategy through taking into
account the available human and financial resources.

Key factors in the decision to undertake a mass vaccination
campaign

Time taken to prepare the campaign

The time period between the start of the epidemic and the start of the vaccination
campaign is of prime importance. A delayed campaign will have a limited impact on the
progression of the epidemic curve.

The time taken to prepare an emergency vaccination campaign should not exceed 1 to 2
weeks. In practical terms, this means that from the crossing of the epidemic threshold the
following must be undertaken as quickly as possible:

– Decision to vaccinate, preparation of a detailed plan and submission of the request
for vaccines (< 2 weeks).
– Preparation of the vaccination campaign immediately following the response of the
ICG and before the arrival of vaccines (7 days after the ICG decision).
– Implementation of the vaccination campaign immediately after receipt of vaccines.

77

Start of the rainy season

In the meningitis belt, the epidemic stops with the arrival of the rains. A vaccination
campaign must therefore start sufficiently in advance of the rainy season to have an effect
on the course of the epidemic curve.

Practical organisation

Logistical considerations, transport and accessibility, human and financial resources and
community acceptance will all have an impact upon the success of a vaccination
campaign.

Target population

A vaccination campaign is directed at the age group bearing the highest age-specific
attack rate. Reliable epidemiological data are therefore necessary to choose the optimal
target population.

– Typically, in the meningitis belt the highest attack rate is found in 2 to 30 year olds,
who represent on average 65% of the population (see Appendix 11), but the precise
age division varies and should be established for each epidemic.
 If resources are limited, especially vaccines, it may be necessary to reduce
the target age group.
 If the attack rate is high among people over 30 years of age, the entire
population should be vaccinated (rarely done).
 Depending on the type of vaccine used, children may be targeted from the
age of 1 or 2 years (polysaccharide vaccines are not effective below 1 year).
– The aim of a vaccination campaign is 100% coverage of the target population.

When vaccine stocks are relatively limited, the choice of population for priority
vaccination becomes more urgent. It is also important to bear in mind problems caused by
pressure from a population dissatisfied at the choice of a restricted age group for
vaccination. Public information and communication is then essential.

Geographic priorities

Priority areas in which to start the vaccination campaign are:

– Areas of high population density and sites of mass gathering where there is a
high transmission risk, especially towns of over 8,000 inhabitants where the
risk of transmission is 2 to 4 times higher than in small villages.
– Areas with the highest incidence rates. It is important to analyse data at the
smallest possible scale in order to identify areas of high incidence within a district
where the overall incidence is low.
– Areas which have recently crossed the operational thresholds and where the
window of opportunity for undertaking a vaccination campaign is optimal.

Approaches

Urban areas

Given that access and logistics are easier, more people can be vaccinated
in a shorter time.

– It is better to establish temporary vaccination points rather than
using health centres, due to the overload of work.
– At the end of the campaign, maintain one or more sites for 8 to 10 days in order to
catch people absent during the campaign.
– It is particularly important to vaccinate in gathering places, such as refugee camps,
prisons, military barracks and pilgrimage areas.

Rural areas

Several approaches are possible.

– Vaccination campaign with mobile vaccination teams:
 Teams may be based for several days in the villages/towns chosen.
 Teams may move from place to place according to an organised circuit, which
carries greater logistic demands.
 Vaccination coverage is lower and the length of the campaign is greater.
 In order to avoid panic and futile population displacements, start by
vaccinating zones or villages where the most cases have been reported.
 Allows nomads to be vaccinated.
– Through strengthening of vaccination capacity in selected health facilities with a
vaccination team and equipment due to the added workload.
 Beyond a distance of 15 km from the health structure, the population is more
difficult to access.

Source of vaccines and the role of the ICG

There is currently a global shortage of vaccines available for a meningitis vaccination
campaign. This shortage is due to several factors, such as the reduction in demand since
the introduction of MenAfriVacTM, unpredictability of demand and the anticipated arrival
of a new low cost pentavalent vaccine.

The ICG (International Co-ordination Group) is a coalition of the WHO and partners
(UNICEF, Médecins Sans Frontières and the International Federation of the Red Cross
and Red Crescent Societies), with the aim of ensuring the availability of a minimum stock
of vaccines for an effective response to epidemics. Each year, the ICG draws up a
prediction of vaccine requirements in terms of doses and composition. The ICG mandates
UNICEF Supply Division to negotiate with manufacturers in order to secure sufficient
vaccines and equipment to respond to anticipated emergency needs. Requests for vaccines
(according to a standard form, Appendix 20) are compiled by the Ministry of Health and
submitted to the ICG, which undertakes to communicate a decision in 48 hours, and
UNICEF SD to deliver stocks 7 days after the ICG decision: a total timescale of 10 days
between submission of the request and arrival of vaccines in the country.

Choice of vaccine

The most decisive factors in undertaking a mass vaccination campaign are the causative
serogroup(s), the interval between the start of the epidemic and the start of planned
vaccination activities, the resources to organise an effective campaign and the availability
of vaccines from the ICG stockpile.

– Vaccination should be undertaken against NmA or NmC.
– If the proportion of NmW identified in laboratory analysis of samples is above
30%, the WHO recommends choosing a polyvalent vaccine active against NmW.
– MSF is less favourable to reactive vaccination against NmW135 as these epidemics
generally have a lower attack rate and shorter duration.
– In the event of an epidemic of NmA in a region not yet covered by the Meningitis
Vaccine Project, MenAfriVacTMis the vaccine of choice.
– The results of a previous vaccination campaign in the region help to predict

Preparing the vaccination
campaign

The masterplan and the timetable

Before starting the campaign it is essential to have a well thought-out plan, in order
to ensure high quality activities and limit unforeseen problems. It is difficult to
correct an error in the masterplan after the start of vaccination.

– Estimate needs.
– Make an inventory of available resources.
– Establish priorities.
– Consider possible delays to implementation:
 Delays in delivery of orders.
 Market days, festivals, food distributions.
– Add some wriggle room for unforeseen problems and rest days for teams.
– Draw up a written plan.
– Communicate with the population.

The masterplan should be readjusted each week according to:

– Results from the vaccination campaign.
 It may be necessary to increase the time at one site if vaccination
coverage is too low.
– Epidemiological data (progression of the epidemic curve).
– Unforeseen events.

The activity timetable is the key tool: a visual summary of the structure of activities
(Appendix 18). The timetable should set out:

– The activities to be performed in each category.
– The name of the person responsible for each activity.
– The timescale of activities.

The vaccination campaign should start only when all the necessary resources are in place:

– Vaccines and vaccination supplies.
– Teams trained.
– Logistics (cold chain, stocking and supply of sites, waste management, transport).
– Population informed.

Cold chain

Inventory of available equipment

Daily tally sheet

Tally sheets are filled out during each session in each vaccination point, compiled each
evening and sent to the co-ordinator at the end of the campaign to assess daily activity.
Each person vaccinated should be counted by age: 2-4 years, 5-14 years, 15-29 years, over
30 years.

Other information should be recorded on the tally sheet:

– Place, vaccination site and date of vaccination.
– Name of the team leader to allow checking of information in the future.
– Number of vials of vaccine and solvent received and the batch numbers to allow
tracking in the event of a problem.
– Number of vials of vaccine used in order to calculate the vaccine utilisation rate
and wastage.
– Supplies received and remaining at the end of the day (number of doses of vaccine,
solvent, syringes, needles, safety boxes etc.)
– Condition of the vaccine vial monitors at the end of the day

Security and order must be strictly organised, especially during the first 2 days of

indispensable for co-ordination and to pass on information to local authorities, other
vaccination teams and the health supervisor.
