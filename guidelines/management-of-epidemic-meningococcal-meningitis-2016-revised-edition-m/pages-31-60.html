Data analysis

Analysis of descriptive data and the epidemic curve allows:

– Identification of the most affected groups within the general population.
– Establishment of the speed of spread of the epidemic and its size.
The main indicators are calculated by district, region or town:

– The weekly incidence rate.
– The cumulative attack rate
 Overall, including all cases from the start to the end of the epidemic
 Specific by area and by age distribution in a sample of health centres.
– The case fatality ratio: crude and/or specific according to place, age group.

Weekly incidence rate

Number of cases during a given week x 100,000

Population

Attack rate

The attack rate is the cumulative incidence rate during the epidemic.

Number of new cases during time interval t x 100,000

Average population during same time t

Example:
There have been 80 newly recorded cases of meningitis among children aged 5-14 years
since the beginning of the outbreak. The total number of children aged 5-14 years in the area
is5,000.
The attack rate for meningitis in children aged 5-14 years during this period is:
80 / 5,000 x 100,000 = 1,600 cases / 100,000

Case fatality ratio

The CFR is an indicator of the quality of case management.

Number of deaths due to meningitis during time interval t x 100

Number of cases of meningitis notified during time interval t

Example:
Of the 80 children aged 5-14 years who contracted meningitis during the course of the
epidemic, 9 died from the disease.
The specific CFR for this age group is: 9 / 80 x 100 = 11.25%.

Initial assessment of a meningitis outbreak

Confirming the existence of an
epidemic

An epidemic of meningitis is defined as an abnormal increase in the number of cases
compared to the number typically seen in the same place at the same time of year.

Thresholds

Thresholds apply to the weekly incidence of suspected or confirmed cases arising in a
population of between 30,000 and 100,000 for epidemics of NmA. Specific thresholds
apply for a population under 30,000. If possible, populations of over 100,000 should be
divided into two or more zones of 30,000-100,000 for analysis.

In 2014 the WHO redefined the thresholds for an epidemic of meningitis. This revision
of thresholds is based on the observation that epidemics caused by non-A serogroups are
generally of a lower amplitude; consequently the thresholds have been reduced to boost
their sensitivity to detect an epidemic. The epidemic caused by NmC in 2015 in Niamey
was not taken into account in this decision.

The alert threshold acts as a warning of an impending epidemic, allowing a rapid
response. When the alert threshold is reached, an epidemic is suspected but not yet
declared. When the threshold is breached:

– Inform local authorities
– Strengthen case monitoring
– Investigate
– Confirm the causative organism by laboratory analysis
– Prepare for a possible epidemic response.

The epidemic threshold is the weekly incidence above which an epidemic is declared.
When the epidemic threshold is breached:

– Start a mass vaccination campaign, if indicated according to the causative
serogroup, within the next 4 weeks
– Distribute medication and equipment to health centres
– Commence case management according to the epidemic protocol
– Inform the population.

Specific situations

In certain situations, high population density leads to the risk of rapid spread of an
epidemic:

– Mass gatherings
– Refugee camps
– Displaced populations
– Closed situations such as schools and barracks.

Normal thresholds do not apply and mass vaccination must be undertaken in the
event of 2 confirmed cases of meningococcal meningitis within a week.

Analysing the local context

Further information may be of use in a future intervention:

– Administrative and health structure of the country.
– Up to date demographic data, whenever possible with age distribution of the
population.
– Maps: administrative boundaries, position of health structures, important towns
and villages and main roads.
– Climate and start of the rainy season.
– Security situation.
– Local events: religious festivals, market days, significant local gatherings.
– Organisation of the ministry of health: organograms, contact details of key
individuals.
– Other partners: UN agencies, bilateral co-operatives, NGOs.
– Presence of a national emergency response plan.
– Availability in the country of medicines and equipment meeting quality control
requirements and regulations for importation of medical equipment and vaccines.
– Attitude of the local population towards vaccination.
– Epidemiological situation in neighbouring countries.

Analysing the first steps of the
response

The initial assessment should describe and evaluate the first measures undertaken
locally:

– Monitoring: case definition, registers, forms for data collection, reporting of data
and feedback, frequency of collection and transmission of data, analysis.
– Laboratory: supplies available (rapid latex tests, transport medium (TI), laboratory
capacity, procedure for sending samples to a reference laboratory.
– Epidemic management: crisis committee, composition, operating methods, official
declaration of an epidemic.
– Case management: strategy, standard treatment protocol, existing stocks, pending
orders, health facilities involved, medical records, free treatment.
– Vaccination: decision to vaccinate or not, type of vaccine, existing stocks, pending
orders, cold chain, training and experience of teams.

Key points

■ The case definition should be available from the start of the epidemic and the same
definition should be used throughout the whole epidemic.

■ The causative serogroup should be confirmed as soon as possible by the reference
laboratory.

■ MSF has a collaboration agreement with the Norwegian Institute of Public Health
in Oslo. It is necessary to negotiate in advance with the Ministry of Health the
possibility of sending samples for export, in duplicate with the national reference
laboratory.

■ The thresholds for the declaration of an epidemic were revised in 2014 by the
WHO.

■ The epidemiological description of an outbreak includes the size, progression,
geographical extent of the outbreak and the groups at risk.

■ Analyse the local context, the previous epidemiological situation and the first steps
taken in the management of the epidemic.

■ A brief and well-structured report should conclude the initial assessment.

Epidemic management

43

Crisis committees

The aim of epidemic management is to match resources to needs in order to ensure an
efficient response. Management of the epidemic should be led by a crisis committee at
national, provincial and district levels.

The crisis committee should meet regularly, initially daily, then weekly. Meetings should
be short, with a clear agenda, and minutes should always be taken and shared. The
composition, terms of reference, frequency of meetings and decision-making procedure
should be clearly defined.

Composition of the committee

The committee should include representatives from:

– The Ministry of Health
– Reference hospitals and laboratories
– The National Programme of Immunisation
– Administrative authorities
– NGOs and other agencies (bilateral development agencies, WHO, UNICEF)

Epidemic response requires strict co-ordination between health services and other
sectors:

– Information: media (radio, newspapers, TV) can be used to broadcast public health
messages such as the occurrence of an outbreak, the first signs of illness, free
treatment, dates and sites of vaccination campaigns.
– Education: schools can be used as vaccination sites; teachers can fill in
vaccination cards.
– Security: policemen may help to keep order during a vaccination campaign.
– Customs: can ease importation of drugs, vaccines and material.

Roles of the committee

Determining the strategy

The management of the response should be co-ordinated by the national crisis
committee. Experience shows that emergencies such as meningitis outbreaks require
strong, centralised management. The teams at regional and district levels are involved in
the alert system and in the implementation of the response policy, as defined at national
level.

The crisis committee should define strategy (Table 2):

– Monitoring and follow-up of data
– Case management
– Vaccination: ensure, in collaboration with vaccination programme officials that the
following documents are prepared as quickly as possible:
 Detailed schedule of the vaccination campaign
 The ICG vaccine order form
– Public information

Free health care

The committee should agree on the principle of free health care and define who is
responsible for each component:
– Consultations
– Hospital admission
– Transfer of severe cases
– Specific drugs
– Supportive therapy
– Laboratory testing

Estimated budget

The crisis committee should decide on an estimated budget, taking into account the
following costs:

– Staff costs: salaries, per diem, training.
– Medical supplies: drugs and equipment.
– Vaccination: vaccines, cold chain equipment, injection equipment, kits, logistics
equipment.
– National and international freight.
– Staff transportation, vehicle rental, fuel, field visits.
– Logistics equipment: ropes, tents, megaphones.
– Administrative equipment and stationary: vaccination cards, stamps, tally sheets,
paper, pens, training materials.
– Communication equipment.

The monitoring system

Demographic data

Demographic data provide the denominators for calculating incidence rates. In many
countries, it is difficult to obtain reliable demographic figures. Often, there is no civil
registration or no recent population census. Furthermore, political or economic reasons
may lead to under- or overestimation of certain population groups, and displaced people
are sometimes not registered at all.

All efforts should be made to obtain the most reliable figures. Data can be obtained from
the last census and updated by extrapolating annual population growth. Local
authorities often have more up-to-date figures than those available at the national level. It
is important to decide from the outset upon the population figures to be used for
monitoring purposes during the outbreak.

Epidemiological monitoring

During the epidemic, epidemiological monitoring is required for:

– Prompt identification of new epidemic foci.
– Monitoring of the course of the outbreak.
– Organisation of appropriate case management.
– Evaluation of the response to the epidemic.

Monitoring systems in meningitis belt countries were strengthened in 2003 by the
Meningitis Vaccine Project, with the introduction of an electronic tool for data entry
in 2005. If the monitoring system is functional and covers all levels, it is not necessary
to add a further system. If this is not the case, the monitoring system should be
strengthened.

Case registration

Registers are the basis of any epidemiological investigation and should be in place in each
health facility. Either existing registers or special meningitis registers can be used.

Registers should provide the name, address, age, sex, date of admission, diagnosis,
treatment, outcome and vaccination status of the patient, indicating if relevant the type of
vaccine and date of vaccination. See Appendix 7.

The weekly report

Number of cases and number of deaths

At the end of every week, each health facility must forward the number of new cases and
deaths due to meningitis for that week (see Appendix 8, Appendix 9).

‘Zero’ reporting

Even if there have been no cases during a given week, this information must be passed to
the health authorities.

Reporting data

Each week, the person in charge of each health facility reports data to the next hierarchical
level. The fastest means of communication should be used (e-mail, telephone, text
messaging, radio). A verbal report should be subsequently confirmed in writing through
data collection forms, and one copy of the verbal report should remain in the health
centre.

Every visit to a health facility in an affected region (supervision of treatment or
preventative measures) should be used as an opportunity for gathering data.

Data collection and analysis

Data are compiled and analysed (time, place, person) at each level on a weekly basis from
the beginning of the epidemic season (see Excel File 1. “Monitoring meningitis cases”).
Presentation of data in visual form through tables, graphs, and maps helps with analysis.
At local level, a simple graph displayed on the dispensary wall allows changes in case
numbers and CFR to be followed.

Laboratory surveillance system

With the emergence of outbreaks caused by new meningococcal strains, it is
important to monitor the causative strain throughout the outbreak in sentinel districts.
The choice of sites (e.g. the most visited) and the frequency of sampling (e.g. weekly)
should be decided in order to ensure good follow-up of the bacteriological pattern
throughout the course of the epidemic.

Case management

57

Case management strategy

Decentralised care

Meningococcal meningitis is a medical emergency which carries a mortality of 50-80%
without treatment. Even with optimal and timely management, mortality remains at 5-
10%, often in the first 24-48 hours, with neurological sequelae in 5-10% of survivors.
The sudden appearance of a large number of seriously ill patients can rapidly
overwhelm the health system. A strategy of decentralised care in health centres should
be put into place. For the patient, rapid access to health care reduces the interval
between the first signs of illness and receiving treatment. For the management of the
epidemic, decentralisation allows effective use of resources. Decentralised care rests on:

– Establishment of simplified protocols for case management.
– Establishment of a referral system for severe cases.
– Supply of peripheral health facilities with medication and equipment.
– Training of staff in peripheral health facilities in case management and data
recording and reporting.
– Support for peripheral health facilities with staff from the referral hospital, if
necessary.
– Ensuring that the principle of free health care is implemented in all health
facilities.

Peripheral health centre

The health centre should:

– Treat uncomplicated cases on an outpatient basis, ensuring compliance with the
full 5 days of treatment. If the single dose ceftriaxone protocol is used, the patient
should be reviewed after 24 hours to check the treatment is working.
– Refer the following cases to hospital:
 Children under 2 months
 Severe symptoms: convulsions, altered consciousness etc.
– It may be possible to treat severe cases as inpatients in the peripheral health facility
provided there are sufficient resources and competent staff.
– Accept referrals from the hospital to complete the full 5 days of treatment on an
outpatient basis.
– Ensure good documentation in the case register.
– Collect and report epidemiological data.
– If appropriate, perform LP and send samples for analysis.

Temporary supplementary treatment centre

If there are a large number of severe cases, supplementary centres should be set up for the
sole treatment of cases of complicated meningitis during the peak times of the epidemic.
These centres are temporary, thus a public building, traditional house or even tents will
suffice.

Training and supervision

In advance of each coming epidemic season, the technical competence, knowledge of case
management protocols and organisational capacity in peripheral health centres should be
reviewed and appropriate training provided:

– Standard updates.
– Extended training sessions in the event of changes in protocols or staff.

Supervisory visits should be organised to respond to practical questions, assess the
quality of case management, stock management and good record keeping.

Single dose ceftriaxone protocol

The WHO recommends keeping to the standard 5 day ceftriaxone treatment protocol
wherever possible. The single dose ceftriaxone protocol may be considered under specific
circumstances:

– Reliable laboratory confirmation of an epidemic of meningococcal meningitis.
– Large scale epidemic which overwhelms management capacity for 5 day treatment.

It is essential that all patients are re-examined after 24 hours. In the case of insufficent
clinical improvement, change to the 5 day ceftriaxone protocol and/or refer.

Children under 2 years should always receive 5 or 7 days’ treatment and should not be
treated with the single dose ceftriaxone protocol.
