Foreword

There is a word that designates meningitis in most – if not all – of the local languages in
the “African meningitis belt”. Populations in this area are familiar with the disease, its
severity and its complications.

Meningitis outbreaks also occur in other areas, though less frequently. Wherever it
occurs, the disease is always associated with fear, sometimes even panic. Effective
treatment and vaccines exist; yet epidemics can reach the scale of national disasters,
causing thousands of deaths. The response to an epidemic usually requires sizeable
means in order to be effective – but above all, it requires an adequate strategy. Responses
aim at reducing mortality and minimising the risk of the outbreak
spreading.
Various types of response can be chosen, based on the epidemiological characteristics
and
the
geographical
location
of
the
epidemic.
When
large
epidemics
occur,
coordination
becomes
a key
factor
in an adequate
response: an International
Coordination Group (ICG) has been in operation since 1997 for that purpose.

This guide is designed for health professionals faced with an epidemic of
meningococcal meningitis. It is divided into several chapters, which cover:

– Initial assessment and definition of an outbreak situation,
– Epidemic response, including case management and vaccination,
– Evaluation and emergency preparedness.

In addition, practical tools are given in the appendices and the CD-ROM attached, as
well as 1) a film showing how to use rapid latex agglutination tests for meningitis and
Trans-Isolate medium and 2) a film showing how to organise a mass vaccination
campaign in emergency situations.

An update to this guide is needed due to the transformation of the situation following on
the one hand the introduction of MenAfriVacTMin 2010, and on the other hand the
appearance of new meningococcal strains. The spectacular success of this conjugate
vaccine against meningitis A has led to the virtual disappearance of Neisseria Meninigidis
A which was until now the cause of large epidemics of meningitis in the meningitis belt.
However, MenAfriVacTMhas not, as hoped, put an end to epidemics of meningitis in
Africa, as other serogroups of N. meningiditis, particularly W135 and C, continue to
spread. In 2015 there was a significant epidemic in Niger and Nigeria of a new
hypervirulent strain of NmC, which seems to have the same explosive epidemic capacity
as NmA.

Features of meningococcal meningitis epidemics

15

Background

The meningitis belt

Epidemics of bacterial meningitis are most likely to occur in sub-Saharan Africa, in an area
known as the "meningitis belt" (or “belt of Lapeysonnie”). This epidemic region stretches
from the Red Sea to the Atlantic, covering parts of Sudan, Chad, Niger, the Central African
Republic, Nigeria, Cameroon, Benin, Burkina Faso, Guinea and Mali (Figure 1). The biggest
outbreaks reported occurred between 1949 and 1951 (250,000 cases, mainly in Nigeria,
Niger, Sudan and Burkina Faso) and between 1995 and 1997 (300,000 cases, mainly in
Nigeria, Niger, Burkina Faso, Ghana and Mali).

The region at risk is increasing to include an ever-larger area. Countries until recently
spared, such as Rwanda, Zambia, Kenya, Tanzania, Burundi, Uganda, and the
Democratic Republic of Congo, have all had recent outbreaks. Countries outside of Africa
may also be affected by meningitis epidemics, but at a lower intensity than that typically
seen in the meningitis belt.

Cycles and seasonality

Classic epidemics caused by NmA typically occur in a cyclical manner every 8 to 10
years. The onset is generally in the middle of the cold dry season (December to
February) and outbreaks usually end spontaneously after 3 to 5 months, at the
beginning of the rainy season (May-June).

The infectious agent

Epidemic bacterial meningitis is caused by one particular Gram-negative organism: the
meningococcus (Neisseria meningitidis). Within this species, 12 serogroups have been
identified: A, B, C, D, X, Y, Z, W135, 29E, H, I and L. Within each serogroup, there are
serotypes, sub-types and clones. For example: the A-4 P 1.9 clone III-1 is a meningococcus
of group A, serotype 4, sub-type P 1.9, clonal complex III-1. Detailed characterisation of the
organism allows the spread of an epidemic to be accurately plotted from year to year.
Before the introduction of a conjugate vaccine against NmA, 90% of infections were caused
by serogroups A, B and C: serogroup A was classically responsible for the majority of
epidemics in Africa, serogroup B is the dominant organism in Europe and serogroup C in
the US. In the meningitis belt, NmA was historically responsible for 80-85% of cases of
meningitis.

While other bacteria can cause sporadic meningitis, they are not responsible for epidemics.
The most common pathogens causing non-epidemic meningitis are Haemophilus influenzae
(the main cause of meningitis in children under one year of age) and Streptococcus
pneumoniae (often associated with pneumonia, otitis, etc. and which carries a high mortality
rate).

During the course of an epidemic, multiple organisms may be identified together: either in
the case of a mixed epidemic caused by more than one serogroup of meningococcus (for
example, a mixed epidemic of NmW and C), or if sporadic cases occur during an epidemic.
The relative proportion of causative organisms varies from one epidemic to another.

Mode oftransmission and carriage

Humans are the only reservoir. Meningococci harboured by a carrier are spread by
respiratory droplets and colonise the nasopharynx of a susceptible individual. This
infection usually goes unnoticed or is heralded by simple pharyngitis. In a very small
proportion of individuals, infection spreads via the bloodstream, leading either to
meningitis or to meningococcal septicaemia. Worldwide, around 10% of the population
are carriers. Almost 90% of asymptomatic carriers develop protective antibodies within
7 to 14 days of acquiring the organism. The duration of this natural immunity isunknown. The
duration of carriage is long: 5-15 weeks and up to 9-16 months in some cases.

Features of meningococcal meningitis epidemics

17

Age groups at risk

During epidemics, meningitis typically affects children over 6 months, adolescents and
young adults. Only 10-20% of cases occur above the age of 30. However, higher attack rates
have been reported on rare occasions among those over 30 years of age (Uganda and
Burundi in 1992, Guinea in 1993).

The age group most at-risk should always be determined during the initial
assessment, especially when the causative serogroup is other than NmA.

Factors influencing the spread of the meningococcus and the
extension of an outbreak

The epidemiological factors contributing to meningococcal meningitis outbreaks are not
fully understood. The following factors predispose to meningococcal infection:
– The virulence of the causative strain.
– Alteration of nasopharyngeal mucosa due to climatic changes, such as dry or cold
weather, seasonal winds (e.g. Harmattan winds), or by a viral infection.
– Immune deficiency.
– Overcrowding: transmission is increased when many people share the same
enclosed living space (densely populated urban areas, poor socio-economic
conditions, refugee camps).

Change in the epidemic situation since the introduction of
MenAfriVacTM

The epidemic season of 1996-97 saw the largest outbreak of meningitis on record, with
250,000 cases and 25,000 deaths. In 2001, the Meningitis Vaccine Project (MVP) was
created as a partnership between the WHO and the NGO PATH, in partnership with
the vaccine manufacturer Serum Institute of India and public health co-ordinators
throughout Africa. The MVP’s goal was to develop and introduce a new vaccine
designed to meet the needs of the meningitis belt. The vaccine should cost under $0.5
per dose, be stable in high temperatures and confer long lasting protection for the
individual and herd immunity for the community, in order to break the cycle of
repeated mass vaccination campaigns in the same region. With the financial backing of
the
Gates
Foundation,
the
vaccine
was
accorded
an
accelerated
process
of
development, clinical trials and prequalification by the WHO. At the end of 2010
MenAfriVacTM, the conjugate vaccine against meningitis A, was used in the first mass
vaccination campaigns in 4 countries in the meningitis belt.

The Meningitis Vaccine Project has been a notable success and has effectively ended the era
of meningitis A epidemics. The last significant outbreak of NmA took place in Nigeria in
2009 and to date there has not been a single reported case of NmA meningitis in a
vaccinated individual. Despite this success, the vaccine has not put a stop to meningitis
outbreaks and since 2010 there have been ongoing outbreaks due to non-NmA serogroups
of the meningococcus, particularly NmW135 and NmC. The most worrying organism is a
new hypervirulent strain of NmC, which was first seen in small outbreaks in 2013 and 2014
and subsequently caused a large epidemic in 2015 in Niger (8,537 cases and 575 deaths,
including the capital, Niamey) and Nigeria (2,696 cases and 130 deaths). Previously, NmC
epidemics were rare in the meningitis belt, the last recorded events being epidemics in
Nigeria in 1975 and Burkina Faso in 1979. The new strain of NmC shows a high capacity to
spread and could potentially cause epidemics on the scale historically seen with NmA.

Features of meningococcal meningitis epidemics

19

Clinical features

The incubation period varies from 2 to 10 days, most commonly 3 to 4 days.

Adults and children over one year

Typical meningitic syndrome of sudden onset:

– Neck stiffness
– Photophobia
– Fever up to 39°- 40°C
– Severe headache
– Nausea, vomiting
– Purpura, localised or generalised, is typical of meningococcal infection
Severe forms:

– Febrile coma
– Seizures
– Purpura fulminans: septic shock, circulatory collapse, necrotic purpura, acute
abdominal syndrome. This complication is more frequent in meningococcus
group B infections and has a poor prognosis.

Children under one year

The diagnosis is more difficult, with varying symptoms:

– Refusal to eat; gastrointestinal disturbances (diarrhoea, vomiting)
– Behavioural change (drowsiness, feeble or abnormal cry, whining)
– Abnormal movements (tremors, localised or generalised seizures, upward
deviation of the eyes)
– Altered tone (hypotonia, neck stiffness often absent, limp neck)
– Fever may be absent
– Bulging fontanelle when not crying, late sign with poor prognosis
– Severe forms: coma, purpura (look specifically for purpura as often associated
with rapid progression)

Sequelae

Neurological sequelae are a major problem. 5-10% of patients who recover will suffer
serious sequelae: deafness, hemiplegia, facial nerve palsy, mental retardation and
epilepsy.

Description of epidemics

Since the introduction of MenAfriVacTM, predicting the course of a meningitis epidemic has
become more tricky. Epidemics caused by other serogroups do not necessarily show the
same characteristics as those caused by NmA.

Duration

Classic epidemics of NmA meningitis last on average for 10 to 14 weeks, varying between
4 to 20 weeks. The peak of the epidemic generally occurs within the 4 weeks after the
crossing of the epidemic threshold. Epidemics of NmW are generally of a shorter duration.
The epidemic of NmC seen in 2015 had a peak similar to a classic epidemic of NmA.
Nonetheless, it is too early to draw conclusions about the duration of NmC epidemics.

Figure 3 Meningitis epidemic. Number of cases and weekly incidence per 100,000
population. Khartoum, Sudan, January-April 1988 (Source: MoH)

Incidence and attack rate

During an epidemic of NmA, in some districts the attack rate can exceed 2,000 cases per
100,000 people (2%). On average, the attack rate is approximately 500 cases per 100,000.
The attack rate is lower for NmW and NmX but seems similar for an epidemic of NmC.

Features of meningococcal meningitis epidemics

Case fatality ratio (CFR)

The CFR of the disease depends on the quality and speed of treatment. Untreated
meningococcal meningitis leads to death in 50 to 80% of cases. With appropriate case
management the CFR can be reduced to between 8 and 15%.

Key points

■
The meningitis belt is the region most at risk of a meningitis epidemic and
comprises 21 countries in Subsaharan Africa.

■
Epidemic meningitis is caused exclusively by Neisseria meningitidis. Before 2010
Nm serogroup A was the main causative strain.

■
Since 2010 the Meningitis Vaccine Project has vaccinated 235m people against
NmA with a new conjugate vaccine designed for Africa.

■
Subsequent epidemics are caused by other serogroups, notably NmW and a new
hypervirulent strain of NmC, which caused a significant outbreak in Niger and
Nigieria in 2015 and which appears to have the same epidemic potential as NmA.

■
Epidemics of Nm follow a seasonal pattern, beginning in December/January
during the cold and dry season and ending in May/June with the rains.

■
Transmission is from person to person and nasal carriage is important.

■
Under 30 year olds are the most affected age group.

■
Incidence and attack rates are well described for classic epidemics of NmA but less
well understood for other serogroups.

■
The CFR is approximately 10% during an outbreak, even with optimal case
management and 5-10% of survivors will suffer neurological sequelae.

Initial assessment of a meningitis outbreak

25

Confirming the diagnosis

Identification of the causative pathogen is even more important in the post- MenAfriVacTM
era, when NmA is no longer the presumed serogroup. Identification of the causative
serogroup is required for the management of the epidemic. Only epidemics caused by NmA,
C and W135 can be prevented by vaccination (see Chapter 6).

It is imperative to confirm the first suspect cases of meningitis with laboratory tests.

Number of samples required

It is not possible to specify a target number of samples. It is important to be guided by the
context and to send a number sufficient for the confirmation and follow-up of the
epidemic.

– At the beginning of the epidemic, particularly when the number of cases
approaches or crosses the alert threshold, it is imperative to have suspected cases
confirmed by the reference laboratory.
– After confirmation of the epidemic, samples should be sent from sentinel districts
in order to establish a representative picture of the epidemic in the region and in
order to monitor the biological course of the epidemic over time.
– It is not necessary to perform LP as a matter of course on every patient once the
epidemic has been declared.

Documentation

– Each sample must be accompanied by a standard form which is correctly filled in
(Appendix 2).
– A register is compulsory in each health structure and laboratory in order to follow-
up tests undertaken (Gram, latex), samples sent for analysis (TI), their destination
and the results subsequently obtained (Appendix 1).
– The same identification number must be used for a given patient throughout the
chain.
– Information should be recorded electronically in an Excel file: only the standard
MSF tool should be used (see Excel File 1. “Monitoring meningitis cases”).

Collecting samples

Lumbar puncture

– Should be performed aseptically by an experienced practitioner.
– The CSF is generally cloudy or purulent, but clear CSF does not exclude meningitis.
– Must never delay treatment of the patient. If it is not possible to perform LP
immediately, give antibiotics.
– See Appendix 3

Tests

Gram staining

Gram staining can identify the meningococcus but is not sufficient to determine the
particular serogroup.

Rapid diagnostic tests: latex agglutination tests (Pastorex®)

Identifies the causative bacterium and if meningococcus, the serogroup.

– See the film “Latex agglutination rapid tests and TransIsolate” for instructions on
use of Pastorex®.
– Kits must be stored between 2-8°C; thus a cold chain is essential.

Pastorex® has been validated for detection of NmC, W and Y but field experience remains
limited. During the epidemic of NmC in Niamey in 2015, the reference laboratory showed
a sensitivity of 75% and specificity of 94% in comparison to gold standard molecular
techniques.

Other rapid tests are in development but are not yet available:

In the meningitis belt, it is essential to have all reagents and equipment, especially latex
agglutination tests, ready from the start of the epidemic season.

Initial assessment of a meningitis outbreak

27

International reference laboratory tests

MSF has a collaboration agreement with the reference laboratory of the Norwegian
Institute of Public Health in Oslo. Efforts should be made to negotiate in advance with the
ministry of health the right to export samples to Oslo, possibly in duplicate with samples
send to the national laboratory.

Transport of samples

2 tubes of CSF should be sent in biological triple packaging:

– 1 ml CSF in a tube with transport medium (TI) for culture, sensitivity and
serogrouping.
– 1 to 2 ml CSF in a cryotube for PCR (polymerase chain reaction).
See Appendix 3 for use of TI.

– TI should be stored in the refrigerator until 30 min before use and should never be
frozen.

Culture and antibiotic sensitivity

Allows identification of the bacterium and determination of sensitivity to antibiotics.

Determining the type, subtype and clone

Allows detailed characterisation of the bacterium:

– Serogrouping by latex agglutination
Molecular analysis by PCR (polymerase chain reaction):

– Identification of the bacterium
– Serogroup genes
– Virulence genes
– Genomic sequencing

description of the outbreak: number of cases and deaths, age groups at risk, and size and
geographical scope of the outbreak. It should be defined by health authorities and must
remain consistent throughout the epidemic in order to ensure reliable monitoring. The
case definition must be simple enough to be used in even the most remote areas.

Any case definition is a compromise: it may include patients who are not true cases (over-
estimation by an insufficiently specific case definition) or, conversely, exclude patients that
are true cases (under-estimation by an insufficiently sensitive case definition).

In practice, given that only a minority of cases are laboratory confirmed, probable cases
form the basis of the monitoring system during an outbreak.

When faced with a suspected meningitis case, medical staff should use their clinical
experience to treat the patient, even if the clinical features do not exactly fit the case
definition.

Counting the number of cases and
deaths

An enhanced monitoring system was put in place in the countries of the meningitis
belt during the Meningitis Vaccination Programme and is relatively functional.
Meningitis cases may be found:

– In hospitals, health centres and dispensaries (by counting cases in the register
books)
– If necessary, in villages, by interviewing village leaders or family members of
reported cases, by visiting households, cemeteries, etc.
– Essential data to be recorded are: name, age, sex, address, date of admission,
treatment given, outcome, vaccination status.

Avoid duplication of notification when patients are transferred.
