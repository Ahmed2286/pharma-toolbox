Performance of teams

Under optimal conditions, 1 vaccinator, using auto-disable syringes prepared by 2
assistants and working 6 hours a day can vaccinate:

– In a densely populated area, 180 to 240 people per hour, or a maximum of 1,000 to
1,500 people per day.
– In a sparsely populated area, 300-600 people per day.

Example:
In 1993 80% of the population of the town of Kankan (Guinée Conakry), 96,000 people,
were vaccinated in 4 days by vaccination teams consisting of a total of 18 vaccinators.

Number of teams needed

The number of teams needed is calculated according to the size of the target
population to be vaccinated, the expected performance per team, and the intended length
of the campaign (see Appendix 31).

Example:
The town of Nzangwa has a target population of 100,750. It is estimated that one vaccinator
can perform 1500 vaccinations per day.

– To vaccinate in 11 days, 6 teams are needed.
– To vaccinate in 7 days, 10 teams are needed.

Training and supervision

– All teams should receive training or have their skills updated prior to the start of
the campaign.
– A practice run should be done before the campaign, involving the entire team, with
all the necessary consumables and equipment.
– Job descriptions should be prepared and distributed to all employees, as well as
other documents or guidelines needed.
– Vaccination campaigns generally start in urban settings, where supervision is easier
and where teams will gain experience through the volume of vaccinations performed.
The same teams should then be sent to rural areas.

Organisation of the campaign

Medical and logistics teams should work together for overall organisation and
planning. The key points to discuss together are:

– Siting, organisation and management of the central warehouse.
– Cold chain organisation and monitoring.
– Organisation of supply.
– Organisation of transport: cars, drivers, fuel.
– Choice of vaccination sites and equipment required.
– The waste management circuit.

Central warehouse

Siting and organisation

If possible, keep everything together in the same site: cold chain, medical stock,
logistics stock, vehicles and fuel, and equip all teams from the central location.
– If not possible, look for sites close together in order to improve organisation
and supervision.
– In preference choose a large town and a site always easily accessible, with
electricity 24 hours a day to support the cold chain.

Divide the warehouse into 5 sections according to the surface area required:
– Active cold chain (freezers, refrigerators, cold room): 0.3 m3/1 000 doses of
vaccine, although conjugate vaccines may take up a greater volume.
– Passive cold chain: 2.85 m3 per vaccination team
– Area for preparation of vaccination equipment: 2.5 m3per team
– Medical equipment and supplies
– Logistics equipment and supplies.

Stock management

Management of the stock should be entrusted to a person with adequate training helped
by one or more assistants, depending on the workload. The job of each assistant should be
clearly defined and one should be trained to replace the person in charge if necessary. The
timetable (orders, distribution, stock-control activities) should be planned to spread the
workload as evenly as possible.

Monitoring of stock

Stock cards
The stock card is the main tool for monitoring of stock (Appendix 41). A stock card is
made out for each item (vaccine, solvent, drugs and equipment), and is updated each
time the item is used or replenished. The stock card should record:

– Product details
 International Non-proprietary Name (INN)
 Formulation and dose
 Batch number.
– Stock movements with the date
 Entry and source
 Exit and destination.
 Quantities are always written in doses and not in vials.
 Only record one action per line, even if several stock movements occur on
the same day.
– Orders made and the date.
 When an order is placed, note the name of the supplier and the amount
ordered but do not update the ‘stock’ column until the order is received and
recorded in the ‘entry’ column.
– Inventories and the date.
 If stock cards are well maintained, the stock column corresponds to the
inventory. Any irregularity should be investigated.

Overview table
For critical items, set up an overview table (Appendix 40) in addition to the stock card and
update it daily:

– Vaccines
– Autodisable syringes
– Needles and syringes for dilution
– Safety boxes for disposal of sharps.

Order and delivery forms
Pre-printed order forms make it easier to prepare orders and inventories and to avoid
transcription errors (Appendix 42). Signed copies of these documents should be kept
by the person in charge of distribution and by each health facility.

Organisation of the cold chain

Central cold chain

A fixed central refrigeration and deep freezing unit should be set up to supply the teams.
Choose a large town or central base which is always accessible and supplied with
electricity 24 hours a day and install a generator for use during power cuts. The entire
cold chain should be centralised, including vaccine management and distribution,
distribution of ice packs and logistics equipment, vehicles and fuel.

A technician is put in charge of the cold chain:

– Ensuring that equipment is in good working order and is correctly maintained.
– Checking and recording temperatures (one monitoring sheet for each apparatus).
– Ensuring that refrigerators are turned on 24 to 48 hours before stocking with
vaccines.
– Cooling the solvents (refrigerator or cool box) 12 hours before use.
– Freezing ice packs.
– Preparing cool boxes and vaccine carriers for delivery to vaccination sites.

A pharmacist or medical practitioner is in charge of:

– Managing stocks and ensuring stock movements are recorded correctly, with dates
and batch numbers noted for each entry/exit.
– Preparation and distribution of vaccines and solvents.
– Checking unused vaccines and solvents and returning them to the stock.

Cold chain at vaccination sites

During a vaccination session, store reconstituted vaccines between +2° and +8°C in a
portable vaccine carrier (which should not be used for storing unreconstituted vaccine).

Vaccinator’s assistants should fill the auto-disable syringes and pass them directly to the
vaccinator for injection. Syringes should no longer be filled in advance.

Store half-melted ice packs in a cool box: this saves time when re-freezing.

Criteria for choosing a site

Depending on the context (rural or urban area, refugee camp), the team can use schools,
places of worship, community halls, tents, or even the shade of a tree. If possible, avoid
using health centres in order not to disrupt regular care and activities.

– The area should be easily accessible to all.
– The site should be large enough to allow the population to circulate smoothly and
for vaccination teams to work comfortably.
– If the site is too spacious, controlling people’s movements can be difficult.
– The waiting area should be shady and large enough to hold a queue of at least 50
metres.
– Separate entry and exit points are essential to avoid confusion and jostling.
– Clearly cordoned areas with walls or barriers facilitate triage and security.

Organisation of the site

Prepare the site and all the equipment the day before. The first two days are usually the
busiest of the campaign.

– Open-air sites should only be used in rural areas.
– If the site is very large, use rope to cordon off the entire circuit from entry to exit.
– Organise queues with ropes or tapes. The line must be at least 50 metres long and
narrow enough to keep the queue single file. A queue that zigzags is better than a
straight queue for reducing the pressure of the crowd.
– Provide drinking water in the waiting areas.
– Vaccination cards are filled out at the entry to the site; tally sheets are filled out
right after vaccination.
– The area where assistants prepare the vaccination syringes should be next to the
vaccinators.
– Tally sheets are filled out immediately after vaccination.
– Equipment and consumables should be stored out of reach of the population but
readily accessible to the vaccination team.
– The waste zone should be set apart and secured.

Transport

Reliable transport is essential for conducting activities (deploying vaccination teams,
supply, waste collection, supervision, etc.). Transport needs will depend on:

– The number and location of vaccination sites
– The number of teams and duration of vaccination
– The number of supervision teams
– The arrangements for storage and supply in the field.

Ensuring transport and storage requires significant resources:

– A central storage facility at district level, ideally with the stock and cold chain in
the same location.
– A thorough management system with standardised tools (Appendix 43).
– Sufficient staff.

Waste management

A vaccination campaign generates a large amount of waste which poses a risk to staff and
the population if not correctly handled. The system for waste management should be
ready from the outset of the campaign.
Every day waste should be:

– Sorted by category
– Collected and gathered at a single site
– Disposed of quickly.

Sorting waste

A vaccination campaign produces 3 types of waste which must be correctly sorted at each site:

– Sharps: autodisable syringes, syringes and needles for dilution, empty vials of
vaccines and solvents.
– Softs: gloves, cotton wool, packaging.
– Other waste: vials containing reconstituted vaccine.

Storage of waste should be organised in specific areas with restricted and monitored
access.

Treatment and disposal

Waste disposal may either be centralised or carried out in situ. For disposal techniques,
see the MSF guide Public health engineering in unstable situations.

The method of choice is centralisation at a site that has good treatment and disposal
facilities. Waste is transferred to this site, where it is treated at high temperature and
disposed of.

– It is important to find a secluded area in each vaccination site, well secured to make
it inaccessible to the local community, for the storage of waste prior to transport.
– Transport of waste to the disposal area must be on a regular basis.
– Staff must wear protective clothing during transport.
– Safety boxes for sharps should be transported in a sealed container (plastic bucket
with cover, metal chest) in order to reduce the risk of spilling dangerous sharps.

If it is not feasible to transport all waste to the central site, waste should be treated and
disposed of on site.

– Softs may easily be burned on site.
– Whenever possible, always dispose of safety boxes at the central site.
– Empty vials or vials containing unused vaccine may only be disposed of centrally
and never on site.

Quality and safety of vaccination

Vaccine quality

Quality control of vaccines is of the highest importance at all levels.
– When the first delivery of vaccines is received, read the manufacturer’s
instructions carefully.
– Check the name, source, labelling, expiry date, amount delivered and the batch
number.
– Only use the solvent supplied by the manufacturer and check it corresponds with
that specified by the vaccine. In case of loss of solvent, contact the pharmacist
and/or the manufacturer.
– Examine the items and check the solvent is clear and without deposit.
– Ensure there has not been a break in the cold chain: check the vaccine vial monitor
(VVM), temperature chart and Stop!Watch® monitoring card (Appendix 25).
– After each vaccination session, throw away any remaining vials of reconstituted
vaccine.

In the event of a break in the cold chain:
– Put the vaccines in quarantine in a refrigerator between +2°C and +8°C and do not
use pending the pharmacist’s decision.
– Fill out a report (Appendix 26):
 Description of the incident: place, date, details, actions taken.
 List the affected items with the manufacturer’s name, batch number and
quantities.

Injection safety

Protecting staff

Vaccinators:

– Always wear gloves.
– Use only autodisable syringes.
– Ensure correct injection technique.
– Ensure correct disposal of waste.

Logisticians and staff in charge of waste disposal:

– Wear protective clothing: thick gloves (as a minimum, heavy-duty gloves), work
overalls with long sleeves and trousers, boots.
– Ensure correct sorting of waste, in particular that sharps are not thrown into plastic
bags.
– Ensure that safety boxes are correctly closed and are never filled above the line.

Accidental exposure to blood (AEB)

– Ensure that all staff have been trained in the correct course of action before the start
of the vaccination campaign.
– Ensure that the protocol for managing AEB, forms for reporting exposure and
treatment kits are available (Appendix 37)
– Nominate a doctor to serve as AEB focal point: to assess staff who have been
exposed, take charge of follow-up and to report the incident.

Surveillance of Adverse Events Following Immunisation (AEFI)

AEFI may be minor (fever, reaction at the injection site) or severe (anaphylactic reaction)
requiring hospitalisation, causing disability or even death. It is important to watch out for
AEFI from the first day of the vaccination campaign until 30 days after the end. All
suspected AEFI must be reported (Appendix 36).

Always read the manufacturer’s instructions.

Key points

■ Vaccinate as a priority the age groups and areas with the highest attack rate.

■ Areas of high population density should be vaccinated first.

■ The cold chain is crucial to ensure the quality of vaccines.

■ Tally sheets are essential for monitoring of vaccination coverage.

■ Vaccination teams are built around a vaccinator.

■ Vaccines must be used as soon as the assistant has filled the autodisable syringe.
Syringes should no longer be prepared in advance.

■ Vaccination sites should be carefully selected and organised.

■ Under optimal conditions, one team can vaccinate 180 to 240 people per hour, or
1,000 to 1,500 people per day.

■ The waste collection and disposal system should be prepared before starting the
campaign.

Monitoring and evaluation of activities

107

Methodology

Evaluation of the response includes the monitoring of activities and evaluation of the
intervention.

Monitoring of activities

Monitoring of activities enables an assessment of the management of the epidemic,
analysis of results and identification of any problems. It should be carried out from the
start to the end of the epidemic.

– Frequency:
 Weekly (monitoring, curative case management)
 Daily (vaccination activities).
– Activities to be monitored:
 Epidemiological and laboratory monitoring
 Case management
 Vaccination.

Patient management

Monitoring of the number of cases and the CFR for each health facility allows the
highlighting of sites where supply and supervision need to be strengthened. The aim is to
ensure that the number of treatment packages distributed is always higher than the
number of cases declared.

Vaccination

Vaccination tally sheets are filled in every day at the vaccination sites in order to estimate
the vaccination coverage. This daily analysis is used to assess the results. If the results are
not satisfactory, identify the causes (shortage of vaccines or medical supplies, too few
teams, lack of public information, poor choice of sites) and, if necessary, take corrective
measures (supply, number of vaccination days at a site, number of teams, public
information messages).

At the end of the campaign, fill out and analyse the summary table for each site
(Appendix 30) and calculate the indicators for the vaccine campaign in order to write the
final report.

Evaluation of the response

The evaluation may be exhaustive or targeted to a few activities. Analysis should be
directed towards:

■ Effectiveness
■ Accessibility
■ Timeliness
■ Safety and quality
■ Resources mobilised

Data are collected during the epidemic, recorded and analysed at the end of

Certain information requires field visits and is only collected in selected health facilities
or vaccination sites, using appropriate epidemiological sampling methods. These visits
must be scheduled during the campaign.

Monitoring and evaluation of activities

119

Key points

■ Monitoring of activities takes place on a weekly basis, from the beginning
until the end of the epidemic.

■ Monitoring of case management allows the tailoring of supply in order to
ensure that the number of treatment packages delivered is always above
the number of cases reported.

■ Monitoring of vaccination takes place daily at each vaccination site using
the vaccination tally sheets.

■ The vaccine coverage and the vaccine utilisation rate should be
calculated.

■ The

the
