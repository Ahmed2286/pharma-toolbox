4.6 Empyema

Empyema is a collection of pus in the space between the lung and the inner surface of the chest
wall (pleural space)1. It is a complication of a bacterial pneumonia, most commonly caused by
Streptococcus pneumoniae or Staphylococcus aureus1,2. It can often occur as a complication of
measles. In children presenting with empyema, evaluate with history and examination if there
are any indications of underlying tuberculosis (TB) as a differential diagnosis.

4.6.1 Clinical features

– Persistent fever and/or cough, chest pain, dyspnoea and oxygen requirement.
– May prefer to lie on the affected side to reduce pain.
– Consider if no improvement after 48 to 72 hours of treatment for community-acquired
pneumonia, or if a child diagnosed with pneumonia has additional signs such as:
• Shallow respirations to minimise pain.
• Decreased air movement heard on auscultation.
• Dullness to percussion.
• Increased resonance of voice sounds (due to enhanced transmission).
• Rarely severe respiratory distress, sepsis and shock.
• Possibly a pleural rub on the side of the fluid collection.

Investigations

– CXR and/or lung ultrasound, if available:
• Check if there is evidence of fluid (see Figure 4.3).
• Check for any radiological signs of TB.
– Pleural aspiration (ultrasound-guided ideally):
• Check fluid appearance (colour, blood-stained, straw-coloured, translucency, etc)
• Perform Gram stain and culture, if available.
– Tuberculosis screening using TB diagnostic algorithms, microscopy and culture or
GeneXpert where available (see Section 4.11: Tuberculosis).
– Malaria RDT in endemic areas
– Hb
– BGL
– CRP, where available
– Blood culture, if signs of systemic illness

4.6.2 Management

– Admit, preferably to an ICU facility, for treatment and stabilisation.
– Treat any fever to improve the patient’s comfort and give analgesia if pain (such as chest
pain) is present (see Chapter 15, Section 15.4: Pain management).
– Administer oxygen if SpO2 < 92% in room air or severe respiratory distress.
– If the child is in severe respiratory distress, nil-by-mouth is recommended.
– Administer antibiotic treatment (see below).
– Ensure adequate fluid and calorie intake and monitor BGL over the first 24 to 48 hours,
as there is a risk of dehydration and hypoglycaemia.
– Monitor and record vital signs as often as required using an early warning system (see MSF
Manual of Nursing Care Procedures, Assessment and vital signs, Charts: Vital sign charts).

Antibiotic treatment

First choice:

– Start (or switch to, if already being treated for pneumonia) ceftriaxone IV (or IM) 80 mg/kg
(max. 4 g) every 24 hours (or cefotaxime IV 50 mg/kg every 8 hours).
– Add clindamycin IV 10 mg/kg (max. 600 mg) every 8 hours if:
• Critically unwell child
• Large empyema
• Confirmed MRSA
• Known high prevalence of MRSA in the region
• No improvement after 48-72 hours

Alternative:

– amoxicillin/clavulanic acid IV (dosage expressed in amoxicillin):
• < 3 months of age: 30 mg/kg every 12 hours
• ≥ 3 months of age: 30 mg/kg (max. 1.2 g) every 8 hours
Continue chosen antibiotics for at least 7 days parenterally (IV) and until afebrile for 3 days,
then:

– Switch to oral antibiotic treatment (if able to take oral medication) to complete a minimum

Surgical drainage
A small empyemac does not require surgical drainage. However, for large a empyemad
or empyema that does not respond to antibiotics, the definitive treatment is removal
of pus through the placement of a chest drain3:

– If clinician is familiar with the procedure and anaesthesia/surgery is available, proceed to
insert a chest drain under anaesthesia in the operating room.
– If surgery/anaesthesia is not available but a clinician trained to perform the procedure
is, insert a chest drain under sedation. Refer to MSF Standards for Paediatric Procedural
Sedation.
– If there is no possibility to insert a chest drain due to lack of trained staff, consider referral.
– As a last option, if no trained staff are available and referral is not an option, perform
repeated pleural punctures for pus drainage.
– Upright chest x-ray (if available and the child is stable) is recommended after placement
of a chest drain or after pleural puncture to confirm position of the drain and exclude a
pneumothorax.
