4.8 Diphtheria

Diphtheria is a bacterial infection due to Corynebacterium diphtheriae, spread from person to
person through inhalation of infected respiratory droplets of symptomatic or asymptomatic
individuals, or direct contact with contaminated objects or diphtheria skin lesions1,2. The
incubation period is 2 to 5 days (maximum 10 days) during which C. diphtheriae multiplies in
the upper respiratory tract and secretes a toxin that causes local and systemic inflammation and
complications3. Mortality may result from upper airway obstruction due to pseudomembrane
formation, or systemic damage from the toxin, including damage to the myocardium and
neurological system.

Diphtheria is a notifiable disease, and all cases should be reported to local or national public
health authorities. It is also a vaccine-preventable disease and vaccination is part of routine
childhood immunisation programmes (EPI).

4.8.1 Clinical features

Respiratory and upper airway signs include:

– Pharyngitis, rhinopharyngitis, tonsillitis or laryngitis.
– Thick, greyish, firmly adherent pseudomembrane of the pharynx, nasopharynx, tonsils, or
larynx, which bleeds profusely when dislodged.
– Dysphagia and cervical lymphadenopathy, at times progressing to massive swelling of the
neck (bull neck).
– Airway obstruction and possible suffocation when the infection extends to the nasal
passages, larynx, trachea and bronchi.

Systemic signs resulting from the toxin:

– Low-grade fever
– Cardiac dysfunction (tachycardia, arrhythmias), severe myocarditis with heart failure and
possibly cardiogenic shock 3 to 7 days or 2 to 3 weeks after onset of the disease.
– Neuropathies in 2 to 8 weeks after the onset of disease leading to nasal voice and difficulty
with swallowing (paralysis of the soft palate), vision (ocular motor paralysis), breathing
(paralysis of respiratory muscles) and ambulation (limb paralysis).
– Oliguria, anuria and acute renal failure.

Diagnosis should be made clinically if pseudomembranous pharyngitis is present4.
Differential diagnoses include epiglottitis (Section 4.3: Epiglottitis), bacterial tracheitis (Section
4.4: Bacterial tracheitis), and acute pharyngitis (see MSF Clinical Guidelines).

4.8.2 Investigations

To confirm diagnosis:

– Collect swab samples before starting antibiotic treatment (see MSF Collection, storage and
transport of samples from field to reference laboratory). Swab affected areas of throat
(tonsils, pharyngeal mucosa, soft palate, exudate, ulcer, etc.) and nasopharynx and send
for culture (and sensitivity) to isolate C. diphtheriae.
• PCR test (detection of diphtheria toxin gene) if available.
Treatment should be started before waiting for laboratory confirmation4.

4.8.3 Management

Infection prevention and control measures

– Ensure transmission-based precautions are taken, including correct use of personal
protective equipment (PPE).
– Admit to a separated area or with physical distance from other admitted children with
dedicated nursing staff and equipment to reduce the risk of cross-infection.
– If multiple cases, group together with other cases in a separated area from other patients
(cohorting).
– Isolation should continue until 2 negative culture results are obtained, 24 hours apart,
after completion of antibiotics4.

Diphtheria antitoxin (DAT)

– Administer diphtheria antitoxin (DAT) (derived from horse serum) as soon as possible
after diagnosis is made based on clinical suspicion (presence of pseudomembranous
pharyngitisa).4
– Ensure close monitoring and immediate availability of resuscitation equipment as there is
a risk of an anaphylactic reaction to DAT, especially in children with asthma (see Chapter 2,
Section 2.4: Anaphylaxis).

Antibiotic treatment

– Start concomitantly with DAT as soon as possible after diagnosis is made based on clinical
suspicion (presence of pseudomembranous pharyngitis). If possible, take swab before
starting antibiotics but do not wait for bacteriological confirmation.
– Oral route is preferred, administer via IV or IM only if the child is unable to swallow3.
Switch to oral antibiotic treatment as soon as possible.
– Give for 14 days or according to length of treatment recommended by the national
protocol.
– Macrolides are the preferred first choice3:

First-line:
azithromycin PO or IV, 10 to 12 mg/kg (max. 500 mg) once daily

Alternative:
erythromycin PO or IV, 10 to 15 mg/kg (max. 500 mg) 4 times daily

– If macrolides are unavailable:

phenoxymethylpenicillin (penicillin V) PO:
< 40 kg: 10 to 15 mg/kg (max. 500 mg) 4 times daily

benzylpenicillin (penicillin G) IM or slow IV
25 000 IU/kg (15 mg/kg) (max. 1 MIU or 600 mg) every 6 hours

procaine benzylpenicillin IM (never by IV route)
50 000 IU/kg (50 mg/kg) once daily (max. 1.2 MIU or 1.2 g daily)

Supportive measures

– Monitor closely using an early warning system.
– Evaluate and manage respiratory distress or any airway obstruction. In case of stridor,
respiratory distress, or bull neck:
• Administer dexamethasone IV, 0.6 mg/kg loading dose followed by 0.1 mg/kg every
6 hours (max. 10 mg per dose).
• Consider nebulised epinephrine (adrenaline), 0.5 mg/kg (max. 5 mg) diluted with
0.9% NaCl up to a total volume of 4 to 5 mL, administered over 10 to 15 minutes.
• Intubation or tracheotomy may be necessary with severe upper airway obstruction
(only to be carried out by trained staff, follow local validated protocols). Administer
oxygen if airway obstruction is imminent4.
– Update vaccination status before hospital discharge. If the patient has been administered
DAT and can receive adequate home-based follow-up after hospital dischar ge, wait
3 weeks after administration of DAT before vaccination.

4.8.4 Complications

Complications in diphtheria are related to the direct effect of the toxin on affected tissues and
are more common in those with severe disease. They may occur acutely or several months
after infection with diphtheria.

Complications in children include:

– Cardiac complications: arrhythmias, heart failure and cardiogenic shock
– Thrombocytopaenia: causing epistaxis and upper respiratory tract bleeding
– Renal impairment
– Neurological disorders: dysphagia, limb weakness, neuropathy

For management of complications, see Diphtheria complications or local validated protocols.

4.8.3 Prevention

– Vaccination prevents severe disease though it does not prevent transmission.
– Clinical disease does not confer immunity, therefore vaccination should be part of case
management.
– Diphtheria vaccine is part of routine childhood immunisation (EPI):
• A primary series of 3 doses of DTP-containing vaccinec, with the first dose at 6 weeks
of age and subsequent doses at 4 weekly intervals.
• 3 booster doses of diphtheria toxoid-containing vaccine during childhood: between
12 and 23 months, between 4 and 7 years, and between 9 and 15 years5.
– Catch-up vaccination (missed routine vaccination):
• A primary series of 3 doses of diphtheria toxoid-containing vaccine (following a 0, 1 and
6 month schedule), followed by 2 booster doses with an interval of at least 1 year apart.
