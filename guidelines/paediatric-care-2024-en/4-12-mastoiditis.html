4.12 Mastoiditis

Suppurative infection in the mastoid cavities that is usually a secondary complication of acute
otitis media (AOM). Like AOM, it is most common in children under 2 years of age1. Often
there is a history of AOM or recurrent AOM, though mastoiditis can be the first presentation
of AOM. If severe and untreated, it may lead to life-threatening complications.

Common pathogens are Streptococcus pneumoniae and Streptococcus pyogenes (group
A streptococcus), while Haemophilus influenza B and Staphylococcus aureus are found less
commonly2,3.

4.12.1 Clinical features

Symptoms are similar to AOM, with fever, irritability, ear pain, and an abnormal tympanic
membrane on otoscopy.

Diagnosis is usually based on the presence of typical symptoms and the following physical
findings:
– The affected ear appears like it is sticking out or protruding.
– The area behind the ear is swollen, tender, and erythematous and feels like there is a
fluctuant mass. A draining fistula may be present.

Rarely, the first presentation of mastoiditis is a complication due to spread of infection into
intra- or extra-cranial spaces, including:

– Meningitis
– Intracranial abscess
– Facial nerve paralysis
– Hearing loss
– Labyrinthitis
– Osteomyelitis

4.12.2 Management

Management is usually conservative in the absence of complications. Surgical interventions
are reserved for complicated cases or those not responding to initial treatment 4,5.

– Clean the ear canal: wipe any drainage with a cotton swab or clean tissue or gauze.
– Treat fever and pain (see Chapter 3, Section 3.1.3: Management of fever, and Chapter 15,

Section 15.4: Pain management).
– Admit to hospital for IV antibiotic treatment:
• First line: ceftriaxone IV, 80 mg/kg (max. 4 g) every 24 hoursa.

a Increase dose of ceftriaxone to 100 mg/kg if evidence of meningeal involvement.
