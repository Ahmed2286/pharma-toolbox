4.5 Pneumonia

Pneumonia is an acute infection of the lower respiratory airways, resulting in inflammation,
fluid or pus in the alveoli and alveolar walls. This causes cough, fever, and difficulty in breathing.
Pneumonia remains one of the main causes of mortality in children under 5, accounting for
14% of worldwide under-5 mortality in 20191.

Aetiology

Pneumonia in children is most commonly community acquired. Infectious organisms can be
viral, bacterial, fungal or parasitic. Common pathogens vary widely by context, region, age, and
underlying clinical conditions (e.g. severe acute malnutrition (SAM), HIV infection, sickle cell
anaemia). Immunisation with H. influenzae type B (Hib) and pneumococcal conjugate vaccines
have reduced pneumonia where vaccine coverage is high, however the following infections
still impact millions of children worldwide2,3:

– Bacterial: Hib, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Salmonella spp., Klebsiella pneumonia,
Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pyogenes, Mycobacterium tuberculosis
– Viral: respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), influenza A and B, adenoviruses, parainfluenza,
measles, rhinovirus, coronavirus, human metapneumovirus
– Atypical bacteria: Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Legionella
pneumophila (common in older children)
– Opportunistic fungal: Pneumocystis jirovecii (in HIV infected), Aspergillus spp
– Aspiration pneumonia caused by oral anaerobic flora

Non-infectious causes include foreign body inhalation, acid or other toxic fluid aspiration, or
exposure to chemical toxins.

Diagnosis is based on history, clinical features and examination, and where available, a chest
x-ray and/or lung ultrasound may be supportive. Assess the severity of pneumonia and
respiratory signs using the CRS (see Table 4.1 and Appendix 7: Clinical Respiratory Score (CRS))
to promptly provide respiratory support if needed and administer antibiotic treatment.

4.5.1 Clinical features

Common signs and symptoms of typical pneumonia include:

– Cough: can vary from very mild to deep/chesty and may be productive of green or yellow
coloured sputum, or even blood-stained sputum.
– Difficulty in breathing: may present as tachypnoea, dyspnoea, cyanosis/hypoxia,
respiratory distress.
– Fever: is often high (≥ 39 °C), but may be low-grade or absent.
– Infants and younger children often present also with difficulty in feeding, restlessness or
irritability, vomiting and diarrhoea.
– Older children may complain of chest pain on deep breathing or coughing (pleuritic chest
pain) and occasionally abdominal pain.
– Children with SAM or underlying immunocompromise (HIV infection) are at high risk of
developing pneumonia but may not elicit a cough or fever.

– For persistent cough and fever for > 2 weeks duration, check for any other signs of TB, any

Atypical pneumonia (due to Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Legionella
pneumophila) has a slightly different presentation, with often an insidious onset of non-
specific symptoms. It is common in school-age and older children and should be considered in
special populations, such as children with sickle cell disease, where it can lead to acute chest
syndrome (see Chapter 10, Section 10.2: Sickle cell disease).

Symptoms include:

– Low grade fever
– Fatigue
– Malaise and myalgia
– Headache
– Non-productive cough: typically slow onset and gradually worsening, can last for several
weeks or even months

Examination

Perform a full clinical assessment and examination, including:

– Adequacy of oxygenation (vital signs, SpO2, cyanosis, neurological state/level
of consciousness).
– Level of respiratory distress (see Table 4.1), effort/exhaustion, need for respiratory
support.
– Percussion: dullness appears on the affected side.
– Auscultation: decreased breath sounds, bronchial breath sounds, inspiratory crackles
(crepitations) may appear over the affected lobe or lung.

Signs of severe pneumonia include4:

– Central cyanosis or oxygen saturations (SpO2) < 90% in room air
– Severe respiratory distress (see Table 4.1)
– Altered level of consciousness
– Inability to drink or breastfeed
– Vomiting everything (all foods and liquids)
– Appears severely ill, toxic or septic

Examine and/or investigate for any underlying causes including tuberculosis (see Section 4.11:
Tuberculosis), malnutrition, malaria (see Chapter 3, Section 3.4: Malaria). In children with
recurrent pneumonia or poor response to treatment, check for HIV infection.

4.5.2 Investigations

Pneumonia should be diagnosed clinically therefore investigations are not required to make
the diagnosis.

Depending on context, consider:

– Sickle cell disease screening in high prevalence contexts
– Malaria RDT in endemic areas
– TB screening using TB diagnostic algorithms (see Section 4.11: Tuberculosis)
– HIV testing in children with recurrent pneumonia, poor response to treatment or
malnutrition

In critically unwell children, check:

– Hb
– BGL
– CRP, if available
– Blood culture, if available (according to local protocol)

In children with signs of severity and/or who do not improve quickly with antibiotics, if clinical
deterioration or suspicion of complications (such as empyema/pleural effusion), check2:

– FBC
– Chest X-ray (CXR), or lung ultrasound (POCUS):

• There is significant overlap in radiographic findings between different aetiologies of
pneumonia (e.g. viral vs bacterial pneumonia), and findings are often non-specific.
• A lobar pneumonia (homogeneous consolidation of an entire lobe of lung) is most
commonly bacterial (see Figure 4.2 below).
• Note that immunocompromised children may have normal chest radiographs despite

4.5.3 Management

Indications to admit for hospitalisation:

– Less than 6 months of age
– Clinically severe pneumonia
– Severe acute malnutrition
– Concerns regarding the ability of the parents/carers to treat or if the parents/carers cannot
bring the child back for a follow-up examination

Children older than 6 months can be managed at home as follows5:

– Give oral amoxicillin 30 mg/kg (max. 1 g) 3 times daily.
– If suspected aspirationb, give oral amoxicillin/clavulanic acid (ratio 7:1 or 8:1).
Dosage expressed in amoxicillin:

< 40 kg: 50 mg/kg (max. 1 g) 2 times daily

≥ 40 kg: Ratio 8:1: 3000 mg daily (2 tablets of 500/62.5 mg 3 times daily)


Ratio 7:1: 2625 mg daily (1 tablet of 875/125 mg 3 times daily)
– Treat for fever if the child is uncomfortable.
– Follow-up after 48 to 72 hours and assess clinical condition:
• Improvement: complete oral antibiotics for a total of 5 days.
• No improvement: continue oral antibiotics for a total of 5 days and add azithromycin PO:
10 mg/kg (max. 500 mg) once daily for 5 days.
• Clinically worse: admit for further investigations and management.

Infants younger than 6 months should be admitted for treatment with oral amoxicillin
(as above). Monitor for 48 hours to ensure good response to antibiotics before discharge home
to complete 5 days of treatment. If any deterioration, treat as severe pneumonia.

Severe pneumonia

– Assess and manage ABCDE (see Chapter 2, Section 2.2: Circulatory impairment and shock)
and admit for close observation, ideally to an ICU, if available.
– Administer oxygen via face mask, aiming for SpO2 between 94 - 98%.
– If necessary, increase level of respiratory support (see Section 4.1.3: Approach to a child
with a respiratory complaint; Respiratory support).
– Administer antibiotics (see following page).
– Treat fever and pain to improve comfort (see Chapter 15, Section 15.4: Pain management).
– Administer anti-malarial treatment if malaria test positive.
– Monitor and record vital signs and BGL as often as required using an early warning system
(see MSF Manual of Nursing Care Procedures, Assessment and vital signs, Charts: Vital sign
charts).

Antibiotic treatment
Severe pneumonia3

– Administer ampicillin (or amoxicillin) IV (or IM) 50 mg/kg (max. 1 g) every 8 hours AND

complication of measles (see below for specific treatment in measles).
– For children over 5 years old or if suspicion of atypical pneumonia in any age group, add
azithromycin PO 10 mg/kg (max. 500 mg) once daily for 5 days.

– Child clinically improvedc and tolerating oral intake: switch to PO amoxicillin/clavulanic acid
(ratio 7:1 or 8:1) to complete 7 days treatment. Dosage expressed in amoxicillin:

< 40 kg: 50 mg/kg (max. 1g) 2 times daily

≥ 40 kg: Ratio 8:1: 3000 mg daily (2 tablets of 500/62.5 mg 3 times daily)

– Administer ceftriaxone IV/IM (dose as above) AND metronidazole IV 10 mg/kg (max. 500

Fluid and nutritional intake support

– Ensure adequate fluid intake (monitor oral intake)
• If unable to drink sufficiently or showing signs of dehydration, support with oral
or nasogastric feeds (see Chapter 15, Section 15.5: Nutritional support for the critically
unwell child).
• If enteral feeding is not tolerated or the clinical condition is unstable, cautiously start
IV maintenance fluids (see Chapter 15, Section 15.2: Parenteral fluids), ensuring careful
monitoring of fluid balance. Fluid restriction should be considered for severe casesg.
• In the case of severe respiratory distress, nil-by-mouth for first 24 hours to avoid risk
of aspiration and start IV maintenance fluids as outlined above. Begin to give frequent
small feeds after 24 hours, as tolerated. Show parent/carer how to support child upright
at 30 degrees after feeds.

4.5.4 Complications

If the child has bacterial pneumonia (such as staphylococcal pneumonia), it can lead
to empyema (see Section 4.6: Empyema). Dehydration can also be a complication of severe
pneumonia if the child becomes too lethargic to drink.

4.5.5 Discharge criteria

Consider discharge from hospital when child is:

– Clinically improving, with no fever for at least 24 hours
– Able to tolerate oral intake
– Maintaining oxygen saturations ≥ 92% in room air
– Tolerating oral antibiotics
– Going home to an environment where completion of treatment can be assured
