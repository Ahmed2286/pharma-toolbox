Introduction

Introduction

Neonatal care is the medical care of neonates from the moment of birth throughout the first 28 days

of life. It encompasses essential preventive and supportive activities to advanced curative

interventions to reduce mortality in the neonatal period – the most vulnerable moment for a child’s

survival and health.

Despite achieving a nearly 60% reduction in child mortality since 1990 worldwide, 7.4 million children

and adolescents died in 2019 of mostly preventable or treatable conditions
. Over 70% of those

deaths occurred under the age of 5 years, and 47% of those under-5 deaths occurred in the neonatal

period. Currently, the risk of dying is highest in the first month of life, with neonatal deaths accounting

for 17 deaths per 1000 live births (around 2.4 million deaths) globally in 2019
. UNICEF reports that the

majority (73%) of neonatal deaths occur in the first week of life, and around 36% in the first 24

hours
. Almost all (99%) of these deaths occur in low and middle income countries with limited

resources
.

Prematurity, intra-partum related disorders, perinatal asphyxia and infections (especially sepsis,

meningitis and pneumonia) account for the majority of these largely preventable deaths. Preterm and

small for gestational age neonates are particularly at risk, with more than 80% of neonatal deaths in

sub-Saharan Africa occurring in this group . Neonates born to the youngest and oldest mothers,

those born to mothers living in rural areas and those born shortly after another sibling also carry a

higher risk of mortality
. In the areas with the highest neonatal mortality, fewer than half of all

neonates and mothers receive a postnatal health check.

Studies have shown that scaling up effective interventions targeting the major causes of neonatal

mortality could prevent an estimated 71% of neonatal deaths
. The highest impact comes from

facility-based care, which although more expensive than community-based methods, accounts for

82% of this effect. The greatest effect on neonatal deaths derives from interventions delivered

through care during labour and birth plus immediate neonatal care (41%), followed by care of small and

sick neonates (30%). Wider coverage and better quality preconception, antenatal, intrapartum and

postpartum care could be provided at relatively low-cost
.

This guide has been developed to address the growing need for a comprehensive, standardised

approach to neonatal care. Most treatments proposed are relatively simple, but require meticulous

monitoring and attention to detail to be effective.

Neonatal care in resource-limited contexts is an area that is growing and advancing as new research

and resources become available. As such, these guidelines may require adaptation from the time of

writing.

About this guidelines

Primary target readers: general clinicians/physicians, clinical officers, and nursing staff caring for

neonates from delivery rooms to maternity to neonatal care units.

Neonate: defined as a child from birth up to and including 28 days of life.

Temperature: all references to temperature apply to axillary (or tympanic) temperature. References to

rectal or central temperature are not included in this guideline. The normal axillary temperature range

for a neonate in this guideline is between 36 °C and 37 °C. Normal core temperature range in neonates

is 36.5 to 37.5 °C. Axillary readings do not measure core temperature. Core temperature can be

measured with a rectal reading however this is not recommended in MSF settings as inappropriate use

or inadequate sterilisation between usages may lead to other complications.

1.1 Prepare for birth

Most neonates will start to breathe and cry spontaneously soon after birth. However, between 5 and
10% of neonates require some level of additional help to start breathing efficiently. Around 3 to 6% of

neonates need basic resuscitation at birth including ventilation .

The safest place for a neonate to be born is in a healthcare facility with a skilled birth attendant .

For every birth, anticipate that resuscitation may be needed and be prepared.

Ensure trained staff and essential medical items are available for resuscitation and for routine care at

birth (in the delivery room):

1.3.2 Apgar score

Table 1.3 - Apgar score

Consists of skin-to-skin care, noting the Apgar score, cord care, preventive treatments, top-to-

toe examination and assessment of risk factors.
Aim to complete routine care in the delivery room within the first 2 hours of birth.

It is important to keep the neonate warm while providing all care. Most of the care should be

delivered while keeping the neonate skin-to-skin with the mother.

Keep skin-to-skin contact with the neonate directly on the mother’s bare chest, both covered with

a blanket or cloth for at least an hour or until after the first feed . This is beneficial for both the

mother and the neonate, supporting temperature regulation and stimulating early breastfeeding.
Place a hat on the neonate’s head.

[1]

Exclusive breastfeeding is recommended for all neonates. Place the neonate to the breast as soon

as possible within 1 hour of birth.

Breastfeeding is recommended even if the mother is infected with HIV (see Chapter 6, Section 6.9).

Evaluate the Apgar score at 1, 5 and 10 minutes after birth. The score helps assess the neonate’s

condition immediately after birth, to identify if respiratory support is needed, and the response to

any resuscitative measures
.
[2], [3]

Evaluate the score for skin colour, respiration, heart rate, muscle tone and responsiveness and

calculate the total score out of 10.

If necessary, it can be done retrospectively (e.g. if resuscitation was required).

1.3.5 Examination and assessment of risk factors

The cord will normally fall off at about 1 week of age (5 to 15 days). If an umbilical granuloma

remains and there are no signs of infection, silver nitrate can be applied (at health centre level) one
or two times per week by trained staff.

Apply 1% tetracycline eye ointment once into both eyes as soon as possible, preferably within 1

hour of birth.

Neonates are born with a low level of vitamin K which is needed for clotting.

Administer vitamin K injection to reduce the risk of haemorrhagic disease of the newborn.
Can be given intravenously for sick neonates who have an intravenous catheter.

phytomenadione (vitamin K) IM anterolateral thigh within first few hours of life:

< 1500 g: 0.5 mg single dose (= 0.05 mL of a 2 mg/0.2 mL vial)
≥ 1500 g: 1 mg single dose (= 0.1 mL of a 2 mg/0.2 mL vial)

Administer one dose (10 micrograms) of monovalent hepatitis B vaccine as IM injection into

anterolateral thigh.

To optimise prevention of transmission of hepatitis B virus from mother to neonate, administer as

soon as possible in the delivery room (or ideally within the first 12 hours of life) .
b

A late birth dose (> 12 hours after birth), is progressively less effective the later it is given, but it may

still prevent horizontal transmission and remains beneficial .
c

In neonates with a birth weight of < 2000 g and confirmed maternal HBsAg negative, it is

recommended to postpone the vaccine until pre-discharge.

If available and indicated by national guidelines, hepatitis B immunoglobulin may be recommended
for neonates born to mothers with known positive HBsAg (see Chapter 6, Section 6.8).

Risk factors for hypoglycaemia

Table 1.4 - Risk factors for neonatal hypoglycaemia after birth

Examine the neonate from ‘head-to-toe’ under an infant warmer to check for any signs of illness,

physical abnormality or birth injuries. This should be done soon after birth in the delivery room by the

birth attendant. If the neonate is born with no risk factors and good Apgar scores, in order to

maximise skin-to-skin care for at least 2 hours, examination could be delayed for these well
neonates until 2 hours after birth.

Assess for any specific risk factors for hypoglycaemia (Table 1.4) and sepsis (Table 1.5), and note

any risks related to the mother, pregnancy, labour and delivery:

Maternal illness or problems during pregnancy: tuberculosis, HIV, syphilis, hepatitis, pre-

eclampsia, diabetes.
Labour and delivery: duration of rupture of membrane, antibiotics received by the mother,
appearance of amniotic fluid, type of delivery, need for resuscitation and Apgar score.

If any abnormal signs, injuries or risk factors are identified, contact the clinician or health provider
responsible for neonatal care.

If any one of the risk factors for hypoglycaemia is present (from Table 1.4), check blood glucose
level (BGL) within one hour of birth.

If BGL < 45 mg/dL or < 2.5 mmol/L:

Call the clinician.
Feed immediately with breast milk, or if that is not possible, give 5 mL/kg 10% glucose solution

orally. Repeat BGL after 30 minutes. See Section 1.6.4 for further management on postnatal
ward.
If BGL < 35 mg/dL or < 2 mmol/L, or neonate is symptomatic, treat as a medical emergency

(see Chapter 3, Section 3.4) and transfer to neonatal care unit.
