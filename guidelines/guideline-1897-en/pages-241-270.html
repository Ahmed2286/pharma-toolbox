The skin of a term neonate is not considered mature until 3 weeks have passed; a preterm neonate

The goals of skin care include: maintaining skin integrity, reduce traumatic injury and minimize

Assessment of neonatal skin should be undertaken upon admission, prior to transfer, after
prolonged procedures, and at least every shift or more frequently as clinically indicated (e.g. in

Describe and measure any abnormalities in the condition of a neonate’s skin in order to monitor

11.3.7 Pressure ulcers

A pressure ulcer is a lesion on the skin and/or the underlying tissue over a bony prominence resulting
from pressure, or pressure in combination with shear forces. It also appears on soft tissues subjected
to external pressure by different materials or medical devices. Hospitalized neonates are particularly
susceptible and therefore risk assessment and early identification of skin complications is a routine
part of nursing care.

Interventions and close monitoring are needed if a neonate scores a single score of 3 in one area
or a combined score of 6 and above.

Dryness: use emollients on affected parts to rehydrate the skin and respect recommendations
on skin care. Can be applied at least every 12 hours.
Erythema: inspection by clinician is necessary to rule out infectious diseases or allergic
reactions to medications.
Breakdown: apply dressing as required according to assessment and localisation of the wound.
The NSCS does not replace a daily head-to-toe examination.

Daily head-to-toe assessment with particular focus on high-risk areas (including occipital region
and ears, nasal septum, nostrils, cheeks, back of the head, fingers and feet).
Ensure that neonates who are at risk of developing a pressure ulcer are repositioned at least every
4 hours as part of their cluster care. For those neonates at higher risk, repositioning should be
considered even more frequently and documented.
Medical device-related pressure ulcers are the most common in neonates. Monitor the skin with
therapeutic and diagnostic devices and rotate sites regularly.
Choose the correct size (neonatal/preterm sensors, neonatal oxygen prongs). Rotate the pulse
oximeter sensor probe site at least every 3 to 4 hours (if on continuous monitoring).
Protective padding (hydrocolloid dressing or foam dressing) should be placed under devices (such
as NGT/OGT, oxygen prongs). This is to avoid friction, is gentle on skin and avoids pulling on hair.
Adhesive removal is the primary cause of skin breakdown in neonates and may be painful. Limit
changing adhesives when possible.

Use proper application technique: no strapping (application under tension) and no use of bonding
agents. Apply tape to the smallest possible area of the skin to secure monitoring

devices/attach a dressing.
Avoid removing adhesives until at least 24 hours after application. Remove adhesives
horizontally using warm sterile water or saline-soaked cotton balls/compresses.
Insert and secure an IV catheter using aseptic technique. Secure with a sterile dressing such as
steri-strips and cover with a transparent film dressing. Cutting a corner of sterile gauze and placing
it under the hub and wings of the device will reduce the pressure on the skin. Sites must be visible,
secure and assessed hourly with continuous infusions/medications for signs of infiltration.
Neonates who are clinically unstable and do not tolerate repositioning can be ‘micro-positioned’
(small movements to reposition).
Positional changes and special pressure relieving mattresses have been shown to decrease
pressure, friction and shear on fragile neonatal skin.

Appendix 3. Breastfeeding and alternative feeding techniques

3.1 WHO Ten Steps to Successful Breastfeeding (2018)

Critical management procedures

1. a. Comply fully with the International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes and relevant
World Health Assembly resolutions.

b. Have a written infant feeding policy that is routinely communicated to staff and parents.
c. Establish ongoing monitoring and data-management systems.
2. Ensure that staff have sufficient knowledge, competence and skills to support breastfeeding.

Key clinical practices

3. Discuss the importance and management of breastfeeding with pregnant women and their
families.
4. Facilitate immediate and uninterrupted skin-to-skin contact and support mothers to initiate
breastfeeding as soon as possible after birth.
5. Support mothers to initiate and maintain breastfeeding and manage common difficulties.

6. Do not provide breastfed newborns any food or fluids other than breast milk, unless medically
indicated.
7. Enable mothers and their infants to remain together and to practice rooming-in 24 hours a day.
8. Support mothers to recognize and respond to their infants’ cues for feeding.
9. Counsel mothers on the use and risks of feeding bottles, teats and pacifiers.
10. Coordinate discharge so that parents and their infants have timely access to ongoing support
and care.

3.2 Alternative feeding techniques

3.2.1 Expressing and storing breast milk

Indications

Neonates who are unwell or suffered trauma during delivery, and some neonates who are preterm or
low birth weight may not be able to feed directly from the breast. In these situations it is helpful for the
mother to express her milk to build up and maintain supply for when the neonate is able to start
breastfeeding. Milk is expressed every 2 to 3 hours.

[1]

Page 254
/ 317

Hand expression is an alternative when breast pumps are unavailable. Show the mother the technique.
Give her a clean cup or container for collecting the milk. The container should be washed, rinsed with
boiled water and air-dried before each use.

Technique

Storage

Feed the neonate immediately after expressing the breasts (by cup and spoon, oro/nasogastric tube
or the double suction technique). If the neonate does not take all of the collected milk, it can be stored
in a clean and sealed container (labelled with mother’s name, date, and time of expression), at room
temperature for up to 4 hours if the ambient temperature is < 22 °C or up to 1 hour if the ambient
temperature is ≥ 22 °C. If there is the possibility of using a refrigerator (2 to 8 °C), milk can be stored
for up to 24 hours. Warm the milk (using a water bath) to room temperature before feeding.

3.2.2 Feeding using a cup or spoon

Indications

Expressed milk can be administered using a cup, spoon, paladai or syringe. It is used in situations where
neonates can swallow effectively but are not able to feed directly from the breast, either because
they have a weak suck reflex, they are unable to feed for long enough or they are unable to take a
sufficient quantity. This method can be combined with the double suction method or oro/nasogastric
feeding.

Figure 1 - Feeding a neonate by cup

Technique

Wash hands, sit comfortably and hold the container under the breast.
With the other hand, hold the breast up with 4 fingers and place the thumb above the areola.
Squeeze the areola between the thumb and the fingers while pressing backward toward the rib
cage. This should not hurt – if it hurts the technique is wrong. Compress all the way around the
breast with the finger and thumb the same distance from the nipple.
Express each breast until the milk drips slowly. Repeat expressing from each breast 5 to 6 times.
Stop expressing when the milk drips slowly from the start of compression and does not flow.
Avoid rubbing or sliding fingers along the skin and pinching or squeezing the nipple.

3.2.3 Supplementary nursing technique

Indications

This technique is used to maintain breastfeeding when milk production is less than the daily amount
needed by the neonate, or in neonates who have difficulty suckling at the breast. The neonate receives
a temporary supplement in the form of expressed breast milk or formula. The neonate suckles and
stimulates the breast at the same time drawing supplement from the tube and is thereby nourished and
satisfied.

Figure 2 - Feeding using a supplementary line

This technique usually needs to be practiced under supervision in a health facility.

Technique

Any clean, open container can be used (cup or container that has been washed in boiling water and
air dried). Use clean containers and utensils for each feed.
Allow the mother to feed her neonate unless she is not available.
Measure the volume of expressed breast milk in the cup (for volume of EBM, see Chapter 2,
Section 2.2.4).
Hold the neonate while sitting down, semi-upright, resting on the mother’s knees.
Gently position the cup on the neonate’s lower lip and touch the outside of the neonate’s lip with
the rim of the cup/spoon.
Tilt the cup/spoon so the milk just touches the neonate’s upper lip.
Allow the neonate to drink the milk at their own pace. Do NOT pour milk into the neonate’s mouth
or try to speed it up.
Stop the feed when the neonate closes their mouth and is no longer interested in feeding.

If the neonate coughs or regurgitates multiple times when feeding using a cup or spoon or is not
able to take a sufficient quantity, it means he/she does not yet have a sufficient swallow reflex. In
this case, the neonate should be fed using an oro/nasogastric tube and reassessed a later time.
Use of infant teats or bottles are not encouraged in most resource-limited settings due to high risk
of contamination and limited ability to adequately sterilise them. Cups and spoons are easy to
clean and can be used from birth, even in LBW neonates.

The neonate may need 2 to 3 days to adjust to the technique. If, for the first few days, the neonate
does not take all the milk in the cup, give the rest with a cup or spoon.

Before each feed

Appendix 4. Fortification of breast milk with breast milk

Indication

Breast milk fortifier (BMF) should be added to all feeds for neonates:

If breast milk amounts are not adequate, attention should be given to increasing maternal breast milk
production.

The product: Nutrilon BMF from Nutricia

The BMF comes in 2.2 g sachets. The correct way of fortifying the expressed breast milk is producing
a maximum 4% solution:

When the neonate is taking 80 to 100 mL/kg/day of breast milk and tolerating it well, add the fortifier
as a 2% solution for all feeds.
After 48 hours of good tolerance, add fortifier as a 4% solution for all feeds.
Continue to increase milk up to 160 to 180/mL/kg/day as long as the neonate tolerates the volume
well.

Administration rules

Procedure for mixing BMF with expressed breast milk

With a birth weight < 2000 g AND
Who are at least 2 weeks of age AND
Receive all their fluid needs by enteral intake and at least partially by alternative feeding methods

(cup/spoon, double suctioning, tube feeding) AND
Who are gaining less than 15 g/kg/day on three consecutive days on adequate amounts of breast
milk.

Add one sachet (2.2 g) of BMF to 50 mL of expressed breast milk to make a 4% solution; or
Add half a sachet (1.1 g) of BMF to 50 mL of expressed breast milk to make a 2% solution; or
Add half a sachet (1.1 g) of BMF to 25 mL of expressed breast milk to make a 4% solution.

BMF is restricted to hospital use. Do not give to mothers to administer at home.
BMF is added to the expressed breast milk directly before the feed.
Mixing fortifier and milk is done by a nurse or nurse assistant, respecting standard hygiene practices
of milk preparation.

Monitoring

If the neonate shows signs of intestinal intolerance (painful and swollen abdomen, vomiting), stop

Duration of fortification

Appendix 7. Fluid volume tables

Parenteral fluids are indicated in sick neonates unable to take adequate amounts of breast milk (or
need to be nil-by-mouth) and in VLBW neonates. The following tables provide a guide for daily IV fluid
and breast milk feed volumes by weight range. Calculations are rounded figures and based on the mid-
point of the weight range, e.g. for birth weight 2500 g to < 3000 g, the volumes are calculated at 2750
g. Note that the weight is in kilograms.

The parenteral fluid of choice for D1 and D2 is 10% glucose (G10%). From D3 the fluid of choice is

1/5 0.9% sodium chloride (NaCl 0.9%) + 4/5 10% glucose (G10%) .

Breast milk (BM) feeds should be increased daily, as tolerated, and IV fluids reduced accordingly,
ensuring the total daily fluid volume is adjusted to age and weight. The aim is to reach 160 mL/kg/day,
or up to 180 mL/kg/day for LBW neonates.

If the neonate is not tolerating the prescribed amount of breast milk, reduce it and increase the IV
fluids accordingly. Do not exceed 150 mL/kg/day of IV fluids.

a

For D1, administer the total daily volume as IV fluids. Give a small amount of breast milk (15
mL/kg/day) to allow the neonate to be stabilised and monitor tolerance carefully. This small amount
of breast milk on D1 is not counted within the total daily volume.
If tolerating well, start on D2 with 30 mL/kg/day of breast milk, increasing daily by 30 mL/kg/day
while monitoring tolerance and appetite.

For D1 and D2, administer the total daily volume as a continuous IV infusion (10% glucose).
If the neonate is stable (i.e. no severe respiratory distress or shock), give trophic feeds of 10-15
mL/kg/day of breast milk on D1 and D2. Give feeds every 2 hours (i.e. 12 feeds/day). Trophic feeds
are not counted in the total daily volume.
On D3, switch type of IV fluids and start breast milk feeds at 30 mL/kg/day, increasing daily by 30
mL/kg/day. Continue to give breast milk feeds every 2 hours. Breast milk volume is calculated as
part of the total daily volume with IV fluids.
Stop IV fluids when full enteral feeds (180 mL/kg/day) is reached.
If tolerating full enteral feeds, change frequency of feeds to every 3 hours (i.e. 8 feeds/day).
Monitor tolerance and weight.
