2.1.3 When to consider palliative care

Depending on the context, palliative care may be recommended for extremely low birth weight or
extreme prematurity. For example, an extremely preterm neonate (< 28 weeks gestation) has a 90%
chance of survival in a high-resource setting while this is reduced to only 10% in countries with limited
resources. The likelihood of survival needs to be taken into consideration in some contexts to choose

the best adapted care for the neonate. For palliative care, see Chapter 10, Section 10.5).

2.2 Overview of caring for LBW neonates

2.2 Overview of caring for LBW neonates

Aim to keep neonates together with the mothers where possible. The clinical condition, birth weight,
GA and set up of neonatal care services and staffing, should all be considered when deciding the best
place to provide care for the LBW neonate. Refer to Chapter 1, Section 1.5 for Admission criteria for
postnatal care.

All neonates who show any danger signs, who are clinically unwell, with a birth weight < 2000 g or GA <
34 weeks, should be cared for in a neonatal care unit. If there is a Kangaroo Mother Care area within
the neonatal care unit, neonates with a birth weight of 1500 to 2000 g (or GA ≥ 32 to < 34 weeks) and
in a clinically stable condition can be cared for there. Clinically well neonates with a birth weight

between 2000 and 2500 g can usually be cared for in the maternity, provided that blood glucose level
(BGL) monitoring is feasible and there are no concerns with breastfeeding.

Regardless of where these neonates are cared for, close collaboration between maternity and the

neonatal care team is essential for the management of LBW neonates.

The level of special or intensive care of LBW neonates will vary with the level of support they require
and complications that may need to be treated.

Specific considerations:

Kangaroo mother care (KMC) is a method of caring for neonates that involves putting them on the

It is important to provide support to parents and families to encourage KMC to be carried out as often

2.2.4 Feeding and fluids support

LBW and preterm neonates may need support with establishing feeding, whether in maternity, KMC or
neonatal care unit. At < 34 weeks gestation, there may still be difficulties coordinating sucking and
swallowing, even if the suck reflex is present. Support with breastfeeding may be needed, and if unable
to breastfeed effectively, alternative methods can be used to support adequate breast milk intake.

VLBW/very preterm and sick LBW neonates may not tolerate advancing too quickly with enteral
feeds; it is recommended to start them on a combination of IV fluids and enteral feeds. VLBW
neonates require a higher intake of fluids (per kg body weight), largely due to increased insensible fluid
loss through their skin. However, volumes must be carefully prescribed as very small volumes of fluid
can be significant in respect to weight in VLBW neonates. See Appendix 7 for detailed instructions and
fluid and feed volume tables by weight range, and refer also to Chapter 10, Section 10.3.1 regarding

administration of and types of parenteral fluids.

Poor weight gain

It is normal for preterm neonates to lose up to < 15% of their birth weight in the first days of life,
however weight gain of around 15 to 20 grams/kg/day should start around D3 to D5. Birth weight
should be regained at around 15 days of life.

As LBW and VLBW neonates have a high demand of nutrients for normal growth, some may not gain
appropriate weight on breast milk alone. VLBW neonates with poor weight gain should receive EBM
fortified with a breast milk fortifier (BMF). The fortifier adds protein, calories and micronutrients.

BMF is indicated if the birth weight was < 2000 g, and the neonate is at least 2 weeks of age, is on
enteral feeds only (and partially supported by alternative methods), and has been gaining < 15 g/kg/day
for 3 consecutive days despite adequate volumes of breast milk.

2.3.3 Chronic lung disease

Also known as bronchopulmonary dysplasia, chronic lung disease presents mostly in preterm or LBW
neonates with chronic respiratory distress or a prolonged need for respiratory support such as oxygen
therapy. It is the most common adverse outcome in neonates born at < 30 weeks gestation, and

neonates who required oxygen therapy and/or ventilation . Ensuring oxygen therapy is given with
attention to keeping oxygen saturations within target range and limiting mechanical ventilation duration

helps to reduce the risk of developing chronic lung disease
. However, prematurity of the lungs and
other underlying conditions such as respiratory distress syndrome, patent ductus arteriosus and

neonatal sepsis may also contribute.

Symptoms will improve gradually as the neonate gets older. Support oxygen therapy and note that
weaning off oxygen will take longer.

2.4 Cardiac complications

2.4 Cardiac complications

Preterm neonates with cardiac complications have a higher mortality than a term neonate with the
same cardiac problem. Being preterm also increases the incidence of several cardiac problems, of
which the most common is a patent ductus arteriosus (PDA).

2.4.1 Patent ductus arteriosus

The ductus arteriosus (DA) is a central vascular duct between the main pulmonary artery and the aorta,
allowing oxygenated blood from the placenta to bypass the nonfunctional lungs and enter the
systemic circulation in the foetus. At birth, one of the essential steps of transition to life ex-utero is the
closure of the DA.

A PDA is when the duct fails to completely close and remains partially patent or ‘open’. The smaller the
birth weight or gestational age, the higher the risk of PDA after birth. Neonates born > 30 weeks
gestational age usually have a smaller PDA which most likely will close spontaneously prior to
discharge home. However, for neonates born at < 30 weeks, complete closure may be significantly
delayed, particularly if the PDA is of moderate or large size. VLBW neonates account for 30% of

PDAs
.

2.5.1 Feed intolerance

Inability to digest enteral feeds without complications such as intrapulmonary aspiration of feeds,

2.5.2 Necrotising enterocolitis

Necrotising enterocolitis (NEC) is an inflammatory condition of the intestinal tract. It consists of a
vascular component in which there is ischaemia and necrosis of intestinal walls, and an infectious
component attributed to multiple pathogens. It is a serious and potentially life-threatening condition
which requires urgent management.

NEC is more common in LBW and preterm neonates. Other risk factors include hypoxia/perinatal
asphyxia, formula feeding, intestinal infection or sepsis, polycythaemia/hyperviscosity
(transfusion/dehydration).

Clinical features

Vomiting, abdominal distension, visible bowel loops and diarrhoea.

Gastric residual volumes of more than half of the prior feed volume and bilious gastric residuals are
considered significant (see Appendix 5).

Withhold the feed. Feeds may need to be advanced more slowly. Adjust IV fluids accordingly, if
necessary.

2.7 Anaemia of prematurity

2.7 Anaemia of prematurity

All neonates have a fall in haemoglobin soon after birth. In preterm neonates, this response occurs
earlier, is more pronounced and is termed anaemia of prematurity (AOP). The primary cause of AOP is

decreased production of erythropoietin (EPO) in the setting of anaemia. A reduced red blood cell life
span and low iron stores also contribute.

Clinical features

Normally occurs 3 to 12 weeks after birth in neonates < 32 weeks gestation. Many neonates remain
asymptomatic despite haemoglobin levels as low as 7 g/dL. Clinical signs include tachycardia, poor

weight gain, increased oxygen requirement, and increased episodes of apnoea/bradycardia.

Management

Prevention

Measures include iron supplementation during pregnancy, delayed clamping of the umbilical cord,
limiting the amount of blood sampled for investigations and iron supplementation.

Iron supplementation: all neonates < 2500 g who are breastfed should commence on iron

supplementation at 4 to 6 weeks of age and continue until 6 months of age.
