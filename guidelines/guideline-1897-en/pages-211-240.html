9.9 Hip dysplasia

9.9 Hip dysplasia

An abnormality of the hip joint, where the head of the femur is not ‘stable’ in the acetabulum or can
even be dislocated or easily ‘dislocatable’. It may also be referred to as congenital dislocation of the
hip or developmental dysplasia of the hip. If the abnormality is not detected early and managed
properly, the affected limb eventually becomes shorter and the hip may become painful. Risk factors

include breech position, multiple pregnancy, family history, and can also be acquired through swaddling
practices.

Clinical features

Examine using Ortolani-Barlow manoeuvres:
Neonate should be lying supine on a bed with hips and knees flexed to 90°.

Ortolani’s: place the forefinger on the outside and the thumb on the inside of the hip with the knees
flexed. Rotate the hips outwards while pushing towards the mattress on the head of the femur.
Ortolani test is positive if a ‘clunk’ is felt and represents a dislocated hip.

Barlow’s: the hip is returned to the starting position and then slightly adducted (knee drawn across the
body). With the fingers in the same position, push the hips backwards into the neonate’s cot. Barlow
test is positive if a ‘click’ is felt and represents a dislocatable hip.

Investigations

In neonates, x-rays are not usually helpful due to immature ossification of the hip joint. Where available
facilities and expertise exist, patients with identified risk factors are usually followed up with ultrasound
(after 6 weeks of age).

Management

In milder cases, keep the hip in flexion and abduction through double nappies or an abduction brace in
the abducted position for 2 to 3 months. The cultural practice of carrying the neonate on the back with
the hip flexed and abducted serves the same purpose.

In more severe cases, keep the hip flexed and abducted in a splint. Depending on the context, seek
options for referral to an experienced paediatric surgeon.

One leg may be shorter than the other.
Asymmetric skin creases in the thigh and groin may be present.

10.1.4 CPAP

A simple and effective treatment for moderate or severe respiratory distress. CPAP maintains positive
end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) by applying constant gentle pressure to the airway to maintain
adequate functional residual capacity in the alveoli and prevent alveolar collapse (atelectasis). CPAP
can only be used in spontaneously breathing patients, since it delivers a continuous low pressure and
does not initiate breaths or provide any additional pressure support during inspiration.

CPAP should only be used where appropriately trained and sufficient HR, validated equipment, and an

approved protocol on the use of CPAP are available. For contexts where CPAP is available, follow
the local protocol.

Neonates who are admitted to a neonatal care unit because they are sick, very preterm or very low
birth weight will require parenteral fluid support. Administration is preferably intravenous (IV), however

10.4 Pain management

10.4 Pain management

Pain prevention and management are an integral part of neonatal care. Neonates, both term and
preterm, experience pain and should receive effective and safe pain relief. Exposure to prolonged or
repeated pain can have many adverse effects including increased duration of illness, increased
morbidity, long-term cognitive effects and development of an abnormal response to painful stimuli.

Acute pain: often related to diagnostic or therapeutic procedures such as tape removal, skin breaking
procedures (such as intravenous insertion or injections), suctioning or OGT/NGT insertion.

Established pain: occurs following surgery, inflammatory conditions or birth trauma.

Prolonged or disease-related pain: results from severe diseases such as necrotising enterocolitis
and meningitis.

10.4.1 Assessment of pain

Neonates cannot verbalise pain, so depend on carers to assess and manage signs of pain. The
frequency of pain assessment will depend on the neonate’s clinical condition and underlying diagnosis.
Every neonate within the neonatal care unit should be assessed for pain at least once per shift.

The following signs may indicate underlying pain in a neonate:

Neonatal Facial Coding System (NFCS)

Physiological indicators: changes in heart rate, respiratory rate, breathing pattern, oxygen
saturation, skin colour and palmar sweating.
Behavioural indicators:

Crying patterns, hand and body movements, increased muscle tone, change in sleep patterns,
behavioural state changes and consolability.

10.5 Palliative and end-of-life care

10.5 Palliative and end-of-life care

Neonatal palliative care is a family-centred approach to care for neonates with a life-

threatening/limiting illness and for their families. Palliative care can be provided alongside other medical
treatments or as the only care with no further curative treatments. The goal is to manage pain and
other physical symptoms while addressing the psychological, social, and spiritual problems of the
neonate’s family.

End-of-life care is the provision of supportive and palliative care according to the needs of patient and
family during the last phase of life. End-of-life care is provided for neonates with conditions in which
intensive therapy is not in the neonate’s and the family’s best interest. The aim is to provide comfort by
managing any pain and other symptoms such as difficulty in breathing and secretions as the end of life
nears. End-of-life care should be discussed openly and honestly with the parents/carers, and an end-
of-life plan clearly written with their involvement and with the support of a multidisciplinary team. The
plan can be adapted according to circumstances, but at each stage should involve both parents and

the multidisciplinary team. Ensure discussions with the family are held in a quiet, private space
.

Indications

Palliative care may be indicated with extreme prematurity, severe hypoxic ischaemic encephalopathy,
extensive congenital anomalies and severe, incurable diseases.

End-of-life care is indicated when all curative treatment is withdrawn or withheld. It is not an individual
decision, but reached through discussion with the parents/carers and the multidisciplinary team.

The following situations may be encountered:

Neonate not viable at birth and decision not to resuscitate

11.1 Nursing care responsibilities

11.1 Nursing care responsibilities

11.1.1 Introduction

Neonates are a vulnerable population and thus require a specialised approach to nursing care. Nurses
caring for neonates are responsible for their care from the moment of birth throughout the first month
of life, including the provision of essential care and support to families.

Sick and preterm neonates often require multiple nursing and medical interventions over 24 hours.
Cares, handling and interventions should be adapted and delivered following observation of a
neonate’s behaviour and physiological responses.

Specialised nursing skills are required in the assessment, monitoring and care of sick neonates.

Routine monitoring and recording include: vital signs (including thermoregulation), assessment of pain,
weight (as clinically indicated), blood sugar levels, oral and parenteral intake, output, positioning and
any assessments and interventions performed.

Using a family-centred approach, nurses work with the neonate, parents and loved ones. Providing
health education to the parents on neonatal care is integrated into every nursing task.

Specific nursing responsibilities include:

11.2 Developmentally supportive care

11.2 Developmentally supportive care

As preterm neonates are born too early, they are exposed to environmental factors such as noise and
light from which they would have been shielded if still in-utero. Being preterm, this exposure and
subsequent stress occurs during a critical period of brain development, which has been shown to be
associated with significant neurosensory impairments and can lead to cognitive and behavioural

problems later as young children . Developmentally supportive care involves adapting the environment
and caring for the neonate in a way that reduces this stress and allows for normal neurological
development. The following steps should be considered where neonatal care is provided, particularly
for preterm and sick neonates.

11.2.1 Healing environment

Sick and preterm neonates admitted into hospital are overwhelmed by sensory inputs. They are
exposed to painful procedures, excessive light, noise and handling, reduced ability to move, interrupted
sleep and separation from their parents. These factors have effects on the immature brain and may
contribute to altered development.

Measures should be taken to mitigate these risks, such as:

11.2.2 Cluster care

Sleep is essential in early preterm brain development. Patient care should be clustered (i.e. to provide
cares at the same time such as: vital signs, weighing, washing, changing bedding, procedures and tests
such as blood sampling), kept to a minimum, and opportunistic such as when the neonate is awake,
during diaper/ nappy changes, at feeding times. Pace according to the neonate’s cues, pausing when
they show signs of stress. Recognising and responding to their cues appropriately may help to
minimise their stress and is positive for their recovery and development.

11.2.3 Involving the parents and family

[1]

Noise reduction: alarms should be set at the quietest level and answered promptly. Ward rounds
and handovers done away from the neonate’s cot area. Avoid speaking over the neonate.
Light reduction: avoid overhead lighting and use indirect lighting. In case direct lighting is needed
(e.g. placing an IV line), cover the neonate’s eyes with a small and clean cloth/gauze during the
procedure.

Overall, involving both parents and family members in the routine care of neonates is encouraged as it

strengthens bonding with the neonate, and can also allow the mother to rest. However, no more than
one carer (or maximum both parents) should be present with the neonate in a neonatal care area at any
one time. The mother and neonate should be kept together and where possible, roomed in together to
promote breastfeeding and bonding. Separation and the neonate’s clinical condition has been shown
to increase anxiety and stress, which impacts brain development later in childhood.

The mother or carer present with the neonate can be involved in cares such as feeding, nappy changes
and bathing, and KMC or skin-to-skin care can be supported at times by the father (see Section
11.2.5). For mental health support for the parents or carer, refer to MSF Mental health and
psychosocial support guideline.

11.2.4 Positioning

The outside environment is extremely different compared to the confined uterus. Poor positioning can
lead to positional disorders, such as muscle imbalances and the development of a ‘flattened posture’
which has the potential to impact on musculoskeletal development. Correct positioning of neonates
may reduce these disorders without harmful effects. The other benefits of correct positioning are the
enhancement of neonatal comfort, reduction of stress and prevention of pressure ulcers.

Change position every 2 to 4 hours with routine care/cluster care, considering the neonate’s overall
medical and skin condition.
To reduce distress to the neonate, change their position slowly and holding them steadily with the
whole hand.
Touch gently and talk to the neonate before changing their position, giving them warning that
handling is about to occur.
Support the neonate's body as closely as possible to the position held in the uterus.
Provide “cloth boundaries” by using rolled up blankets to create a nest. Use of a ‘U-shape’ provides
boundaries to push against and minimises the effects of gravity, thereby promoting improved motor
behaviour and postural development.
Make sure the neonate is not placed on any tubing or cables (e.g. OGT/NGT, IV lines, oxygen
tubing, pulse oximetry cables).
In all positions, aim for:

Head in middle
Shoulder protracted (forward)
Face to midline
Hips towards midline
Knees flexed

Feet neutral
Support the head in-line with the main axis of the body with upper and lower limbs flexed.
Promote hand to mouth/face contact.
Where feasible, use a validated and reliable positioning assessment tool (e.g. Infant Positioning
Assessment Tool (IPAT)). IPAT should be used:

To ensure correct positioning practices.
As a training tool for correct positioning of neonates (bedside trainings, daily reminders).

11.3 Routine hygiene and skin care

11.3 Routine hygiene and skin care

This is a primary responsibility of nursing care. Preservation of skin integrity, prevention of the
development of skin disorders and education of parents are key nursing priorities in the care of
neonates.

11.3.1 Bathing

Involve the mother or carer present with the neonate.
Take the opportunity to observe the neonate’s behaviour, alertness and muscular activity.
Use standard precautions (gloves) when handling the neonate until blood and amniotic fluid are

removed. Delay the first wash until 24 hours after birth . If this is not possible for cultural reasons,
delay for at least 6 hours and until the neonate’s temperature and cardiorespiratory status have
remained stable for 2 to 4 hours. Axillary temperature should be 36 to 37 °C.

a

If neonate is visibly dirty (with blood and/or meconium): remove with warmed water and non-woven
compresses or cotton and complete with the first wash later.
Note that immersing the neonate into water increases the risk of hypothermia. Keep the wash to a
short duration (approximately 5 minutes).
Ensure a safe and a heated external environment, with room temperature at a minimum of 26 °C.

Avoid draughts and close the doors and windows in the room to minimize heat loss.

Fill a container with warm water, ensuring that both are clean and disinfected before use as per
IPC protocol.
Use non-woven compresses or cotton moistened with warm water to wash the neonate. Use
separate compresses for each area of the body.
Start with clean areas moving to dirtier areas (face, head, thorax/arms/hands, legs/feet, back,
genitals/peri-anal area).
Expose only the area being washed, keeping all other areas covered to maintain temperature.
Dry with clean, dry towel or fabric, or non-woven compresses, ensuring all skin folds are dried
thoroughly (armpits, groin, neck, between fingers and toes and behind ears).
Throw each used compress immediately into the appropriate waste bin accessible at the
bedside.
Observe skin and skin folds for any dryness, redness, rashes or any breakdown.
Clean and disinfect the container after use as per IPC protocol.
A complete wash does not need to be performed more frequently than every 48 hours.
Apply a moisturizing cream if skin is dry or cracked and/or to areas prone to breakdown.

Use an unperfumed product approved for use in neonates.
Do not apply soft yellow paraffin and/or liquid paraffin on neonates undergoing phototherapy.
There is a low but possible risk of causing burns to the skin when exposed to phototherapy.

11.3.4 Nappy care

The perineal area is prone to changes in the skin barrier causing skin irritation. Increased moisture,
prolonged contact with stool and urine irritants and an alkaline skin surface may contribute to skin
breakdown. Risk factors for perineal dermatitis (nappy rash) include frequent stooling, antibiotic use,
malabsorption, opiate withdrawal, abnormal sphincter tone.
Excess moisture on neonatal skin should be avoided, as it leads to maceration of the area increasing
the risk of frictional damage. To keep the skin in the perineal area dry and intact, and to prevent and
manage dermatitis and/or excoriation, nappy changes should occur immediately after passing stool or
at least every 3 to 4 hours.

Provide mouth care at least every 6 to 8 hours, preferably clustered together as part of the usual
care and/or prior to every feed for neonates receiving OGT/NGT feeds.
Soak a clean non-woven compress in fresh expressed breast milk (EBM), approx. 0.2 to 0.3 mL.

Use of fresh EBM is preferred. EBM is easily available, cost-free and hygienic (when expressed
correctly) and is the preferred substance for oral care due to the presence of maternal
antibodies and anti-inflammatory substances, protecting against disease and infection. In
addition to the prevention of infection, this also provides a positive oral experience, providing an
early taste of milk and stimulation of sucking reflex for babies who are not feeding orally.
Gently roll the wet compress along the neonate’s lips to remove any dry skin or debris. Use a
new compress around the gum line and over the tongue. The idea is to coat the mouth in a layer
of milk.
If there is no EBM available, sterile water can be used.
Invite and teach mothers/carers to perform mouth care, reaffirming to mothers the importance
of their milk.

Use warm water and disposable cotton/non-woven compress. Dry with gentle patting using
cotton/ non-woven compress.
