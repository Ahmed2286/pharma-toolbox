1 INTRODUCTION

Medical equipment helps save lives. It can also put lives at risk, reduce the quality of care,
waste significant operational funds, create a significant volume of hazardous waste and
cause endless frustration. The difference lies in how well we integrate these technologies
into our activities and our organisation.

This guideline provides practical advice for implementing a structured management of
medical equipment in MSF missions, and is aligned with the various policies relating to
medical equipment which have been created in the operational sections and MSF
International. Rather than giving detailed how-to information, it aims to provide broad
advice which can be implemented in different contexts.

It is intended for anyone involved in any way with medical equipment, regardless of
department or position.

Specific terms are used in this guideline, and their definitions are given in Annex §II.

OBJECTIVES

The objective of this guideline is to help all MSF missions successfully choose, use, care for
and maintain their medical equipment. It provides the fundamentals for assigning
responsibilities and for recruiting and training staff. It also aims to raise awareness of issues
related to selecting and installing equipment, workshop needs, planning and conducting
regular preventive maintenance, ensuring quality repairs, organising stocks and ordering and
planning in the event of equipment failure. Medical equipment directly affects the health
and safety of both patients and staff. We must ensure that appropriate standards of
equipment functioning and safety are maintained.

•
This equipment often becomes an essential part of MSF’s operational activities, and
we must ensure continuity and quality of service.
•
The cost of the equipment often represents a large investment, in most cases a
significant part of a mission’s budget.
•
Medical equipment creates significant volumes of waste, often in places with limited
recycling possibilities.

Medical equipment is increasingly being adopted in MSF missions with a view to increasing
the scope of our medical services and support the high quality of care we aim to provide.
Adding equipment drastically increases the complexity of management and the resources
needed (HR and budget): managing compatibility of accessories and consumables, staff
training needs and technical support – and failure to do so is regularly a contributing factor
in health-care associated infections and patient safety incidents. With this in mind we need
to be sure that the equipment we choose will have a positive effect on the patient
outcomes.

The objective for each mission/project must be to select equipment which is suited to the
needs of the mission and have the capacity to use and maintain it well. Once selected our
objectives must be to achieve good reliability, availability and high quality of service for all
medical equipment.
