Remember: the use of chlorine solutions to disinfect medical equipment

should be avoided if possible as they are corrosive and toxic. They will
damage the equipment and considerably shorten its life. Especially sensors

(for example those used with the ultrasound equipment) are very sensitive
to this and are damaged very quickly. The toxicity presents a potential risk

to patients if used to disinfect respiratory accessories. If chlorine solutions

are used, the equipment should be rinsed thoroughly afterwards,
remembering to avoid water entering the equipment! Disinfection solutions

should be sprayed onto a lint-free cloth to humidify but not saturate it. This

avoids moisture ingress while ensuring good disinfection.

Good organisation of storage for medical equipment can not only help ensure that it is well
looked after but also that it is available when needed. Medical equipment that is used
frequently should be cleaned and disinfected after use, then stored in a place where
everybody who might need it can find it easily. Smaller equipment (e.g. pulse oximeter or
multi-parameter monitors) might have a shelf or cupboard in the nursing-care preparation
room. If there is insufficient space, larger devices (e.g. oxygen concentrators) might be
stored in another room, but with restricted access so as to protect them from damage or
contamination or from being misplaced.

BIOMEDICAL SERVICE ACTIVITIES

As previously described in the Roles & Responsibilities chapter, the logistics staff is
responsible for conducting routine preventive maintenance work.

After contingency plans have been made and a schedule of routine maintenance has been
drawn-up (see chapter §5.1: Planning & Organising) the schedule must be followed.

The tasks and frequencies for each item of equipment are listed in the "Protocol II, Technical
Maintenance". These maintenance protocols provided by MSF for common items of
equipment give clear instructions for the work. Maintenance checklists for the most
common items of equipment are also provided, and these must be used to record each
maintenance.

Preventive maintenance work must be done according to the schedule. The Biomedical
Technician should remind the clinical supervisor the day before planned maintenance and
agree if replacement equipment is needed. This will also help the clinical supervisor ensure
that the equipment will be cleaned and disinfected before handing it to the Biomedical
Service. On the day of the service the equipment can be collected and, and if needed,
temporarily exchanged with replacement equipment (see §5.5: Organising Stocks & Storage
Requirements) for the time it takes to complete the task. If the replacement equipment is a
different model, the Biomedical Technician should make sure that the medical supervisor is
aware of that and can ensure that the clinical staff are able to use it correctly.

Preventive maintenance protocols exist for most MSF standard equipment with a
corresponding checklist to keep a record of the machine’s condition and the work done on
it. In some health facilities non-standard equipment may be used, either purchased locally or
belonging to a partner organisation. In these cases there is no MSF protocol or checklist, and
maintenance should be done according to the manufacturer’s recommendations described
in the user manuals or technical guides. The Biomedical Technician should create a protocol

and checklist based on similar ones for MSF standard equipment, modified using the
information in the manufacturer’s documentation, as the working principles will be very
similar. Equivalent parts may have different names as there is no international standard
nomenclature, and the values and range of the standard parameter can also vary. For
example, different oxygen concentrator manufacturers could use different names for the
various filters, and the acceptable range of oxygen concentration and output pressure can
also vary.

Even though the outside of the equipment has been cleaned and disinfected by the medical
team before being given to the Biomedical Technician, there are a number of precautions
that the technician should take. It is possible for some contamination to still be present
inside the equipment, so wearing gloves and following standard hand hygiene practices are
essential. Wearing safety glasses can provide useful additional protection from
contamination as well as from physical objects. The use of anti-static bracelets and earthed
mats when working on equipment can reduce the risk of electrostatic damage.

Essential points for any technical preventive maintenance are:

•
A physical check of the equipment’s condition.

Look for damage to the casing, screens or displays, power cords, cables, tubes, etc.

•
Clean the equipment thoroughly.

In many cases it is possible to remove accessories or covers so as to carefully remove
any dust or debris that may have collected. It is preferable to do this with
compressed air as this reduces the risk of damage by direct contact with sensitive
parts. Precision mechanical parts can be damaged by knocking, and electronic
components are sensitive to electrostatic discharges which can be produced when
fingers come into close proximity with components.

In some areas dust may be difficult to remove with compressed air and may need to
be loosened with a soft brush first. Take care with electronic components as the
brushing action can cause static electricity which could damage them.

If there are any signs that liquid has got inside, this could be an indication that there
are issues with the way the equipment is used or with the cleaning and disinfection
procedures. There may be a need for refresher training sessions or a discussion on
the suitability of the equipment. Inform the clinical supervisor so this can be
addressed.

•
Functional check of the equipment.

Check if the equipment switches on correctly, that any controls (buttons, dials,
switches, etc.) are functioning according to the description in the user manual and
that any audible or visual alarms (beeps or lights) can be triggered. If the equipment's
documentation describes any specific values which need to be verified for any of the
functions, check these also.

•
Condition rechargeable batteries if fitted.

Over a period of time these batteries will lose their capacity to hold a charge, so the
autonomy of the equipment will be reduced. In some cases, the equipment will have
a maintenance function to recondition the battery so that it retains its capacity for
longer. It usually consists of an automatic discharge and recharge cycle. This must be

done as part of the periodic (preventive) maintenance. If the equipment does not
have this facility then you should do it manually by turning the equipment on and
letting the battery run down completely, then recharging it again to 100%.

Do not do anything of which you are not absolutely certain, or remove any

component which you are not confident that you can refit. Equipment can
easily be damaged by removal of covers or parts by inexperienced persons.

If in doubt ... DO NOT DO IT!

The header of the checklist must be completed with all the equipment information
requested, and each step of the work should be confirmed after successful completion. If
during maintenance any problems are encountered with the equipment which can be
repaired immediately, the additional parts used should be recorded on the maintenance
checklist.

If the spare parts for repair are not available, the Biomedical Technician should order them
and store the equipment in a separate area of the biomedical workshop, with the
maintenance form attached to ensure that it is clearly labelled as "work in progress".

The technician should inform the medical staff of the problem, give an expected time for the
work to be completed and, if possible, provide a replacement item of equipment to cover
the intervening period. If the contingency plan needs to be adjusted because the situation
has revealed a gap in equipment availability, this should also be done, see chapter §5.1:
Contingency Planning.

After the completion of a maintenance job, the Biomedical Technician signs the form and
files it in the records (see chapter §5.4: Record Keeping). The equipment can then be
returned to its place in the health facility and the next item taken for servicing.

Returning the equipment to the medical service is the ideal moment for the Biomedical
Technician to check the user maintenance follow-up sheets and to provide feedback and
recommendations to the medical supervisor if the technical maintenance highlighted any
need to improve care of the equipment.

It could be that cleaning is not being done often enough or that equipment is left lying on
the floor with cables and tubes trailing everywhere, creating a risk of tripping and possible
damage to cables. This is a major cause of problems with medical equipment. The problem
could be a lack of understanding by the medical staff, or it could be that there is simply no
good place to store the equipment when it is not in use (see §2.2: Essential Tasks – Use &
Care recommendations).

The Biomedical Technician should raise any issues relating to the care of the equipment with
the clinical supervisor (and staff) and propose solutions that fit the needs of the clinical
team. In case solutions cannot easily be found, medical team meetings can be a good
platform to discuss this and reach an agreement.
