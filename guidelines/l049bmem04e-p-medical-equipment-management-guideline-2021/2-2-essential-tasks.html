ESSENTIAL TASKS

Whatever the size of the project or the quantity or type(s) of medical equipment, we must
always keep in mind the key objectives mentioned in chapter §1; safety (in functioning and
in (correct use), availability and cost. These are essential if we want the equipment to have a
positive impact for our staff and patients.

There are a number of essential tasks which contribute to this, requiring a range of
knowledge, skills and experience shared among various members of staff. To see how these
are essential and how key people need to interact we will look at things from the
perspective of the equipment: what needs to be done, and who needs to be involved at each
step through the lifecycle of medical equipment in an MSF project?

Below is an overview, more details can be found in the individual chapters of this guideline.

Needs

Medical needs guide the selection of equipment, based on the medical activities. The
equipment needs must take into account context and available medical skills (or training
needs). To successfully integrate medical equipment into a service will also require looking
at the space needed and the way the service is organised, considering also the heat and
noise this may add. Logistic input is needed prior to ordering so as to ensure that the correct
conditions for use are met (electrical needs and electrical protection, adequate protection
from the environment and protection of staff (e.g. x-ray screening etc.) and the available
technical support.

For critical equipment the breakdown of which could lead to a breach in a medical service,
the med and log teams should agree on a contingency plan. Options can simply be spare or
alternative equipment but can also be critical spare parts or an external service contract,
either at project or mission level.

Most often equipment can be found in the standard lists. If no suitable equipment can be
found, the mission should discuss the needs with the Medical and Biomedical Advisor in HQ
and by discussing what suitable equipment can be found. Validation by the Medical Cell in
charge and the Medical Director is needed if this is for equipment which has not previously
been used.

Ordering & Procurement

Medical devices are medical items which are coded and managed - subscriptions in
UNIDATA/supply-centre database(s), Medical Master and Standard Lists and ordering tools -
by the med/pharma team. That includes all related items, so also those items needed for
technical maintenance and repair.

Accurate prediction of needs (including equipment, consumables, accessories and spare
parts) can help ensure that the budget is correctly estimated and will not cause difficulties
further down the line.

The best approach is generally that the medical team prepare periodic international orders
for medical devices and equipment according to the supply strategy and add those
consumables and accessories needed for all medical services, and then they include the
spare parts requested by the Biomedical (Technical) Service.

It is essential for this to be discussed between the medical team and the biomed. As
previously mentioned, critical equipment backup strategy can be either additional
equipment or critical spare parts. The final quantities ordered will therefore need to take
this agreed contingency plan into account, as well as the stock in project or capital. After the
needs for all related items have been defined these will be transferred into the supply
system according to the procedures and responsibilities of each section, taking into account
back-orders, pipeline and on-going loans.

Stock shortages of spare parts needed to keep devices working well can have just as much
an impact on the quality and continuity of medical services as shortages of medical
consumables or other medical items.

Shipment, Reception & Storage

Shipping, importation etc. are generally managed by the supply team. All medical orders are
received by the project pharmacist/medical stock manager and stored until such time as the
equipment and items are needed in the medical services. The technical parts can be just as
sensitive to heat and humidity as medication, so for this reason the medical stock
warehouse is generally the best place to store the main stock. The Biomedical Service can
give valuable support to the person in charge of medical stock in identifying devices and
parts.

Working stock of consumables and accessories can be kept in the medical services, and a
working stock of spare parts can be kept in the Biomedical Service for upcoming
maintenance, but the main stock is best kept in the project medical stock.

Some equipment will also require periodic maintenance when kept in stock. If for no other
reason than this all equipment in the project medical stock must also be included in the
inventory that the Biomedical Technician keeps up-to-date.

Examples of spare parts that need to be maintained even in the stock

include some types of batteries. If not maintained they can lose their

capacity over time and will no longer be able to provide the backup

function they perform in many items of equipment.

Sometimes the equipment itself needs maintenance. An oxygen
concentrator that is returned to stock needs to be run regularly to avoid
absorbing moisture, which will render it unusable. Also, certain items of lab

equipment can become non-operational if tubing becomes clogged due to

residual fluids.

Installation

In a few cases installation will require specialist technical support by an external contractor
(e.g. for radiology equipment or the PSA oxygen plant), but usually equipment can be
installed by the project team. The medical team will have to specify the positioning of (new)
equipment and agree with the log team for any support work needed (electrical, building
modification, furniture etc.).

As the Biomedical Technician keeps the inventory up to date, it is very important that they
are informed of any medical device requested from – and therefore exiting – the medical
stock.

Equipment is always delivered with hard-copy manuals, and a limited number of
consumables and spare parts are also often included. This documentation should be made
available for reference in any medical service in which an item of equipment is used.

Training

It should never be assumed that people are familiar with medical equipment. Both medical
staff and the Biomedical Service must familiarise themselves with the equipment to ensure it
is installed, used and maintained correctly. Misuse or poor care and maintenance can have
serious consequences for the patient, so technicians must familiarise themselves with the
maintenance requirements, and users must be trained by the medical team on correct use in
relation to the medical protocols.

Basic training of the medical staff should be organised by the medical team. Be sure to train
everyone involved with medical equipment (doctors, nurses, nurse aides, cleaners etc.) for
the tasks they need to do. Manuals and MSF support documents are available for training
content, and the assistance of the Biomedical Technician and the IPC Supervisor can ensure
that training covers all aspects that the user must be aware of. More advanced user and
technical training may also be available from the manufacturer, either in the form of
tutorials or in face-to-face training sessions. Missions can also contact the HQ or Regional
Advisors for information on MSF training support options.

Use & Care

Unlike medications and medical items used in our medical services, the medical equipment
itself is not single use! That makes it a perfect vector for the transmission of pathogenic
microorganisms; users must follow the MSF User Maintenance Protocols for clear
disinfection instructions. In addition, these protocols include instructions for the most basic
tasks that will help ensure that the equipment enjoys a long and happy life.

Supervisors must follow-up to ensure these protocols are adhered to and to be the focal
point for problems. Often issues can be resolved by the medical team themselves, especially
if they involve replacement of batteries or accessories. Manuals will give clear guidance
whenever devices give error codes or alarms. The supervisor should ensure user manuals
and protocols are in the wards and coordinate the support of the Biomedical Service when
problems are of a more technical nature. Any device deficiencies occurring during the use of
the equipment which could impact user or patient safety should be evaluated together with
the biomed. Many issues can be corrected by providing appropriate training. Some may be
related to quality issues related to the equipment itself, or the support documentation
provided by the manufacturer; these should be reported to the pharma so the information
can be passed on to the HQ Pharma team and the Medical Devices Coordinator (see §2.1).

The medical supervisor is also responsible for ensuring that there is adequate storage for
keeping equipment safe from damage when (temporarily) not in use and is available when
needed. All facilities are different but generally include a nurses' office for patient files and
administration and a room dedicated to the preparation of care and the storage of
medication. Sometimes there are other storage rooms. (see §6.1).

Technical Maintenance

Medical equipment is often highly specialised, so in projects where there is a large inventory
of devices in use or where more complex devices are used, these projects should ideally
recruit trained Biomedical Technicians. For the more basic needs or smaller inventories a
technician with good electrical skills can do most of the preventive maintenance with the
support of the MSF Technical Maintenance Protocols, possibly with a trained biomedical
person giving mission support.

Preventive maintenance is scheduled for each device in the inventory and is done to ensure
correct functioning and the general good condition of the equipment. The servicing must be
recorded to keep a record of each device which can be of considerable help in deciding
when a device is reaching the end of its useful (and reliable) life. In most countries there is a
regulatory requirement to have traceability of maintenance work in support of patient-
safety incident investigations. Maintenance files must stay with the equipment if it is
transferred to another project.

Servicing must avoid disrupting medical activities. The maintenance planning should be
coordinated with the supervisors and be shared with the medical teams in each service so
they are aware of when equipment will be taken for servicing and how long it will be
unavailable.

Servicing should be done in a dedicated workshop space within the health facility to avoid
unnecessary transport and ensure the quickest turn-around time possible.
