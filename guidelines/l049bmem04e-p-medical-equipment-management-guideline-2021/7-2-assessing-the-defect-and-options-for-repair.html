ASSESSING THE DEFECT AND OPTIONS FOR REPAIR

The Biomedical Technician must first fill in the information on the location and equipment
type in the record-keeping system to ensure that there is a record of the repair work.

The Biomedical Technician will do an assessment using the defect-finding help section in the
manufacturer's documentation and try to identify the defect. For some items there are also
troubleshooting guides or maintenance manuals written by MSF.

As mentioned in chapter §6.2, even though the equipment has been cleaned and disinfected
by the medical team, the same safety precautions must be taken as for the periodic
preventive maintenance work.

Defect-finding steps:

If you remove any parts, be sure to make notes and take pictures of the disassembly
method. Keep fasteners (screws bolts, etc.) in bags (medication bags from the dispensary are
great for this!), and label them so they do not get lost. It can be useful to have a box to keep
all the removed parts in so they stay together.

Make notes (and maybe pictures too) of your observations as you go
through these points. These notes will be a useful reference later if you are

not able to complete the repair immediately.

•
Ask questions of the medical staff member who reported the defect.

Probably the defect description in the intervention request is not complete or still
raises questions. It may be useful to know if the machine was making any unusual
noises before it failed, or if there were any other indications or alarms shown by
lights or displays.

•
Check the easiest things first.

Without taking anything apart, you can first check power cords, tubes and
attachment points, cables, etc. for damage. Try exchanging the cables and sensors
with new cables and sensors. If no new parts are available, the parts from other
devices of the same type which you know to be working may be used. Be aware that
sometimes defective accessories could cause damage to equipment. Check for
warnings in the manufacturer’s documentation.

Check the casing for cracks or signs of damage from knocks or falls. This may indicate
that there could also be internal physical damage: parts which have become
disconnected or fragile components which have cracked.

•
Before disassembling try starting the device.

If nothing happens at all, the power supply is possibly damaged. This could simply
mean a blown fuse, or it could be other components. Any damaged components are
likely to look burned and show black traces. If the equipment starts, check for any
alarms or defect messages if it has a screen or display. These can be checked with the
manufacturer's documentation.

If the equipment starts, but there are no alarms, listen for any unusual noises or
vibrations. Try using the different functions, as a defect may be triggered by one of
these. It is useful to know what equipment normally sounds like, so familiarise
yourself with all the different items of equipment when they are working normally.

•
Open the device.

Do not do this if it invalidates the warranty or if the equipment has a service contract.
Read the manufacturer's documentation before removing covers. Pay attention to
warnings, particularly for devices with large capacitors in the power supply which will
need to be discharged to avoid electrical shock. Pay attention also to the disassembly
method. Do not force parts while disassembling, they can easily be damaged.

Check for blocked vents or dust or debris choking cooling fans, filters, etc., and clean
if needed. Check for damage to any internal tubes, pipes or valves.

With the cover removed you can again start the machine and try the different
functions. Any unusual noises will be easier to locate, and you can also check for
leaks or other indications of a defect. Be sure to observe good safety practice, and
avoid contact with live electrical parts.

Remember, to ensure the reliability of medical equipment it is important
that repairs are only attempted if the technician has the skills and training

to do the repair well. Badly repaired equipment can be more dangerous

than broken equipment!

Repair options after identifying the defect:

•
Repair on the project by the Biomedical Technician.
•
Repair externally by another contractor, either in the project area or in coordination.
•
Return to the supply centre for repair by the manufacturer.

The LogCo and MedCo can agree to best method per situation, with advice, as needed, from
the HQ Biomedical Advisor.

If a repair can be done in the project, the Biomedical Technician will conduct the repair.
Possibly the problem can be solved by the replacement of consumables and accessories
(cables, etc.) or spares which are in stock. If not, the Biomedical Technician will order the
required spares. Your Biomedical Referent can help identify the needed parts.

If a repair can be done by an external contractor, it is essential that this contractor be
validated for the work. A contractor must have sufficiently skilled and trained staff and use
quality spares (original manufacturer parts). See chapter §4.3: Local Suppliers & Contractors
for more info on contractor validation.

If there is no 100% guarantee of repair in the mission (i.e. if there are any doubts about the
skills of staff or contractors to conduct a repair), the equipment should be returned to the
supply centre for repair. This must be done according to the procedure for After-Sales
Service returns.

Whichever option is chosen for repair, it is essential that an estimate be made of the
expected repair time, and this is communicated to the medical supervisor. If there is a need
to replace equipment but there is no device available, the Biomedical Technician and

medical supervisor should discuss and propose an alternative solution which should then be
agreed upon with the LogCo and MedCo.

Equipment continuity-of-service alternatives:

•
Borrowing equipment temporarily from another section.
•
Outsourcing the service (e.g. for Lab or radiology) in consultation with the MedCo
and HQ Medical Advisor.
•
Placing an emergency order (and revising the contingency plan!). Remember that
medical equipment is expensive and rarely in stock at the supply centre; even an
emergency order could have a long lead time.
•
Temporarily changing medical procedures (consulting the MedCo/HQ Med. Advisor).

The Biomedical Technician must complete the repair form to reflect the action taken:

•
For project repair

the form must detail the defect found, action taken and any spares used to effect the
repair. If spares are ordered, the equipment is placed in the biomedical workshop
with the form attached until the spares arrive and the repair can be completed.

•
For repair by an external contractor

the form must be completed to reflect the order placed with the contractor and be
kept on file until the equipment returns. When the equipment returns to the project,
the Biomedical Technician should check the functioning and confirm that the repair
has been done correctly. Invoices and reports from the external contractor should be
kept in the file for the device, together with the other maintenance records.

•
For After-Sales Service by the manufacturer,

the procedure of your supply centre should be followed. A record must be kept on
file to show that the equipment has been returned. When the equipment returns to
the project, the Biomedical Technician should check the functioning and confirm that
the repair has been done correctly.

Whenever a repair is done outside the project (external contractor or after-sales service),
the equipment must be disinfected and a disinfection certificate made before handing it to
the Biomedical Service. Before sending the equipment for repair confirm that the medical
team has saved any patient data they wish to keep and that the memory has been erased.

Whichever repair procedure has been followed, it is vital that when the
repaired equipment returns it be tested before being put back into service.

This must be done together with a member of the medical staff who is
familiar with the equipment (e.g. Nurse Focal Point for Biomedical Service

or Nursing Supervisor). The final part of the repair form has provision for
both the medical staff and Biomedical Technician to sign off on the repair.

If after discussion between the supply centre and the LogCo, MedCo and Biomedical
Referent, it is decided that the equipment cannot be repaired, it must be disposed of
correctly. See chapter §8.3: Disposal.
