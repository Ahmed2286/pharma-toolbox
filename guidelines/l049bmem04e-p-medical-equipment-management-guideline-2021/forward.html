Forward

Recent years have seen a huge increase in the adoption of medical technologies in MSF.
Indeed, promoting medical technologies features strongly in the Medico-Operational
Orientations Policies 2020-2023, largely to support a desire to improve the quality of the
care we provide. However, if we only transpose the equipment to our projects and not the
organisational best practices then we risk having quite the opposite effect.

MSF has seen a spectacular increase in the
numbers of items of medical equipment in
our international cataloguesa. The increase
has been driven by the desire to improve our
medical services and sponsored by the
growth in the financial means available to,
and the ambitions of, our organisation.
Changes in the contexts in which we work,
and changes in the nature of our medical
activities have further driven the growth in
the adoption of medical technologies.

Unsurprisingly, the spending of MSF’s missions on medical equipment has also increased at a
similar rate. A decade ago only a fraction of our International Order (IO) spending was on
medical equipment, in 2020 this was 41M euros, second only to our spending on drugs,
and only by a narrow margin.

The rapid increase in the adoption of medical equipment represents a shift in the way we
provide healthcare and an increase in the complexity. Fifteen years ago, we regularly saw
simple autoclaves for sterilisation yet very little else. Now it is impossible to find a project
that does not have at the very least oxygen concentrators, pulse oximeters and basic point-
of-care diagnostic equipment. Most projects have much, much more.

This change has created additional tasks and responsibilities that need to be integrated into
the way we manage our projects.

Given the cross-cutting, multi-disciplinary nature of these tasks they must be shared
between many different members of the project teams; consequently, the organisational
structure must be designed to promote this collaboration. This includes the organisational
structure of our projects to ensure adequate communications and job descriptions that
ensure that the responsibilities and needed skills be more clearly defined.

Any chain is only as strong as its weakest link, and good management of medical
equipment will only be possible if all the people concerned are fully and actively involved.

This guideline is the result of a collaborative effort. Based on an existing OCG guideline from
2014, this version was first adopted and updated by the Biomedical Working Group and then
passed through several rounds of peer review and contribution from the Logistics and
Medical Working Groups and Contact Groups as well as the Medical and Logistics
Coordinators in the International Office and the European Supply Centres.

The authors would very much like to express their gratitude to everyone who has
contributed their time to this guideline, and most especially to those whose significant
contributions from all perspectives have helped to make it, we hope, accessible to and
understandable for the wide diversity of readers for whom the advice is intended.

The names listed in the acknowledgments represent members of the various working
groups, contact groups and taskforces in the various HQs and in the supply centres, whose

name (we also wanted to have some space to actually give some guidance
) but you
know who you are. Thank you all for your efforts in improving the management of medical
equipment in MSF’s missions!
