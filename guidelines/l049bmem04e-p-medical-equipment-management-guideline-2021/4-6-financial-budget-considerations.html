Battery powered equipment sometimes uses standard AA (HR6) size batteries, but
sometimes other standard sizes are used. Using rechargeable batteries can reduce waste.
Chargers can be installed in the health facility, for example at a nurses' station in a hospital,
so the medical staff can charge when needed. Be sure the staff know how to do this
correctly. However, rechargeable batteries can reduce the usage time or reliability so verify
they work with the equipment before putting this in place.

Most items of equipment have model-specific batteries, traditionally lead-acid or nickel
metal-hydride technology but increasingly lithium-ion is being used. Check your inventory
and stocks to be sure that you have spare batteries of each type used. If you have
equipment which uses rechargeable batteries it is usually good to have a spare in stock.
Batteries are classified as dangerous goods, with restrictions imposed on shipping methods
and quantities. The various lithium technologies are particularly difficult to air freight so this
can impact lead times, or even make international air freight impossible. This must be taken
into account when planning orders, or when selecting equipment.

Batteries create significant problems for disposal, therefore only select
battery-powered equipment when functioning without electricity is essential.

FINANCIAL (BUDGET) CONSIDERATIONS

When ordering equipment, it is easy to focus only on the initial purchase costs. It is equally
important to ensure that resources will continue to be available to cover not only the initial
investment but also the on-going costs. Depending on the nature and complexity of the
equipment, the total cost of ownership can include:

Initial investments

•
Purchase price.
•
Building costs for construction or modification, installation technicians, etc.
•
Improvements to energy systems (generators, protection devices, installation costs).
•
Recruitment of sufficient staff to care for and service the equipment.
•
Staff training in use or maintenance.
•
On-going (operational) running costs
•
Consumables (filters, etc.) and spares.
•
Maintenance and service contracts.
•
Running costs for improvements in electrical supply (additional fuel for generator).
•
HR costs for additional (specialised) staff to use the equipment.
•
HR costs for (additional) the maintenance staff.

Remember these points during the annual planning period and budget
revisions! To ensure that the equipment will continue to be well cared for

and available, be sure these costs are included in the project budgets.

On average, the annual costs for maintenance (including spare parts) will

be between 10% and 25% of the purchase price.
