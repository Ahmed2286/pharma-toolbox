Repair

Breakdowns are not always avoidable however; repair work must not compromise the
correct functioning of the medical devices. For some common standard devices there are
MSF Trouble-shooting Protocols which can give a technician basic advice. Manufacturers’
service manuals are also available for all medical equipment. The presence of a qualified
Biomedical Technician can increase the chance of successful and responsible repair.

The spare parts needed must also be available (see §2.2: Ordering & Procurement and §2.2:
Training for the technician). After any repair it is advisable to conduct preventive
maintenance to verify the repair.

Any repair work done must be signed-off by the nursing supervisor of the service to which
the device belongs, so we have at least a minimum of assurance that the device is
functioning correctly. In many countries there are similar regulatory requirements for QC
(Quality Control) and record keeping as exist for preventive maintenance.

Decommissioning

All devices wear out however well used and maintained they are. With the help of the
records kept by the Biomedical Service, decisions can be made to replace equipment before
its reliability is in question. This will help avoid disruption of medical activities.

The relevant medical service must make the final decision to decommission, and a
decommissioning record must be added to the maintenance file. Decommissioned
equipment can be disassembled, and certain parts may be useful to keep for future repair of
similar equipment. Other parts will need to be disposed of in accordance with local
regulations.

Programmes also evolve, so often equipment needs will also change. As technology evolves,
new models replace older ones which are taken out of production. With the advice of the
Biomedical Service, the medical team can decide if replacement equipment is needed or not.
And then we are back to the §2.2: Needs.

ORGANISING POSITIONS

Medical staff positions are organised according to the needs of the medical activities, and
staff are obviously selected for their medical skills rather than their technical knowledge of
the equipment used. Some, such as radiographers or anaesthetists for example, will have
good technical knowledge of specific equipment. Others will probably need additional
training. Clinical staff are ultimately responsible for patient safety, so equipment training is
essential.

Check that the tasks and responsibilities for medical equipment are included in the clinical
staff job descriptions. This will also make it part of staff evaluations and encourage the staff
to pay attention to this.

Responsibility for good care, cleaning and disinfection tasks should be assigned to each
member of staff who uses an item of equipment. Overall responsibility for the equipment,
and follow-up of routine cleaning and user maintenance could in most cases be given to
each medical service supervisor (i.e. one person per ward/department/facility).

Check that the following (or similar) are included in each job description:

Clinical Staff:

•
Ensures that cleaning and minor maintenance tasks are done (as defined in the
protocols) for each piece of (medical) equipment used. Follows hygiene protocols.
•
Takes care of any item of medical equipment and uses it in accordance with the
documentation and instructions given.
•
Verifies the correct functioning of the equipment and alerts the supervisor in case of
any doubt.

Nursing Supervisors:

•
Signs off when receiving all medical equipment intended for use in the medical
service, and signs off on equipment removed from the service permanently.
•
Ensures that all staff who use the equipment are qualified and trained in its correct
use and care and that manuals and protocols are available for reference.
•
Follows-up on and ensures that cleaning, disinfection and minor maintenance tasks
are performed for each piece of equipment.
•
Coordinates with the Biomedical Service to establish the maintenance schedule and
plan the periods during which the medical equipment will be unavailable.
•
Forwards any reports of damaged or malfunctioning equipment to the Biomedical
Technician (see also §5.1: Contingency Planning).
•
Reports device deficiencies with potential impact on user and/or patient safety to the
project/mission pharma.

Logistics staff

Positions will have to be organised according to the support required. A smaller project with
a limited number of simple medical devices could already have a general technician who can
also take on the biomedical maintenance tasks. Projects with a lot of medical equipment will
likely need a fulltime Biomedical Technician. Hospital projects may need more than one
biomedical position to handle the work. It is important to have a Biomedical Service with a
level of professional training matched to the complexity of the equipment.

When determining how much work these responsibilities involve, do not underestimate the
time needed. Servicing of an item of equipment can take from 30 minutes to a few hours.
The maintenance protocols will give an idea of the time needed for each item of equipment.
Coaching clinical teams and the follow-up of equipment use can also be time consuming, as
is participation in training sessions for specific clinical staff.

When deciding if a fulltime position is needed consider:

•
Quantity and type of equipment (technical complexity, size, weight, maintenance
intervals).
•
The number of locations (one hospital, multiple smaller facilities).
•
Distances and travel times between workshop and health facilities.

In missions with multiple projects or when the recruitment of qualified staff at project level
is difficult, it can be useful to have staff at each location with training for basic maintenance
and a more highly qualified staff member travelling between the projects to deal with more
complex work and to supervise biomedical activities and stocks at mission level. See your
section’s IRFFG for details on defined positions.

Whatever the case, there are a number of tasks and responsibilities that need to be
allocated and included in job descriptions; either to one person or allocated to existing
positions. These tasks are:

Asset Management:

•
Must know all the medical devices present in the project. Must be able at any time to
give the condition and location of this equipment.
•
Understands the technical documentation, operating principles and use of each piece
of medical equipment in the project/mission.
•
Organises and participates in the installation of medical equipment.
•
Participates in user (clinical staff) training where required.
•
Monthly Biomedical Report – Identifies any issues or problems in any of the locations
the equipment is used. Reports these to the supervisor and proposes solutions. (The
training of staff on a device, operating procedures, etc.)
•
Maintains the equipment files securely and ensures they are up-to-date:
identification cards, maintenance forms, intervention requests, repair forms,
warranty certificates, etc. There is a standard ID sheet template available, and this
should be completed on reception of the equipment. Also, ensuring that the MSF
supply centre provides details of the warranty.
•
Proposes equipment replacement to the medical team, based on the assessment of
reliability and running/repair costs.
•
Arranges for packing, shipment and contact with after-sales service providers or the
MSF supply centre for the repair of equipment that cannot be done in-situ.
•
Tracks all maintenance contracts and proposes renewal before they expire.
•
Ensures that as few items as possible are out of service at any one time.

Maintenance:

•
Creates and regularly updates the preventive maintenance schedule according to the
support documentation, the manufacturer’s or the Biomedical Referent.
recommendations
•
Ensures the maintenance schedule is followed and is shared with the clinical team to
facilitate access to the equipment. Conducts all planned preventive maintenance.
•
Receives notice of equipment failures (intervention request form) from medical
supervisors; makes an initial diagnosis of the equipment and drafts a repair proposal
to the supervisor. Conducts curative maintenance where appropriate; arranges
return to supplier for repair if required or ensures correct decommissioning.
•
Follows-up on use and care of equipment by the clinical teams, and supports the
medical supervisor(s) to ensure that cleaning and simple maintenance are properly
carried out.
•
Creates and files records of all maintenance (preventive and curative). The date of
intervention, replaced parts, problems and solutions should be included.

Stock Management:

•
Collaborates closely with the pharmacist / medical stock supervisor.
•
Manages the inventory of spare parts, and avoids stock outs by making regular and
timely proposals for orders.
•
Supports item identification (code and designation).
•
Supports the ensuring of adequate stocks of medical consumables for medical
equipment (consumption and needs estimation by the medical team).
•
Supports the reception of orders (identification of equipment and parts) and
returned repairs; requests information in case of delays and/or emergencies.
•
Ensures that equipment in stock is included in the maintenance planning.
•
Ensures the correct installation of equipment requested by the medical team.
