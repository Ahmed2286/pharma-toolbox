DONATION & DISPOSAL

P a g e |53

Before considering donating equipment to other organisations it is important to first
consider if the recipient is able to use and maintain it. When closing a project or handing
over a medical activity to others, consider the recipient’s capacity.

Making an equipment donation, questions to ask first:

•
Does the recipient's staff have the skills and experience to use the equipment
correctly? If not, then consider providing training before making the donation.
•
Is there sufficiently skilled staff to carry out maintenance and repair work? If not,
consider training or finding a local service contractor. You should consider giving a
service contract for a given period together with the donation.
•
Are spares and consumables available, and does the recipient have the (financial)
means to procure them? If not, you should consider giving spares and consumables
for a given time period together with the donation.

Equipment which MSF intends to continue using and supporting as part of on-going activities
should obviously not be donated as there is then no control over its use and maintenance.
This includes projects where we collaborate with a partner organisation. Donation should of
course be considered when we cease those activities.

If you have equipment which is in good condition but you have no immediate need for it,
you should consider transferring it to stock, other projects, other missions in your region or
other organisations. Equipment should always be packed with a complete set of accessories
and the complete maintenance file packed with it (maintenance records, purchase
information and service contract details). This will ensure that the equipment is immediately
usable when it is unpacked for re-use, and the history is known.

DISPOSAL

If you have an item of medical equipment which is broken and can no longer be repaired,
you will need to dispose of it in order to be sure that it cannot accidentally be used.

Be sure you have investigated all the repair options first. If you have more equipment of the
same type, there may be some parts you could remove and keep for other repairs. Be sure
the parts are in good condition.

Generally, equipment should be disassembled, and the different types of
materials segregated for disposal according local regulations and available

options. Both the availability for local disposal and MSF’s guidance on
waste are continually evolving, so check the latest recommendations and

continually assess the waste management options in your mission.

Medical equipment often contains electronic parts, and the exportation of this type of waste
to developing countries is a major source of environmental pollution. Some medical
equipment can also contain parts which are a direct hazard to the environment and require
specialised decommissioning companies. This is the case for any type of battery and for
some items of laboratory testing and x-ray equipment, for example.

Consult the manufacturers' documentation for disposal information or contact the
Biomedical Referent to see if there are other options for correct disposal.
