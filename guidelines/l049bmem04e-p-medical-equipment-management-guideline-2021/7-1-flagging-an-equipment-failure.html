7 REPAIRING & RETURNING

This chapter covers the following topics:

•
Flagging an equipment failure.
•
Assessing the defect and the options for repair.
•
Arranging After-Sales Service returns.

To ensure the reliability of medical equipment it is important that only skilled staff conduct
repairs. Sometimes repair can be done locally, but if specialist skills or equipment are
required, equipment should be returned to the manufacturer or official distributor.

FLAGGING AN EQUIPMENT FAILURE

Medical staff will usually be the ones to discover a device failure as they are the ones using it
and are responsible for care and cleaning. Before reporting it they must confirm that the
device has actually failed by cleaning it and changing batteries (if applicable) and ensuring
that the device is receiving power. These are the most common causes for a device not to
work.

If the problem is not solved, the equipment must be kept apart and clearly labelled to avoid
its being used by mistake. The medical staff will inform the Supervisor, and together they
must initiate an intervention request. A computerised Maintenance Management System
may have this integrated, otherwise there is a standard template for a paper-based system
available on each section’s platform for sharing documents and tools. A record of the
intervention requests should be available both in the medical service and with the
Biomedical Service, to ensure traceability of the equipment and work. The description of the
defect must be as complete as possible to help the Biomedical Technician identify the
probable cause.

Equipment, which is not functioning correctly, must be disinfected and
removed from active use until it has been successfully repaired, and the
correct functioning has been confirmed. This avoids the risk of accidental

use by other staff members who are not aware of the defect.

All medical equipment must be cleaned and disinfected before it is given to the Biomedical
Service for any maintenance work.

The clinical supervisor and the Biomedical Technician should discuss whether a temporary
replacement is needed. This will depend on the impact that the failure has on the medical
services. If there are sufficient devices, alternative equipment available or alternative
medical procedures which can be used, there is probably no need for a temporary
replacement. If there is a backup device available (and needed to ensure continuity of
service), the Biomedical Technician should install that for the duration of the repair. The
Biomedical Technician will then conduct a full assessment to decide the course of action to
take for repair.
