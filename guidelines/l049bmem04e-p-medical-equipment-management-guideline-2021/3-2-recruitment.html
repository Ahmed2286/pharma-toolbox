Many countries offer biomedical-related courses. The websites of the WHO and the French
organisation Humatem have lists of courses around the world. These lists are not complete,
so also try an internet or local search to find colleges and other courses which might provide
candidates for positions.

It may not be possible to find qualified biomedical profiles everywhere. Knowledge of an
international Language (EN, FR, SP) is essential, as well as a local language. Most medical
equipment is electrically powered so good knowledge of electricity and a practical
understanding of logical trouble-shooting methodology are essential. For the general
management of equipment servicing, planning and record-keeping, ICT knowledge, good
organisation and writing skills are also needed.

A Biomedical Technician will also need planning and organisational skills. Pedagogical and
language skills would also be an asset in coaching and training the medical staff on correct
care and basic maintenance tasks. Experience of working in a medical environment is equally
important for a basic understanding of the use of medical equipment, the need for flexibility
and a continuous dialogue with the clinical staff. Direct user support is especially important
for biomedical positions.

Whether the role be medical or technical support, the practical skills required for managing
medical equipment in an MSF project can be learned with the right training and coaching.
The most important things are good technical knowledge and a willingness to learn.

RECRUITMENT

Having defined the required positions and consulted the IRFFG, for examples of job
descriptions (see Chapter §2: Roles & Responsibilities, you will have to advertise the job
openings. For staff with fewer skills or qualification requirements a poster on the gate of the
compound may suffice; for more technical positions it will not. In these cases, developing
contacts with university hospitals and technical colleges will help get the job announcement
to the people who will most likely fit the required profile. In some cases, it may even be
possible to develop traineeship positions which could help MSF contribute to the local
health-education system whilst also gaining the benefits of a pool of enthusiastic new
graduates.

When there is time pressure to recruit, it can be tempting to lower the selection criteria, but
it should also be remembered that this will place a heavier burden on training and on-the-
job coaching (see section §3.3).

Any recruitment process should include an assessment of both theoretical and, where
possible, practical skills. Suggested steps in the selection are:

Pre-selection

Select the best candidates based on qualifications, experience and references.

Medical and biomedical qualifications can often be verified by the universities or colleges
that issued them. For qualifications and certifications issued by other institutions it can be
more difficult to assess the real value as there are many more organisations offering
courses. Check diplomas and try to find out about the school, college or university where the
qualification was earned.

For biomedical positions, aside from experience in medical facilities and college or university
diplomas, lists of pertinent manufacturer training courses following various models of

Written test

MSF has model Recruitment Tests available for many clinical and biomedical positions. These
are available through the Medical Advisors and Technical Referents of each section and can
be adapted to meet the specific requirements of each mission.

Select the best candidates to go through to the 3rd stage. Remember that some people
perform poorly in written tests but might still have important skills. Strong communication
skills are often as equally important as technical skills for both clinical staff in their
communication with patients and colleagues, and for the biomed, who has to communicate
clearly with many medical counterparts.

Important aspects to evaluate during the written test for a biomedical position:

•
Level of comprehension of the mission’s international language (EN or FR).
•
Knowledge of electricity and electronics.
•
Knowledge of basic medical equipment and its functioning.
•
Knowledge of basic human anatomy/physiology and bodily functions to understand
the fundamental purpose of understanding the medical equipment.
•
Skill to search and extract key information from catalogues and databases.

Practical test and/or interview

For medical staff it can be difficult to organise practical evaluation as the recruitment
process cannot be allowed to impact our on-going activities. It is however possible to
integrate questions in an interview which provide a more “scenario based” insight into
practical experience.

For biomedical positions practical testing is easier to organise. Choose a common item of
medical equipment in your project (one which is not critical and for which you have backup!
e.g. an oxygen concentrator and ask the candidate to do a preventive maintenance task
according to the protocol and checklists provided. By observing the candidate during the
practical test you will get a good idea of their skills and ability to work methodically, and how
they complete the checklist will give an idea of their understanding of the purpose of the
protocol and their writing skills.

Important aspects to evaluate during the practical test and/or interview for a biomedical
position are:

•
The skill to search and extract key information from protocols and manuals.
•
Understanding the role of a Biomedical Service and its interaction with other
departments.
•
Communication skills, written and verbal.
