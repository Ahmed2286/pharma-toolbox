Where there are no MSF maintenance protocols, the manufacturer’s maintenance
recommendations should be followed. Take into account that the manufacturers’
recommendations are often based on use in ideal conditions so are rarely perfectly suited to
the environments in which MSF works. The technical referent can give guidance on
additional maintenance tasks or changes to recommended maintenance intervals for the
conditions in your project. Do not underestimate the time needed for preventive
maintenance work and remember that this is only part of the Biomedical Service’s
responsibilities. When filling in the planning schedule, be realistic about the time it takes to
do the maintenance work well and the number of items of equipment, which can be
serviced each day. Each device can take between 30 minutes and several hours to service
correctly. Remember also that preventive maintenance is just one of the Biomedical
Technician’s tasks. They must also have time for repair work, staff coaching, and all the other
tasks listed in the job description (see chapter §3: Roles & Responsibilities).

SETTING UP A WORKSHOP

Equipment maintenance cannot be effectively performed in the clinical area. This would get
in the way of the medical activities, and the technician is unlikely to be able to work
productively. A workshop is therefore needed. Due to the sensitive nature of biomedical
equipment the workshop facilities should be free of dust, as cool and dry as possible and
must be easy to keep clean. Similar conditions to those in the health facility where it is used
would be ideal.

The workshop should preferably be located close to the health facility, ideally in the same
building. This is for a number of reasons:

•
The Biomedical Technician can respond more quickly to any problems reported.
•
The equipment does not have to be transported far or be exposed to rain or dust.
medical equipment is sensitive to heat, dust and moisture, it can also sometimes be
large or heavy.
•
The Biomedical Technician is better able to monitor the care and cleaning follow-up
and coach the clinical staff where needed.
•
The Biomedical Technician will be able to spend more time in the health facility and
be able to see and hear the equipment working well. This makes it easier to recognise
potential problems or trace defects.
•
The proximity will strengthen communication between the medical services and the
Biomedical Service, reinforcing this essential collaboration.

The size of the workshop depends on the quantity and type of equipment in the project. In a
multi-level health facility, the workshop would be ideally located close to the largest
concentration of (bulky) medical equipment.

For some types of equipment, such as laboratory analysers, sterilisers and operating theatre
equipment, going to the equipment is the only option because the sterility of the tools is
essential. In this case, additional time may be needed to sterilise/disinfect essential tools
and test equipment, or a separate stock could be kept in a space closer to the equipment.

The maintenance schedule will give you an idea of the quantity of equipment you will need
to service and how often. Allow additional space for maintenance records (see chapter §5.4
Record Keeping) and a small stock of parts for maintenance work (see chapter §5.5
Organising Stocks and Storage Requirements). It is also useful to keep a library of
manufacturers’ manuals for all the equipment used in the health facility. Space to store
medical equipment for (temporary) replacement during maintenance work or equipment
that is awaiting repair will also be required (see §5.5. Organising Stocks & Storage
Requirements).

At the very least, the workshop must have a stabilised and protected

source of electricity and water supply.

A cleaning/disinfection area and procedures/protocols are essential, not only to minimise
the risk of exposure of the technician but also to ensure that equipment is returned to
service safely. Electrical supply in the workshop must have the same level of electrical safety
and protection as in the medical service (see chapter §4.5 Power Supply). A DC power supply
may also be required for testing certain items of equipment.

Tools and test equipment commonly needed will be similar to those used by electricians.
You may need additional tools for specific items of equipment so check the technical
documentation to see if this is the case. Remember that often if special tools are required, it
is likely that more specialist skills are needed too.
